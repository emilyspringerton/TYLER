## STRUCTURAL NOTE

These three outlines run in parallel. Events in one series cause events in the others — sometimes immediately, sometimes with a delay that the reader will recognize before the characters do. The synchronization points are marked:

**⟳ SYNC** — A moment that lands differently depending on which series you read first.
**◈ EMILY** — Emily OS registers an event. A tile appears somewhere. Nobody who should see it does.
**✦ TYLER** — Tyler's position or awareness shifts across all three series simultaneously.

Each series has its own chapter numbering. The document is organized by **BEAT BLOCKS** — thematic clusters where all three series are operating on the same underlying problem from different angles.

## BOOK 2 PREMISE — ALL THREE SERIES

**What Book 1 ended with (canonical across all three):**

- The Camera Op knows the documentary is a ritual. Has not yet acted.
- The Chrono-Cube's location (Bacon) is known to Protagonist A. Not yet to the factions.

**What Book 2 is about:**

Book 2 is about **custody**. Who owns the Chrono-Cube. Who owns the documentary footage. Who owns the decision the Camera Op hasn't made yet. Who owns the consequences of Tyler handing Bacon the Cube in 1623. Who owns the timeline when it starts tearing.

Emily OS's first law runs underneath all of it: *Clean builds first.* Nobody has clean builds. Book 2 is what happens when you try to run features on broken infrastructure and the substrate starts noticing.

**The Subscriber moves.** For the first time in 380 years, they do something visible. It is small. It is surgical. It changes everything.

**CHAPTER 1 — "THE MORNING AFTER THE DETONATION"**

*POV: Protagonist A*

Opens three days after the love triangle detonated. Protagonist A is not okay. The book doesn't pretend they are. They are going to school because the alternative is staying home and thinking, and thinking is currently a hostile environment.

The Edinburgh Exchange Student is waiting at the school entrance. She has a file folder. She always has a file folder. She says: "I need to show you something and I need you not to tell anyone I showed you."

The file is an Eastwind Owl field note — not the official kind, the footnote kind. Handwritten. The Cataloguer's handwriting, which looks like someone tried to write calligraphy during an earthquake. The note is about a sighting. The sighting is of a person matching Protagonist A's description, in a location Protagonist A has never been, in a year that hasn't happened yet.

**CHAPTER 2 — "PROTAGONIST B MAKES A CHOICE THEY DON'T TELL ANYONE ABOUT"**

*POV: Protagonist B*

Protagonist B is Jiangshi-adjacent. They believed in the documentary. They believed it was real and important. Book 1 ended with them knowing it's a ritual and not yet deciding what to do with that knowledge.

They decide in Chapter 2. They decide to keep working on the documentary. Not because they're complicit. Because they've realized: if the Camera Op destroys the footage, the ritual fails and Tyler stays unbound and the factions keep operating on broken infrastructure forever. And Protagonist B has seen what broken infrastructure does to people. They watched it happen to someone they loved.

They believe — incorrectly, but sincerely — that a bound Tyler is a safer world.

This chapter is the most morally complicated chapter in the book. It should be written with complete sympathy. Protagonist B is not a villain. They are someone who made a reasonable calculation with incomplete information.

The chapter ends with Protagonist B sending one message. To the Producer (O.S.). Three words: *I know where.*

They don't finish the sentence. They learned that from Tyler without meeting him.

**CHAPTER 3 — "BACON HAS BEEN EXPECTING THIS"**

*POV: Protagonist A*

Protagonist A goes to find Bacon. His current identity: a culinary historian named Dr. Aldous Vane, visiting lecturer, department of food anthropology, the kind of academic position that moves every two years and leaves no permanent address.

He is, somehow, expecting them. There is dinner. There is always dinner.

The table is extraordinary. The Exchange Student, who came along because Protagonist A didn't say not to, eats three helpings and doesn't notice she's been fed information the entire time.

Bacon talks about the Chrono-Cube the way you talk about a difficult houseguest: with affection, with exhaustion, and with the careful avoidance of the one thing you most need to say.

What he says: "It has been in my custody for four hundred years. I have not neglected it. I have not used it. I have not delivered it. These are not the same thing."

What he doesn't say: *Why.* Not yet. That's a Chapter 14 answer.

**CHAPTER 4 — "THE SHELL PARLIAMENT SENDS AN INVOICE"**

*POV: Kei (Heikegani Crustocrat, 16)*

Kei receives an invoice. This is not unusual. Kei has been receiving invoices since they were eight years old and their father explained that the family business was "financial services" and stopped explaining after that.

This invoice is different. It is addressed to Protagonist A. It has been routed through Kei because the Shell Parliament determined that Kei and Protagonist A share a social graph node — they go to the same school, they have been in proximity seventeen times in the last month, they have never spoken.

The invoice is for 340 shell-marks. Line item: *Unauthorized access to contested temporal asset, compounded at standard rate, 1623 CE to present.*

The asset is the Chrono-Cube. The debtor is listed as TYLER (unresolved). The co-debtor, added in a recent amendment, is listed as: *PROTAGONIST A (pending confirmation).*

Kei's shell-debt is almost paid off. They were almost free. Now they've been handed a collection case that will take another century to close.

They are furious. They are also, against their better judgment, curious about the person they've been assigned to invoice.

*POV: Protagonist C (partial Cephalopod Host)*

Protagonist C's partial infestation has been stabilizing. They've been finishing sentences correctly for months. They thought they were becoming more empathetic. They have not examined this thought carefully.

They finish it in the middle of a conversation about something completely unrelated, and they don't realize they've done it until three seconds later when the person they're talking to stops and stares.

The fragment: *"—the Cube was never the point. The fixed coordinates are the point. The Cube is just the key."*

Protagonist C doesn't know what this means. Their octopus does. The octopus is not sharing. Yet.

**CHAPTER 6 — "THE SPINNER ARRIVES"**

*POV: Protagonist A, then Spinner*

The Kuroshio Arachnid partial-host — the teenager who can see the web but hasn't been consumed — arrives in the city. She doesn't introduce herself. She sits down next to Protagonist A in a coffee shop and says, in present tense about the future: "You are going to need what I know. I will have told you already. This is that conversation."

Protagonist A says: "That's not how sentences work."

The Spinner says: "I know. It's very annoying." She sounds genuine. "The web sees all timelines at once. I just translate. Some of the translation is bad."

What she knows: the Kuroshio Arachnids are not coming for the Cube. They are coming for the **fixed coordinates** the Cube creates. Every time the Cube pins a temporal point, it creates a permanent node in the web — a stable anchor. The Arachnids have been waiting for someone to use the Cube so they can harvest the nodes.

Nobody has used the Cube. That's why the Arachnids have been patient.

The Subscriber is about to use it.

**CHAPTER 7 — "THE DOCUMENTARY CREW COMES TO SCHOOL"**

*POV: Protagonist B, then Camera Op*

The documentary crew arrives at the school. This is, officially, because Tyler was sighted in the area. This is also true. But Protagonist B sent that message — *I know where* — and the Producer (O.S.) has deployed the crew with a secondary objective that the Camera Op doesn't know about yet.

They film the school. They film the courtyard. They accidentally film Protagonist A talking to the Edinburgh Exchange Student. They review the footage that night and notice something: in the background of the shot, visible for exactly four frames, is a figure who should not be there.

Tyler. Standing by the library entrance. Not looking at the camera.

Looking at something the camera can't see.

The Camera Op doesn't report this frame to the Producer. This is the second thing they haven't reported. They are keeping a list.

*POV: Kei*

Kei delivers the invoice. This involves speaking to Protagonist A for the first time, which involves choosing between the Shell Parliament's approved script (formal, financial, no personal pronouns) and their own judgment.

**CHAPTER 12 — "THE SPINNER EXPLAINS THE WEB (AND IT'S WORSE THAN EXPECTED)"**

*POV: Protagonist A, with Kei and the Exchange Student*

The Spinner meets all three of them. She hasn't been invited. She arrives because "this conversation will have happened and I might as well be there for it."

She explains the web. She does it non-linearly, which is the only way she can, which means Kei has to take notes and reconstruct the chronological version afterward, which becomes the clearest explanation of Kuroshio Arachnid mechanics in the entire series.

Enough nodes and the web becomes impossible to disrupt — no frequency, no humming, no odd time signature will touch it.

The hum is Tyler's counter-magic against the web. If the Cube is used, the counter-magic stops working. Permanently.

### ACT THREE — "WHAT CUSTODY MEANS"

**CHAPTER 19 — "BACON'S FULL TESTIMONY"**

*POV: Protagonist A*

Bacon tells the whole story. The table listens. This is the chapter where the reader gets everything they've been waiting for — and it lands differently than expected because Bacon tells it with the exhaustion of someone who has been right about something terrible for 281 years and found no comfort in being right.

The Subscriber wants to use the Cube to pin the timeline not to preserve one version of history — but to **end migration entirely.** Fixed coordinates across every contested point means no superposition windows. No 72-minute ambiguity. No edges to live in.

Tyler would be bound. Every migrant would be bound. The timeline would be clean, singular, and unchallengeable.

"It sounds like order," Bacon says. "It is order. But order imposed on a system that requires ambiguity to function is not order. It's a build with no exit condition."

The Exchange Student writes this down. The footnote writes itself.

**CHAPTER 20 — "THE CAMERA OP MAKES THE DECISION"**

*POV: Camera Op*

The chapter the entire series has been building toward.

The Camera Op has the footage. They have had it since the pilot. They have been keeping a list of things they haven't reported. They are, at this moment, the only person in both series who can complete the Jiangshi binding ritual or destroy it.

They think about Protagonist B. They think about the 2.3 billion people whose memories would be reclassified. They think about Tyler's coin, and the date that changes, and the way he smiled at the camera in the pilot like he was smiling at something behind it.

They think about Emily OS. About the end logs. About *BIRD CORRECTION PENDING* appearing in every single one.

They open their laptop. They look at the footage. They look at the list.

They do not destroy the footage.

They do not submit it.

They do something that neither the Jiangshi Syndicate nor the Eastwind Owls predicted: they **publish it**. All of it. Unedited. With annotations. On a platform everyone has heard of.

Because a binding ritual requires a controlled documentation environment. It requires that the documentation be managed. It requires the Observer's attention to be directed.

If everyone is watching, nobody is directing the observation. The wavefunction doesn't collapse. It spreads.
