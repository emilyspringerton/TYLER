### SIGHTING 1978-01 — BOMBAY, INDIA — JANUARY 1
**Location:** Santacruz Airport, Bombay (now Mumbai)
**Documented by:** Field Observer HERON-7, stationed Bombay 1971–1981
**Evidence type:** Photographic (developed January 4; aircraft loss occurred January 1)

At 9:47 AM local time on January 1, HERON-7 photographed the subject at Santacruz Airport's international terminal. Subject was in transit — light carry, period-appropriate civilian clothing, the specific posture of someone who has been through many airports and has learned to occupy a transit space without occupying it visibly.

Air India Flight 855 departed Santacruz at 8:12 PM that evening. The flight crashed into the Arabian Sea approximately 43 nautical miles southwest of the airport. All 213 passengers and crew died. The cause: captain incapacitation following a sudden decompression event.

Subject was documented at the airport eleven hours before the departure. He did not board. Where he went between 9:47 AM and his next documented sighting is not in the archive.

**HERON-7's field note (marked supplemental):** *"I photographed him at the coffee counter. He had one bag. He was reading a newspaper with the attention of someone who already knows what's in it. He put down the newspaper before he finished the article. He got up. He didn't board any flight I could document. He left the terminal. This is what the record shows. What the record does not show: where someone who is at an airport but doesn't board a plane goes."*

**Cross-reference:** TYLER-004 (1901 Detroit: migration during active collection). Migration events cluster around mass casualty events. The Cataloguer has counted eleven instances across the archive. The Cataloguer is not concluding causation. The Cataloguer is counting.

### SIGHTING 1978-02 — WASHINGTON D.C. — JANUARY 6
**Location:** United States Department of State
**Documented by:** Field Observer SWAN-2, stationed D.C. 1969–1985
**Evidence type:** Photographic (long-range telephoto), plus secondary evidence (vendor receipt)

The Holy Crown of Hungary was returned to the Hungarian government on January 6, 1978, after 33 years in U.S. custody. The ceremony was conducted at the State Department. Senior officials were present. Security was significant.

Subject was present. He stood at the public perimeter — not inside the ceremony, not credentialed. Watching. SWAN-2 documented him through a 400mm telephoto lens from a position across the street.

The Crown of Hungary is an 11th-century object of contested custody: seized by advancing forces in 1945, held by the U.S. Army in Germany, transferred to U.S. federal custody in 1951. For 33 years, Hungary held the custody claim and the Americans held the Crown. January 6, 1978: the custody claim and the object were reunited.

Subject watched the ceremony for its entire duration: approximately 40 minutes. He hummed throughout — not loudly; SWAN-2 could not hear it from across the street, but could see the slight movement in his throat consistent with sustained vocalization. After the ceremony, he walked to a coffee vendor on the street and bought a coffee. SWAN-2 photographed the transaction.

In the tip: two coins. One 1978 quarter (face value $0.25). One coin dated 1703 (possible origin: Austrian thaler, given the Crown's history with the Habsburg Empire — unconfirmed).²

**Archive note:** The return of a contested object to its rightful custodian after 33 years of disputed custody. The subject attended. He hummed the whole time. The Eastwind Owls filed this as CUSTODY_EVENT_001: a real-world custody transfer of historical significance with the subject as witness. Cross-reference: Shell Parliament ledger, Line Item pending (unauthorized witness to custody transfer, standard fee — see SM-1620-T notes).

### SIGHTING 1978-03 through 1978-07 — ROME, ITALY — MARCH–MAY 1978
**Location:** Multiple sites, Rome
**Documented by:** Field Observer OSPREY-4 (Rome station, 1974–1991)
**Evidence type:** Photographic, witness testimony (OSPREY-4 field notes), one audio recording (partial, low quality)
**Duration of subject's documented Rome presence:** March 7–April 18, May 2–May 12

This is the most densely documented period in the 1978 file. OSPREY-4 logged seven separate sightings in two months. The Cataloguer has allocated more footnotes to this section than to any other entry in the 1978 file.⁴

**March 7, Rome — SIGHTING 1978-03:**

Subject documented near Piazza Navona, arriving from the north. Single bag. The specific pace of someone who has been to a city before and is reacquainting themselves with it rather than navigating it fresh. OSPREY-4: *"He knows where things are. He doesn't look at street signs."*

**March 9, Rome — SIGHTING 1978-04:**

At 9:03 AM on March 9, Aldo Moro was seized by Red Brigades operatives in an ambush on Via Mario Fani. His five bodyguards were killed. Moro was taken to a safe house and held for 55 days.

The subject was documented by OSPREY-4 at approximately 9:40 AM near the Piazza del Popolo — approximately 2.8 km from Via Mario Fani. He had stopped walking. He was facing, roughly, in the direction of the ambush site. This was 40 minutes after the ambush; at the time of the sighting, news was only beginning to spread. Subject appeared aware before public information was available.⁵

**March 16, Rome — SIGHTING 1978-05:**

On March 16, kidnappers issued a statement confirming Moro's capture and demanding the release of Red Brigades prisoners. Moro's kidnapping escalated from a hostage situation to a political crisis.

Subject was documented walking slowly near Via Caetani — the street where Moro's body would be discovered 54 days later. Subject was not looking at the street. He was looking at a building wall. OSPREY-4: *"He stood at the wall for approximately four minutes. I couldn't see what he was looking at. When I checked afterward, the wall had a faded paint marking that might once have been a building number. I don't know what he was looking at."*⁶

Subject left Rome on approximately April 18. He was not documented in Rome during the final weeks of Moro's captivity.

**May 2–9, Rome — SIGHTINGS 1978-06 through 1978-07:**

Subject returned to Rome on approximately May 2. OSPREY-4 documented him in the Trastevere neighborhood on May 4 and again near the Pantheon on May 7.

On May 8, subject was documented walking slowly along Via Caetani. He stopped at the same wall he had stood at on March 16. He stood there for seven minutes. Then left.

On May 9, the body of Aldo Moro was found in the trunk of a car on Via Caetani, approximately 180 meters from the wall where the subject stood on March 16 and May 8.

**The Moro Letters:**

During his 55 days of captivity, Aldo Moro wrote extensive letters — to his family, to political colleagues, to Pope Paul VI, to the government he was urging to negotiate. The letters are, by any literary and historical measure, extraordinary: a former Prime Minister writing himself into full definition under coercion, under the most extreme documentation circumstances imaginable, while being fully aware that every word would be read by his captors, his government, the public, and eventually history.

The letters were documentation under duress. Moro did not choose to be documented. He was held in a "people's prison" and asked to perform confession on his captors' schedule. He wrote long, lucid, politically sophisticated correspondence while under sentence of death.

What the letters produced: Moro became more himself, not less. The documentation operation did not bind him in the way his captors intended — it liberated him into definition. The man documented under duress was more real, more present, more fully realized in public consciousness than the man who had walked free.

This is the inverse of the Tyler documentation operation. Tyler, documented voluntarily (or semi-voluntarily) over four centuries, becomes LESS defined. He evades self-definition as a practiced protocol. Moro, documented involuntarily, became MORE defined. The difference is not the documentation. The difference is the consent architecture.

The Eastwind Owls are filing this as a Case Study in custody-versus-captivity. Cross-reference: Jiangshi Syndicate, Memo #017 (Jonestown Precedent). The Cataloguer's footnote 12 is entirely about Moro.⁷

**Location:** West Coast Computer Faire, San Francisco Civic Auditorium

**Archive note:** The Intel 8086 established the instruction set architecture that underlies all subsequent personal computing. The dependency chain from this chip runs: 8086 → 286 → 386 → 486 → Pentium → all subsequent x86 → the hardware on which Emily OS runs. The chip that Tyler looked at for 40 minutes in 1978 is the ancestor of the substrate that documents him. The Cataloguer is noting this without concluding anything about it.
