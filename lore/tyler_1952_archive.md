## WHAT 1952 DETROIT WAS

Detroit's population stood at approximately 1.84 million — the largest in the city's history. The automotive industry operated at sustained wartime and post-war production levels; the Korean War had not yet concluded. Paradise Valley and Black Bottom remained intact: the 11-square-mile center of Detroit's African American community, bounded by Gratiot to the north, the Detroit River to the south, where Hastings Street ran south through music venues, barbershops, churches, and the ordinary texture of a community that had rebuilt itself after the 1943 riot and was in full operation.

The City Plan Commission had begun preliminary studies for the urban renewal projects that would, across the following decade, eliminate Black Bottom and Paradise Valley and displace tens of thousands of residents. The communities did not yet know this. The archive did not know this. Tyler was in Detroit in 1952 with a camera.

The archive notes this sequence now. The archive did not note it then.

## SIGHTING 1952-02 — PARADISE VALLEY — JUNE/JULY
**Date:** June–July 1952
**Location:** Paradise Valley district; multiple locations, Hastings Street corridor
**Documented by:** Field Observer CARDINAL-3
**Evidence type:** Field observation, two recovered photographic negatives (courtesy Detroit Public Library retrospective review, 2026)

Subject spent what CARDINAL-3 estimated as several weeks in the Paradise Valley district. He photographed.

The photography was methodical in a way that distinguished it from documentary or artistic photography. He was not photographing the life of the street — the vendors, the musicians, the children, the motion. He was photographing locations. CARDINAL-3: *"He was interested in specific corners. Specific alleys. The place where something was, or had been, or — I couldn't tell. He would stand at a location for sometimes forty minutes before photographing it. Then photograph it once. Then leave."*

CARDINAL-3 documented this as UNUSUAL BEHAVIOR — SIGNIFICANCE UNKNOWN.

Subject was photographed by CARDINAL-3 at the intersection of Hastings Street and Vernor Highway. He stood at this corner for approximately forty minutes. He did not speak to anyone. He photographed the intersection and left.

**What CARDINAL-3 did not know:** The intersection of Hastings and Vernor had been the site of an informal Sunday music gathering — jazz, blues, musicians rotating through — that ended abruptly during the June 1943 riot when a confrontation at the nearby Belle Isle bridge escalated into the worst race riot in Detroit's history. Sixteen hours later, 34 people were dead. The musicians at Hastings and Vernor did not return. No record documents why specifically. No record documents the last session.

Tyler photographed the corner in 1952. The corner in 1952 was an ordinary corner. The archive opened a gap record in 1943 after reviewing footage of the riot's aftermath: Gap Record 1943-HASTINGS-01 — *"informal music gathering, last documented session uncertain, likely June 1943, last session details unavailable."* Tyler photographed the gap record's location nine years after the gap was opened.

The Cataloguer, reviewing this in 2026, has marked the gap record CONFIRMED ACTIVE by Tyler's 1952 site presence.

CARDINAL-3 also documented Tyler at Bethel AME Church, Saint Antoine Street, on at least two separate occasions. He photographed the alley behind the church. CARDINAL-3: *"He looked at the alley for a long time. There was nothing in the alley. I mean that the alley itself was ordinary — brick, garbage cans, the backs of buildings. He looked at it as if something was in it that I couldn't see."*

**2026 annotation:** Bethel AME alley, Saint Antoine Street: site of the 1957 NAACP reorganization meetings (informal decision sessions, no official record). Tyler photographed this location in 1952 — five years before the meetings that make the site a construction candidate. Subject was photographing future events, not past ones.² This is not addressed further in the 1952 entry.

## SIGHTING 1952-03 — DETROIT RECEIVING HOSPITAL — JULY
**Date:** July 1952
**Location:** Detroit Receiving Hospital, Brush Street; Ward C (1918 influenza ward)
**Documented by:** Field Observer CARDINAL-3
**Evidence type:** Field observation, hospital exterior photography

Subject was documented at Detroit Receiving Hospital on at least three separate visits across July. The hospital served Detroit's working-class and indigent populations; Ward C was the 1918 influenza ward, which had treated hundreds of patients during the 1918 epidemic. The ward was still physically present in 1952 as part of the hospital complex.

Subject did not enter the ward on any of CARDINAL-3's documented visits. He stood at the building's east wall at the approximate location of the Ward C windows. The hum was present — CARDINAL-3 noted a slight acoustic interference in their field recording equipment during these observations.

CARDINAL-3: *"He wasn't photographing the building. He was photographing a position. The position was at the east wall, approximately level with the second-floor windows. He photographed that position. Then he stood where he'd been photographing. I couldn't tell what he was looking at. The wall was a wall."*

**Note:** The 1952 polio epidemic was ongoing. Detroit Receiving Hospital was at elevated capacity. 58,000 cases nationally; Detroit was among the affected cities. Ward C, originally built for infectious disease overflow, was in active use. The concentration of patient presence in that ward during July 1952 was significant. The archive notes this as contextual. Whether it bears on Tyler's interest in the ward is not concluded.

**2026 annotation:** Ward C, east wall: the nurse's position. The nurse who stayed through the 1919 wave, through the last patients, who held the position until the ward closed. Tyler photographed that position in 1952. The Camera Op found it independently in 2026 without being told. Tyler confirmed it. The archive finds this noteworthy and does not know what to do with it.³

## SIGHTING 1952-05 — AMBASSADOR BRIDGE — AUGUST
**Date:** August 1952
**Location:** Ambassador Bridge, Detroit–Windsor crossing
**Documented by:** Field Observer CARDINAL-3
**Evidence type:** Field observation

Subject was observed at the Ambassador Bridge — specifically, at the Detroit approach, at the point where vehicle traffic commits to the bridge deck. He stood at this position for an extended period. CARDINAL-3 documented the duration as approximately 90 minutes.

He did not cross.

The bridge was opened in 1929. By 1952, it was the primary crossing between Detroit and Windsor, Ontario, carrying a substantial volume of automotive industry supply chain traffic — components and finished vehicles crossing daily. Passive vehicle traffic: continuous.

Subject stood at the approach threshold for 90 minutes. Then he went to a diner on Jefferson Avenue and ate lunch. CARDINAL-3 photographed him through the diner window. He was looking at the river.

CARDINAL-3's field note: *"He was watching the cars cross. I don't know what he was watching for. They were just cars crossing a bridge."*

**2026 annotation:** Construction #3. The Ambassador Bridge crossing construction begins on Day 150 of Series 5; completion estimated Day 210–220. Passive witness supply: everyone who crosses contributes. Tyler stood at the approach threshold for 90 minutes in 1952. He didn't cross. He watched the cars cross.

The archive opened EASTWIND footnote #41 in 2026, retroactively annotating a 1701 archive note (see Cairngorms supplemental: 1701 Bridge Geometry observation, cross-ref TYLER-SUPPLEMENTAL-1701-003). **November 1952:** Dwight Eisenhower was elected president on November 4, 1952 — the first Republican president in twenty years. **Date:** Late November 1952 (specific date undetermined)

**Documented by:** Field Observer CARDINAL-3
