## CHAPTER 1 — "THE SCHOOL THAT APPEARED ON THURSDAY"

---

The thing about the school was not that it appeared. The thing about the school was that nobody was surprised.

Protagonist A walked past the lot every morning for two years. Every morning: chain-link fence, weeds, a mattress that had been there so long it had achieved a kind of permanence that mattresses are not usually capable of. Every morning: the vague civic sadness of an empty lot in a city that had decided to stop being surprised by empty lots.

Then Thursday: a school.

Three stories. Brick the wrong color and weight for the neighborhood — older, denser, the kind of brick that doesn't exist in current manufacturing catalogs. Tall windows. Double front doors with the specific hardware of an institution that has been telling students to be somewhere at a certain time for longer than anyone currently alive can remember.

Protagonist A stood on the sidewalk across the street for ninety seconds and watched other students file past them toward the school's entrance. Students with backpacks. Students eating things out of containers. Students doing the universal shuffle of people who would rather be asleep but have accepted that they aren't going to be.

Nobody else stopped. Nobody else looked at the school the way you look at something that wasn't there yesterday. Everybody looked at the school the way you look at something that has always been there and is therefore invisible.

The sign above the doors: DETROIT PREPARATORY INSTITUTE.

Protagonist A had never heard of it.

They went inside.

---

**[PANEL: Wide exterior shot. The school in the morning light. Students flowing toward it. In the lower right corner, one figure standing completely still on the sidewalk across the street. Everyone else is moving.]**

---

The lobby was correct. This was the part that unsettled Protagonist A more than the school's sudden existence: the lobby was completely, precisely correct. Fire exit maps on the wall. A trophy case with trophies. A front office with a frosted glass window and a sign that said PLEASE SIGN IN. Linoleum floors with the particular scuff patterns of a building that has had thousands of pairs of shoes cross them.

The scuff patterns were old. The linoleum was old. The building had not been here on Wednesday. The scuff patterns were decades old.

Protagonist A looked at the fire exit map. They looked at it for longer than fire exit maps are usually looked at. The map was in a font they couldn't name — clean, institutional, the specific weight of official communications — and the map showed evacuation routes in a building they were standing inside of for the first time, and somehow they understood the evacuation routes. Not from reading them. From the feeling of the building itself. As though the building had already told them where the exits were.

They were late for first period. They found their classroom. It was where it was supposed to be. Their name was on the roster.

Their name had always been on the roster.

---

**[PANEL: Close-up of a class roster. Third from the bottom: Protagonist A's name. The ink looks the same age as everything else on the page — not newly added. There. As though it had always been there.]**

---

The teacher didn't notice anything. The other students didn't notice anything. The schedule was correct and the textbooks were the right edition and the cafeteria menu had been updated for the week. Protagonist A sat through three classes before admitting to themselves that the school was not, in fact, wrong.

The school was perfectly right.

That was the thing.

## CHAPTER 2 — "WHAT WAS WRONG WITH THE SCHOOL"

---

The school was wrong in one specific way that Protagonist A didn't identify until fifth period.

The cameras.

Not security cameras — different. Smaller. The kind that came with a small rectangular lens and a built-in microphone and the kind of operator who spent the morning walking the perimeter of a space before filming anything inside it.

A documentary crew. In their school. Filming.

Protagonist A asked the kid next to them in fifth period: "Do you know who those people are?"

The kid — dark hair, glasses, scarf even though it was not a scarf kind of day — said, without looking up from their notebook: "Documentary crew. They've been here since morning."

"Filming what?"

"They said Tyler." The kid — this is the Edinburgh Exchange Student, though Protagonist A doesn't know this yet — still didn't look up. "I don't know what Tyler is."

"Who Tyler is," Protagonist A said.

The Exchange Student made a note in their notebook. Said, still not looking up: "I said what, not who."

---

**[PANEL: Protagonist A and the Edinburgh Exchange Student, desk-adjacent, in a fifth-period classroom. The Exchange Student is writing. Protagonist A is looking toward the window. Through the window: the documentary crew in the hallway. The Camera Op has a camera up. The lens points slightly left of center, like it's uncertain where to look.]**

---

After school, Protagonist A went looking for Tyler.

They didn't know this was what they were doing. They thought they were staying late to find out about the documentary crew — who had approved a film crew in their school, what they were filming, whether there were consent forms Protagonist A had missed. These were reasonable questions. These were the questions Protagonist A used to justify the walk.

But the walk took them to the library entrance. The west side of the building, where the ground floor met a small courtyard that didn't appear on the fire exit map but was nevertheless exactly where it was supposed to be.

There was a man standing at the library entrance.

Not old. Not young. Not the age you can just name without thinking. The specific age of someone who has been the same age for longer than makes sense, and has gotten so comfortable with it that the discomfort has transferred to anyone who looks at them carefully.

He was looking at a window on the third floor. Not moving. Not waiting for something. Looking, with the quality of attention you give to something you've been thinking about for a very long time and are now finally able to see in person.

Protagonist A stopped.

The man didn't look at them.

After a moment, he stepped back, away from the entrance, and was no longer visible.

Protagonist A stood where they were for eleven seconds. Then went home. Did not tell anyone what they'd seen.

Made a note in the margin of their history homework: *the school has always been there, and so has he.*

---

**[PANEL: The library entrance. The west courtyard. Empty. The window on the third floor: lit from inside. In the glass of the window: a reflection. The reflection shows the courtyard as it was, perhaps, in a different decade — the same bricks, different light, a different quality of air. Nobody in the courtyard currently. The absence is more visible than a presence would be.]**

## CHAPTER 3 — "PROTAGONIST B MAKES A CHOICE"

---

Protagonist B heard about the documentary crew from three different people before lunch.

Version one (from a friend): a documentary about someone called Tyler, filming local witnesses who'd seen him, very boring, they just asked if you'd seen anything unusual.

Version two (from the school office): a project from a media studies program, all proper permits, nothing to worry about.

Version three (from the Edinburgh Exchange Student, who was somehow already on a first-name basis with the Camera Op): "They're not from a media studies program. The permits are real but the project isn't what the permits describe. The Camera Op is interesting. The rest of them don't know what they're making."

The Exchange Student said this in a tone that suggested they were leaving several things unsaid and were aware that this was what they were doing.

Protagonist B asked: "What are they making?"

The Exchange Student: "That's a question worth keeping."

---

The documentary crew's setup was in the school's main hallway during lunch. A camera on a tripod. A mic boom. The Camera Op standing to one side, watching the space without filming it. Someone in an earpiece who gave the impression of listening to a voice nobody else could hear.

Protagonist B watched them for a long time. Then walked over.

"Can I ask what you're filming?"

The Camera Op looked at them. Directly, with the particular attention of someone who has learned to see things other people miss. They said: "A documentary about someone who was seen in this neighborhood."

"Tyler."

A pause. The Camera Op said: "Yes."

"Is he dangerous?"

Another pause. Longer. The Camera Op looked at Protagonist B the way someone looks at a question they've been waiting for someone to ask. Not alarmed. Something more complicated than alarmed.

They said: "No. I don't think so."

"You don't think so?"

"I know it's not a complete answer."

Protagonist B nodded. They asked: "Can you tell me more about what you're looking for? I might have seen something."

---

This is the choice. Not the interview they gave that afternoon — honest, detailed, the kind of testimony that anyone being documented would give without knowing the documentation was anything other than what it said it was. Not the way they answered every question completely.

The choice was: they asked.

They walked across the hallway. They said can I ask. They decided, in that moment, that knowing what was happening was better than not knowing, and that if something unusual was happening in their school — their impossible school, which appeared on Thursday with scuff marks already in the linoleum — they wanted to be part of the record of it.

They believed the record was neutral. They believed documentation was observation. They had not yet understood that observation is participation.

The Camera Op, that night, reviewed the interview footage. Protagonist B had given a complete, honest account of every unusual thing they'd noticed at the school. Everything. The brick that was wrong for the decade. The fire exit map font. The man at the library entrance.

The Camera Op added a note to their log: *Subject #2 — spontaneous, unprompted, complete. No apparent agenda. This is the most honest interview I've filmed.*

They paused. They looked at the note.

*It's also the most useful interview I've filmed.*

They sat with that for a while.
