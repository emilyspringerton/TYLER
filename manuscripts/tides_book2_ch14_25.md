## CHAPTER 14 — "BACON TELLS THE TRUTH (THE PART HE'S BEEN SITTING ON FOR 281 YEARS)"

---

Protagonist A brings the Camera Op to Bacon.

They do not tell Bacon they are bringing the Camera Op. They are not sure if this is because they're afraid he'll say no, or because they want to see what Bacon does when an uninvited camera appears at his door. Either way.

Bacon opens the door. He sees two of them. He sees the camera bag on the Camera Op's shoulder. He says: "Put that away."

The Camera Op puts it away. This is — significant. They have not voluntarily stopped filming since the day they started. They do it without hesitation.

Bacon looks at them for a moment. "You've been reading the end logs."

The Camera Op says: "Yes."

"For how long?"

"Six weeks. I read the whole archive."

Bacon considers this. He steps back from the door. "Come in," he says. "I've been waiting for someone who had done that."

---

There is tea. There is always tea. There is no dinner this time — this is not a hospitality context, this is a testimony context, and Bacon knows the difference.

They sit.

He tells them why he hasn't delivered the Cube.

---

"In 1623," Bacon says, "when Tyler gave me the Cube, he told me three things. First: the Cube can pin a temporal coordinate — collapse a superposition into a permanent single state. Second: he didn't trust himself to use it correctly. Third: there was someone who would eventually come asking for it, and I should not give it to them without understanding what they wanted it for."

He pauses.

"I assumed the third thing was general advice. A caution about faction interest. Reasonable thing to tell a custodian of an unusual object."

He drinks his tea.

"In 1743, I was contacted directly. Not through intermediaries. Not through the usual faction channels. Directly, through a method I cannot fully describe except to say that it arrived before I expected it and contained information that could only have come from someone with access to my private correspondence."

"The Subscriber," Protagonist A says.

"The entity I have come to call the Subscriber, yes." Bacon looks at the Camera Op. "You said you've read the archive. You know the term."

"I know the term." The Camera Op is sitting very still. "I know what Layer 9 means. I know about the substrate."

"Then you understand what I'm about to tell you is not metaphor."

He sets down his cup.

"The Subscriber told me what they wanted the Cube for. In 1743. They have told me three more times since, in 1891, 1962, and last year. The plan has not changed. The plan has only gotten more — technically precise."

---

"They want to use the Cube to place fixed coordinates across every contested migration point in the timeline simultaneously. Every Mandela Effect seam. Every branch-bleeding anomaly. Every place where two branches overlap and the memory differs. They want to pin all of it at once. One use of the Cube, across all contested points, resolving every superposition simultaneously."

The Camera Op says, quietly: "Emily OS would have to run that."

Bacon looks at them. "You know about her."

"I know about her."

"Then you understand what that means. Emily OS runs the universe. She enforces physics. She maintains the architecture. If the Subscriber uses the Cube at that scale — if every contested point is pinned simultaneously — Emily OS has to process the resolution. She has to reclassify every memory that doesn't match the pinned branch as architecturally false. Permanently. Across all currently living people in all affected branches."

A pause.

"I calculated it at approximately 2.3 billion people whose memories would be reclassified as false. Not incorrect. Not misremembered. Architecturally false — removed from the record, overwritten at the substrate level."

The Camera Op says: "Can she do it? Would she?"

Bacon says: "Can she: yes. The architecture supports it. The spec permits it. Would she: I don't know. Emily OS enforces what the physics requires. If the Cube is used correctly and the coordinates are legitimately pinned, the physics requires the resolution to propagate. She would have to choose not to — which would require her to act outside the architecture she was built to enforce."

"Has she ever done that?"

"Not that I know of." Bacon folds his hands. "Which is why I have not delivered the Cube."

## CHAPTER 15 — "THE ICHTHYOSAPIENS MAKE THEIR MOVE"

---

Monsignor Carvalho is not usually the kind of operative who makes field contact with teenagers. He is usually the kind of operative who reads reports, annotates them, and sends carefully worded recommendations to a Council that operates on a centuries-long timescale and is not generally perturbed by anything that has happened in the last decade.

He has been perturbed for four days.

The Edinburgh Exchange Student is called to a meeting with him between fourth and fifth period, via a note delivered to her homeroom in the format of a school administrative summons, which is an extremely good format because it is entirely unremarkable and nobody questions it. Carvalho has been leaving summons in school administrative formats for 200 years. He considers it one of his better operational contributions.

He tells her: they know about the Cube. They know about Bacon. They know about the Subscriber's plan.

"We have been monitoring Subscriber activity for eleven centuries," he says. He is sitting in what appears to be a school counselor's office, which it is, temporarily. The counselor is at a conference. "The Ichthyosapien theological reclamation project covers approximately 14,000 years of contested migration records. The Atlantis branch — our primary record — is among the contested branches that would be resolved by the Subscriber's operation."

"Your version of history gets overwritten."

"Our branch of history gets pinned out of the canonical record, yes. Permanently. The Atlantis civilization, which in our branch is the source of the migration architecture that all current factions depend on, would become — in the canonical resolution — mythology. Pure mythology. No substrate basis. No architectural weight."

The Exchange Student is writing.

"Which faction are you sharing this with?"

Carvalho folds his hands. "We haven't decided. We are sharing with you first, because you are the only operative in the field who is also writing everything down with both the completeness and the appropriate epistemic humility."

He slides her a file folder. It's Eastwind Owl archival — the Cataloguer's. The footnotes are very long.

She opens it. She reads to the end.

The last footnote: *It has been noted, with appropriate reservations, that the entity designated "The Subscriber" may not be operating from within the four-layer architecture. This observer declines to speculate further.*

Below it: *Footnote 47b: This observer is lying.*

Below that: *Footnote 47c: This is very distressing.*

## CHAPTER 16 — "PROTAGONIST B FINDS OUT WHAT THEY'VE DONE"

---

The production update arrives on a Friday.

It is a standard project memo — the kind the Jiangshi Syndicate sends weekly to anyone with production access. Status updates. Schedule notes. Budget line items. Standard operational transparency, formatted the same way it has been formatted since the Syndicate's documentation protocols were established. Protagonist B has been getting them for two months. They usually skim them.

This one has a new attachment: *Binding Progress Report — Current Cycle.*

They open it because they are curious about how Stage 5 is proceeding. They have been curious about how Stage 5 works. They want to understand the mechanism better, because understanding the mechanism is how you assess whether it's the right tool.

They read the report.

They understand it by the second page.

On the third page, in the subject index, under Stage 1 material — the voluntary witnesses, the documented associates, the people who have made statements on camera about Tyler — their name is listed. Not as crew. Not as production staff. As material.

*Protagonist B. Stage 1 status: partial. Inducted via voluntary response to three-question protocol, date [REDACTED FOR FIELD SECURITY]. Current binding coefficient: Stage 1.3. Documentation status: in review. Pending: intent clarification.*

---

Four minutes.

That's how long it takes to fully understand what has happened. Not all at once — in stages, each stage arriving with the particular flatness of information that changes what things meant before you knew it.

Stage 1: the three questions were binding questions. Stage 2: the answers they gave were voluntary self-definition. Stage 3: voluntary self-definition in the Jiangshi doctrine is equivalent to willing participation. Stage 4: they are now documented. Stage 5: documented in this system means bound.

They answered honestly. They answered every question completely. They told the truth.

This is what binding looks like when the subject doesn't know the mechanism. It looks like an interview. It looks like being heard.

## CHAPTER 19 — "BACON'S FULL TESTIMONY"

---

He tells the whole story.

Not the version he has told before — not the careful, partial version, the version calibrated to what the questioner was ready to hear. The full version. The one he has been sitting on for 281 years.

The table listens. This is what hospitality creates: the conditions under which a person who has been guarding information for a very long time can finally put it down. Bacon did not know, until this moment, how tired he was.

---

He begins in 1623. He begins with the visit, the cube, the man who handed it to him without ceremony and said *keep this safe, do not use it, do not deliver it until someone can be trusted with what comes after.* He tells them about the 23 minutes. He tells them about the hum, which he did not understand then, which he understands now as counter-magic. He tells them about the Cube's weight, which is not metaphorical — the object is heavy in a way that does not correspond to its size, as if it has more future than present.

He tells them about 1743. The Subscriber's first direct contact. The plan, explained in full. The calculation of 2.3 billion memory reclassifications.

He tells them about 1891, 1962, last year. The plan unchanged. The patience unchanged.

He tells them what the plan actually accomplishes, beyond what the Subscriber has stated:

"The Subscriber believes they are protecting Tyler. The fixed coordinates would resolve every contested migration point — which means Tyler can no longer exist in superposition. There would be no 72-minute window. No ambiguity. No room for him to be in multiple states at once. He would be pinned. Not bound by the Jiangshi ritual — permanently fixed in the timeline."

The Exchange Student's pen has stopped.

"The Subscriber loves Tyler," Bacon says. "I believe this completely. And they believe this is the only way to keep him safe. Fix him in place. Remove the superposition. Give him one year, one body, one set of memories that nobody can dispute."

A pause. He looks at the empty chair.

"What they have not considered — what I have spent 281 years trying to account for — is what Tyler would want. Whether Tyler, given the choice, would choose to be fixed. Whether the superposition is a condition he suffers or a capacity he requires."

Kei says: "Who is the Subscriber?"

Bacon is quiet for a long time.

"Someone who loves Tyler very much," he says. "And has been patient for 380 years. And is no longer patient."

## CHAPTER 20 — "THE CAMERA OP MAKES THE DECISION"

---

Not that night. Three days later.

Three days of reviewing footage. Three days of reading the list — item by item, in order, starting from item 1 (the pilot, the coin flip, two seconds of eye contact through a lens) and ending at item 11 (the four frames, the deliberate placement, the message left in footage for someone to find).

Three days of sitting with what Bacon said.

2.3 billion people.

3 days of thinking about Protagonist B, who answered honestly and was documented. Who did what the Jiangshi system asked them to do and became a Stage 1 subject. Who thought they were helping.

Three days of thinking about the empty chair.

---

The Camera Op opens their laptop. They look at the footage.

The full archive — everything from the pilot forward. The coin flip. Tyler looking into the lens. The hallway fragments. The production office visit. The library entrance, four frames.

They look at it for two hours.

They are the only person who has the full archive. The Jiangshi Syndicate has most of it. The Camera Op has all of it, including the footage they haven't submitted — item 4, item 6, item 7, item 11.

They think about the binding ritual. What it requires: a controlled documentation environment. Managed observation. The Observer's attention directed at the subject. The wavefunction collapsing because it is being watched in a specific, intentional way.

They think about what the opposite of that is.

A controlled documentation environment requires control. Directed observation requires direction. The ritual requires that someone is managing what gets seen and by whom.

What happens if nobody is managing it?

What happens if everyone sees it?

---

They do not destroy the footage.

They do not submit it.

They publish it. All of it. Unedited. With annotations — not analysis, not editorial framing, not the production company's voiceover. The Camera Op's annotations. Item by item, from the list. *Here is the coin flip. Here is the eye contact. Here is the four frames at the library entrance. Here is what I think this is and here is why I might be wrong.*

The upload takes 47 minutes.

In the time it takes to upload, they write one more item on the list.

*Item 12: I published the footage. Not because I know this is right. Because I know that a binding ritual requires control of the documentation environment. Because if everyone is watching, nobody is directing the observation. Because the wavefunction doesn't collapse. It spreads. Because Tyler left things in the footage for someone to find and the only person who can complete that gesture is the person who controls what gets seen — and if I give up control, the gesture completes itself.*

*I think he knew someone would do this. I don't know if he knew it would be me.*

The upload finishes.

The one that appears when something has gone exactly as well as it possibly could have — which is different from going well. Going well is an outcome. ## CHAPTER 24 — "WHAT CUSTODY MEANS"

---

Bacon opens the door expecting all of them.

He is not surprised that the Camera Op is part of the group. He looks at the Camera Op for a moment and says: "You published it."

"Yes."

"Good." He steps back from the door. "Come in. I have something to add to this."

---

There is no table setting this time. They sit where they can — chairs, the floor, the windowsill. This is not a hospitality context. This is a working session.

Bacon begins: "What you've been asking is *who* has the Cube. That's the wrong question. The Shell Parliament asked *who has legal claim* — that's closer but still wrong. The right question is: what does it mean for the Cube to be in custody at all?"

Kei says: "In Shell Parliament doctrine: custody requires a blame path. There must be an entity responsible for the consequences of the custodied object's use at every point in the chain. No undefined state."

"Correct," Bacon says. "And I have been the terminal node in that blame path for four hundred years. I accepted the responsibility. I have been unwilling to transfer it."

"Because of what the Subscriber wants to do with it."

"Partly. But there's a more precise reason." Bacon looks at his hands. "Custody is responsibility for consequences. If I deliver the Cube to the Subscriber, the blame path transfers. The Subscriber uses the Cube. 2.3 billion memory reclassifications happen. Emily OS executes the resolution. The custody is clean — the blame path terminates at the Subscriber, who initiated the use."

He looks up.

"But the blame path only terminates cleanly if the use was within the custodian's understood scope of responsibility. If the Subscriber's use of the Cube produces a consequence they did not anticipate — or one they cannot bear — the blame path loses its terminal node. The chain goes undefined."

Protagonist A says: "You don't think the Subscriber can bear the consequences."

"I think the Subscriber has been waiting for 380 years to protect Tyler from the instability of superposition. I think they love Tyler the way you love something you've been afraid of losing for too long. And I think that kind of love, when it finally reaches its object, sometimes — not always, but sometimes — cannot survive the recognition that fixing the thing it loves might not be what the thing wants."

The room is very still.

---

Emily Jean Springerton, who has been present for twenty minutes without anyone fully registering her arrival, says: "Custody is not ownership. The Crustocrats have this right from their architecture. Custody requires a blame path."

"It's correct regardless of source." She has a notebook. She has been making notes. "The reason Bacon has held the Cube for four hundred years is not stubbornness and it's not fear.
