## CHAPTER 8 — "THE GEOMETRY"

---

On Day 7, all three of them were in the library at the same time.

Not arranged. Not orchestrated. The Exchange Student was there because he was always in the library. Protagonist A was there because they had been going to the library every day after school since the incident with the homework margin and the question Callum had left in the air. Protagonist B was there because — they would try to explain this later and couldn't quite — because they'd felt, sometime around sixth period, that the library was where they needed to be.

Protagonist B walked through the library door and stopped.

The Exchange Student was at his usual table, writing. Protagonist A was at a table near the window, reading. Neither of them had noticed the other yet. The library had the specific late-afternoon quality of a place that doesn't know it's about to become significant.

Protagonist B stood in the doorway for a moment.

They went to the third table — the one that completed the triangle.

They sat down. They did not speak. Nobody spoke.

After approximately four minutes, without looking up from his notebook, the Exchange Student said: "This is interesting."

"What is?" Protagonist A said.

"The three of us in this room," the Exchange Student said. "It's the first time."

Protagonist B, who had not been included in any of the previous conversations and did not know yet how much the Exchange Student knew about them, said: "Is that significant?"

The Exchange Student looked up. He looked at Protagonist B, then at Protagonist A, then at the space between all three of them — the specific geometric center of the triangle formed by three tables in a library on a Tuesday afternoon.

"I don't know yet," he said. "But I think so."

He went back to writing.

For the next two hours, they did not have a conversation. They had three parallel conversations, each with the Exchange Student at the center. He talked to Protagonist A about the geological survey and the Edinburgh field note (careful, precise, giving Protagonist A exactly as much as he'd calculated they could receive at this stage). He talked to Protagonist B about the documentary, about the Camera Op's methodology, about what it meant to walk across a hallway toward something instead of away from it.

He did not introduce Protagonist A and Protagonist B to each other.

He did not need to. Sometime around the second hour, both of them had become aware that there was a third person in the room. They had not looked directly at each other. But the library's particular quality of attention had shifted — the way a room shifts when everybody in it is aware of something they're not acknowledging.

At seven o'clock, the library lights shifted toward closing. The Exchange Student closed his notebook. He said, to the room rather than either specific person: "I need to show both of you something. Not tonight. Day 10."

He left.

Protagonist A and Protagonist B were alone in the library.

They looked at each other.

"Hi," Protagonist B said.

"Hi," said Protagonist A.

This is not the scene. This is the setup. The scene is Day 9.

## CHAPTER 9 — "WHAT BELETH DOES WHEN IT'S PERSONAL"

---

Day 9 was the documentary crew's last scheduled day at the school.

The Senior Producer had announced this on Day 7: they would wrap school filming on Day 9, move to external locations. The Camera Op had filed this with a note she didn't share with the production team: *the school is not done with us. The school being done with us and us being done with the school are not the same thing.*

She was correct. The school was not done.

---

Protagonist A was in the east hallway, again, for reasons that were entirely explicable (their locker was there, they had a reason to be there, everything was ordinary) and that felt, in the specific way things feel when they are inevitable rather than coincidental, like the location had arranged itself around them rather than the other way around.

Protagonist B came around the corner from the stairwell.

This had happened before. On Day 5. The seven seconds. The three rectangles of light. Both of them walking away in opposite directions. They had not discussed this. They had not discussed it with the Exchange Student, though both of them separately suspected he knew.

This time neither of them walked away.

The difference between Day 5 and Day 9 is not dramatic. It is very small. On Day 5, both of them felt the frequency and made the decision, without deciding, to end the encounter. On Day 9, both of them felt the frequency and made the decision, without deciding, not to.

What followed was twelve minutes of conversation in the east hallway about the documentary, about the school, about Tyler and the library entrance and the way the building seemed to know where everything was supposed to be. Twelve minutes. The Beleth signal, which had been ambient since Day 5, went from ambient to personal. Not a spike — the field activation log would record it as a sustained elevation rather than a detonation. The detonation came later.

The detonation was a sentence.

Protagonist B said: "I've been thinking about the east hallway. Day 5. Have you—" A pause. "I don't know how to ask this."

Protagonist A said: "I know."

Protagonist B said: "You felt it."

Protagonist A said: "Yes."

Protagonist B said: "What do you think it was?"

Protagonist A thought about the Exchange Student's answer: *a signal that something is beginning. Something much bigger than what you felt.* They thought about this. They looked at Protagonist B — at their face, which was doing something complicated and careful, the face of someone who has asked a question knowing the answer might change something.

Protagonist A said: "I think it was real."

Protagonist B said: "I think it was too."

The detonation is not always loud. Sometimes it is two people in a hallway saying they think something was real. Sometimes the 2.22 Hz of love as a frequency is two people confirming to each other that they both felt something. The field activation log registered this as the highest Beleth reading in the school record. It lasted four seconds. Then it resolved into a lower sustained hum.

Neither of them knew what to do with this. The conversation ended without concluding. They went in different directions. Both of them, independently, went to the library and found the Exchange Student.

The Exchange Student looked at each of them in turn. He said, again to the room rather than either specifically: "Okay."

He didn't explain what he meant. He made a note in his notebook. He looked at the door.

The one resolution: both of them knew the east hallway was real.

The one complication: the Exchange Student.

He looked at Protagonist A and Protagonist B in the library and he knew, and they could see that he knew, that something had shifted. And he looked at Protagonist A specifically — with the look of someone who is holding information about a person that the person doesn't have about themselves — and he said: "Day 10. I need to show you both something."

He was looking at Protagonist A when he said "both." The word was addressed to both of them. But the look was for Protagonist A. Protagonist A understood, in the way you understand things that have not been said but have been shown, that whatever the Exchange Student needed to show them — it was primarily for them. The information was theirs in particular. Protagonist B was the witness.

This is the open wound: Protagonist A did not ask for this. The Exchange Student has been deciding how much they need to know and when, and the decision is his, and Protagonist A is the subject of the decision. What you are in a story matters less than who is telling it.

Protagonist A went home. Wrote in their notebook: *What happens on Day 10.*

## CHAPTER 10 — "TYLER IN THE LIBRARY"

---

Day 8, after school, was the third Tyler sighting.

Protagonist A was alone in the library. The Exchange Student was not there — for the first time since they'd started coming. Protagonist B was in a scheduled documentary interview (the Camera Op had asked for a follow-up). The library was quiet in the specific way it was quiet when only one person was in it who was paying attention.

Tyler came in.

Not the east entrance. The west entrance, the one that connected to the courtyard. He opened it precisely, the way someone opens a door who knows exactly how much force it requires. He did not look at Protagonist A. He went directly to the stacks in the northwest corner — the section Protagonist A had identified, on Day 2, as containing things that predated the school's supposed founding.

He stayed there for eleven minutes.

Protagonist A did not move. They watched. From the angle they were at, they could see the section but not the specific shelves. They could see Tyler's hands when he reached for something. They could see, when he turned slightly, the profile of a man looking at a book with the quality of someone reading something they already know in order to remember it correctly.

He put the book back. He left through the west entrance.

Protagonist A waited thirty seconds. Then went to the northwest corner.

The section: philosophy, natural history, 17th and 18th century. The shelf he had been at: a gap where a volume had been recently returned. The books on either side of the gap: a 1982 collected edition of Restoration-era pamphlets, and a 1979 reference encyclopedia of early modern natural philosophy.

The book he had been holding: Protagonist A scanned the gap. Both volumes on either side were angled slightly away from it, the way books angle when a neighbor has been removed and replaced frequently. They pulled the 1979 encyclopedia. Wrong — it was undisturbed.

They pulled the other one. Inside the front cover: a library card. Detroit Preparatory Institute. Checked out three times. The checkout dates: 1947, 1966, and the most recent — the date stamp was smeared, unreadable, except for the year: 2022.

They opened the book to the table of contents.

The pamphlets listed were mostly anonymous. One was not. One entry, page 847: *New Atlantis, Francis Bacon, published 1623. Excerpt: "We have also houses of deceits of the senses, where we represent all manner of feats of juggling, false apparitions, impostures and illusions..."*

They read the excerpt. They did not understand what it meant yet. They wrote the page number in their notebook: 847. They wrote: *New Atlantis. Bacon. 1623.*

They put the book back.

## CHAPTER 11 — "THE GRANDFATHER'S ACCOUNT"

---

Day 10.

The Exchange Student had his grandfather's notebook. Not a copy — the actual notebook. Worn cover, pages thin from use, the specific smell of something that had been written in and referred to and referred to again across decades.

He set it on the table between the three of them.

"My grandfather," he said, "met Tyler in Edinburgh in 1981. Not in a school — in a library. The Edinburgh Central Library. He was doing research. Tyler was using the reference section. My grandfather is — he was a careful observer. He wrote down everything."

He opened the notebook.

The entries were in dense, careful handwriting. Cross-references. Dates. A methodology that looked, to Protagonist B's eye, like the approach of someone who had decided that what they were observing was worth documenting correctly.

"He saw Tyler three times in the library over the course of a week," Callum said. "The third time, Tyler sat at the table where he was working. They didn't speak. But my grandfather noticed that Tyler was working from a reference text. He looked at the title when Tyler stood to return it."

"What was it?" Protagonist A asked.

"A survey of 17th-century British natural philosophy. He wrote the title in the margin here." He pointed. "And below it, something Tyler had written in the book's margin. My grandfather had good eyes. He wrote down what he read."

He pointed to an entry. The entry read:

*The book had a margin annotation in old handwriting. The handwriting read: "The island is not an island. It is a method." Below that, in what appeared to be a different hand and a different era: "B — the correct location is not the text but the custodian." Below that, the same handwriting as the original annotation: "Still correct."*

"'Still correct,'" Protagonist A said.

"He added that last line himself," Callum said. "In Edinburgh. In 1981. My grandfather watched him."

"The island," Protagonist B said.

"New Atlantis," Protagonist A said. They looked at Callum. "The island in New Atlantis."

The Exchange Student looked at them. His expression was the expression of someone watching a variable arrive at a value he had calculated in advance. "My grandfather left the notebook for me specifically. He said: 'when you find the school that always existed, find the person who will understand that.'"

He was looking at Protagonist A.

"He didn't mean Protagonist B," Protagonist A said.

"He didn't mean me," Protagonist B confirmed, without rancor. "I walked across the hallway. I'm the witness. You're—"

A pause. Protagonist B looked at Protagonist A with an expression that was complicated by the east hallway and the Beleth and everything that had happened in the past ten days.

"He said the person who would know where the custodian is," Callum said. "He said they wouldn't know they knew. He said: look for the one who writes in the margin."

Protagonist A thought about the history homework. *The school has always been there, and so has he.* Written before they understood anything.

"The custodian of what?" Protagonist A asked.

Callum looked at them. He said: "That is the load-bearing question."

He didn't answer it. He couldn't — he didn't know. The grandfather's account ended there. Whatever the custodian was custodian of: the account didn't say. The grandfather had observed Tyler writing "still correct" in a margin in a library in Edinburgh in 1981. He had written it down. He had given the notebook to Callum.

Callum had brought the notebook to the school that always existed.

The school had brought Protagonist A to the library.

The library had put them at the shelf with the 1979 encyclopedia.

"847," Protagonist A said.

"What?" Protagonist B.

"Page 847. The New Atlantis excerpt." They pulled their notebook. Showed them the entry. "I was in the library on Day 8. Tyler was there. He was at that shelf. He put the book back."

Callum looked at the notebook page. He looked at Protagonist A. He said: "He knew you would find it."

## CHAPTER 12 — "THE DRY INTERVAL BEGINS"

---

Day 12 was the last day Tyler was in the school.

Protagonist A knew this afterward. At the time, Day 12 felt like any other day in the school — the particular quality of an institution that has always existed and intends to keep existing, the brick and light and schedule that would go on regardless. They did not know it was Tyler's last day in the building.

He appeared in the library again. Not the northwest corner — this time he was at the main reading tables, the long ones in the center. He was sitting. He had a book open. He was reading.

Protagonist A came into the library and stopped.

Tyler did not look up.

Protagonist A sat at the table perpendicular to his. They opened their own book. They did not look at him.

For twenty minutes they were both in the library reading.

At the twenty-minute mark, Tyler closed his book. He stood. He put the book back — not the northwest stack, the return cart, the ordinary return cart that any student would use. He picked up his jacket from the chair back.

He looked at the window. Then, not at Protagonist A — at the room, at the space — he said, quietly:

"The island is not in the text."

He walked out through the west entrance.

Protagonist A sat very still for a long time.

*The island is not in the text.*

From the grandfather's account: *The island is not an island. It is a method.*

From Tyler, one sentence, not addressed to Protagonist A, addressed to the air: *the island is not in the text.*

Protagonist A wrote in their notebook: *The custodian is not in the book. The book is a record. The custodian is a person.*

They looked at this.

*Bacon.*

Not a place. Francis Bacon had published New Atlantis in 1623. Tyler had said in 1623 he was given something to keep safe — the Camera Op's footage had this, though Protagonist A would not see that footage for another year and a half. In 1623 someone had given Tyler something. Tyler had been keeping it safe. "Still correct." Edinburgh, 1981.

Protagonist A did not know, sitting in the library on Day 12, that what Tyler had been given in 1623 was a Chrono-Cube, or that the person who gave it to him was Francis Bacon, or that Bacon — a name, not a location — was where Tyler had kept it. They knew only: 1623, a custodian, an island that is not an island but a method.

The dry interval is what the universe calls the period between understanding something and knowing what it means. Between the information and the interpretation. Between the seed and the plant.

The dry interval began on Day 12 in the library.

The school continued. Protagonist A continued attending it. The Edinburgh Exchange Student continued his enrollment. Protagonist B continued interviewing, continued their margin notes, continued being in the east hallway by coincidence.

The documentary crew left on Day 14. The Camera Op kept her footage. She filed one final production note: *The school is real. The school is also what the school is. Both of these are in the record.*

Tyler did not return to the school.

The dry interval held.

Three days after the love triangle detonated — which is to say, three days after something finally moved at 2.22 Hz in the east hallway into a different register than it had been — Protagonist A woke up at 6:47 in the morning and lay in bed looking at the ceiling and thought: *Bacon is a person. The custodian is Bacon. Bacon has been alive since 1623, which is the same year Tyler was given something to keep safe.*

They got up. They got dressed. They went to school.

At the school entrance, the Exchange Student was waiting with a file folder.

He said: "I need to show you something and I need you not to tell anyone I showed you."

*End of Book 1.*
