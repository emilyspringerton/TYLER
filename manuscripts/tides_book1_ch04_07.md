## CHAPTER 4 — "WHAT THEY DON'T KNOW ABOUT EACH OTHER"

---

Protagonist A had never been asked to be a source before.

On the second day, a production assistant found them between second and third period and asked, very politely, whether they'd be willing to give a brief on-camera statement about the neighborhood — general impressions, nothing specific required, five minutes of their time.

Protagonist A said no.

They said no because the correct answer to a stranger asking if you want to be on camera, between second and third period of a school that has existed since Thursday, is no. They said no and went to third period.

In third period, Protagonist B was sitting two seats to their left.

They had not noticed Protagonist B in any previous class, which was strange because when you noticed Protagonist B you noticed them — the precise, quiet quality of someone who was always paying more attention than the situation seemed to require. Not a performer of attention. An actual practitioner of it.

Protagonist B was taking notes. This was not notable. Everyone took notes in third period. What was notable was what they were taking notes about. Not the class — Protagonist A could see the angle of their pen, the movement of their hand. The notes were going down the left margin of the page. Margin notes. A whole separate column of observation, parallel to whatever the official notes were.

Protagonist A looked away before Protagonist B looked up.

They did not speak.

After school, in the library, the Edinburgh Exchange Student found Protagonist A.

This was not a coincidence, though it looked like one. The Exchange Student had a visible reason to be in the library (books) and a visible reason to sit at the table near the window (light). They opened a book. They appeared to be reading.

They said, without looking up: "You were in second period today."

"Yes," Protagonist A said.

"The production assistant found you between second and third."

A pause. "Yes."

"You said no."

"Yes."

The Exchange Student made a note in the margin of whatever they were reading. They said: "The Camera Op said the people who say no are usually more interesting than the people who say yes."

Protagonist A sat down. Not because they had decided to. Because the sentence had gravity.

"The Camera Op said that about me specifically?"

"The Camera Op said it in general." The Exchange Student turned a page. "But they were looking in your direction when they said it."

---

**[PANEL: The library. Window light. The Exchange Student and Protagonist A across a table from each other. The Exchange Student's book is open. The Camera Op is visible in the background, in a hallway visible through the library's glass door, walking past without looking in. Neither the Exchange Student nor Protagonist A is watching the Camera Op. They are watching each other. The Camera Op is not watching them. This is filmed.]**

---

The library had been there since before the building, in the same way the school had always existed. The card catalog was real — not a prop, not a decoration. The books had stamps inside their covers with due dates going back decades. The periodicals section had issues of magazines from years that, if you did the math, were before the current librarian was born.

Protagonist A did the math.

"How long have you been at this school?" they asked.

The Exchange Student looked up for the first time. They had grey eyes and the kind of glasses that make you look like you're studying something even when you're just standing in a room. "Officially, three weeks. I came from Edinburgh as part of an exchange program."

"And unofficially?"

A long pause. The Exchange Student looked at their book. Back up. "What did you write in your history homework margin? On Thursday."

Protagonist A felt their stomach do something that was not quite fear and not quite recognition. It was the feeling of someone knowing a thing they couldn't have known.

"How did you—"

"What did you write?"

A long pause.

"That the school had always been there," Protagonist A said. "And so had he."

The Exchange Student closed their book. They said: "That's the most accurate observation anyone has made about this school since it appeared."

They picked up their bag. They left.

They did not explain what they meant. They didn't need to. Protagonist A sat in the library until the building's lights shifted toward evening closing, and they thought about what it meant that an Exchange Student from Edinburgh, in the third week of an officially three-week exchange, knew what they'd written in a homework margin.

They did not find an answer. They found something better: a question worth keeping.

## CHAPTER 5 — "THE EDINBURGH EXCHANGE STUDENT KNOWS MORE THAN THEY'RE SAYING"

---

The Edinburgh Exchange Student's name was Callum.

Protagonist B found this out on Day 3 by the straightforward method of introducing themselves. Protagonist A found this out on Day 4 by asking the Exchange Student directly.

The Exchange Student answered to Callum or to the Exchange Student depending on who was asking. Protagonist B he answered to Callum. Protagonist A he answered to the Exchange Student with a small expression that suggested this was the correct distance and he was comfortable with it.

This is not significant yet. It will be.

They are not aware of each other's awareness. The stairwell light comes from a skylight that should not exist in a building of this construction era but does. Everyone is lit from above.]**

On Day 3, the Exchange Student found Protagonist B between fourth and fifth period and told them, without preamble: "You gave the Camera Op the most complete interview they've filmed. You told them about the library entrance."

Protagonist B had told the Camera Op about a man at the library entrance. Not a lot. An impression. A man looking at a window, the specific quality of someone looking at something they had been thinking about for a long time.

"Was that wrong?" Protagonist B asked.

"No," the Exchange Student said. "But you should know it's been filed."

"Filed where?"

The Exchange Student looked at them with the expression of someone deciding how much to translate. "The people doing the documentary. They keep a record. The record is more formal than you know. You're in it now."

A pause. "Is that bad?"

"No," the Exchange Student said. "But it means you're visible. And being visible to this particular record means something."

Protagonist B thought about this. They said: "What does it mean?"

The Exchange Student said: "That's a question worth keeping."

He walked away. Protagonist B noticed, for the first time, that the Exchange Student moved through the school the way someone moves through a building they have memorized — not the tentative navigation of someone three weeks into an unfamiliar place, but the economy of motion of someone who knows where everything is and has stopped needing to think about it.

They filed this away in the separate column of their margin notes.

---

On Day 4, Protagonist A asked the Exchange Student: "Are you in this school because of the documentary?"

"No," the Exchange Student said.

"Are you connected to the documentary?"

A very long pause. "I'm connected to the subject."

"Tyler."

"The man at the library entrance, yes."

Protagonist A looked at the Exchange Student. The Exchange Student was doing what he always did — not quite looking at Protagonist A, looking at the space a few degrees adjacent to them, as though the most interesting thing was slightly off to one side.

"How?"

"My grandfather," the Exchange Student said. "In Edinburgh. In 1981."

He let this sit. The implication was obvious: in 1981, someone in Edinburgh had seen Tyler or someone who looked exactly like Tyler, and Callum's grandfather had told Callum about it, and now Callum was here.

In a school that appeared on Thursday with scuff marks already in the linoleum.

"Your grandfather sent you here?" Protagonist A said.

"My grandfather told me to look for a school," the Exchange Student said. "I found it."

He made a note in his notebook. Whatever he wrote, he wrote quickly, without looking at the page.

"He told you to come to Detroit," Protagonist A said. "He told you to find a school that appeared."

"He said if I found a school that seemed to always have existed, I should stay."

A pause. Protagonist A looked at the window at the end of the hall. The window that looked onto the west courtyard. The library entrance, visible from the right angle.

"Are you staying?"

"I'm enrolled through the end of term," the Exchange Student said. "After that—" A small gesture. Not a shrug. Something more precise. An acknowledgment that "after that" was genuinely uncertain in a way that not many "after thats" were.

## CHAPTER 6 — "THE FREQUENCY"

---

Day 5. Last period.

Protagonist A was in the east hallway when Protagonist B came around the corner from the stairwell.

This had happened before — they were in overlapping territory, the school was not large, collisions were statistically inevitable. The previous collisions had produced the ordinary frictionless contact of people who don't know each other yet: the brief recalibration of direction, the nod or non-nod depending on the specific geometry of the encounter, the immediate return to forward motion.

This one was different in a way Protagonist A could not, afterward, explain.

They stopped.

Not because Protagonist B was blocking the hallway. Not because anything notable happened. Protagonist B came around the corner and Protagonist A stopped and the hallway was very quiet for a moment and the light was the light of late afternoon coming through the east-facing windows at the flat angle of a day that was almost finished.

Protagonist B also stopped.

They looked at each other across approximately eight feet of linoleum.

The Exchange Student later — much later, when Protagonist A asked — said that the frequency at 2.22 Hz is not perceptible to human hearing, and that human bodies are capable of feeling things that aren't sound, and that what Protagonist A and Protagonist B felt in the east hallway on Day 5 was not something either of them caused and was not something either of them could have prevented.

He said this matter-of-factly, the way you describe a weather phenomenon to someone who has just experienced one.

In the east hallway, on Day 5, none of this was available as language. There was only: the hallway. Eight feet of linoleum. The flat afternoon light. Protagonist B's face, which was doing something complicated. Protagonist A's own face, which they couldn't see but which was probably doing the same thing.

Seven seconds.

Then the bell.

Both of them walked away. In opposite directions. Without speaking.

Three buildings away, in the Syndicate's mobile production unit, the Field Activation Log registered a Beleth signal at 2.22 Hz. Duration: approximately seven seconds. Location: consistent with the school building, east wing.

The production team notified the Senior Producer. The Senior Producer asked whether the signal was associated with the subject.

The production team checked. The subject was not in the building.

The Senior Producer filed this under: ambient Beleth, origin unknown, location: school, east hallway. Notation: *love/emotional detonation frequency. Both protagonists present in east hallway at time of signal. See also: first recorded Beleth signal not associated with subject directly.*

The subject was, in fact, at the west entrance of the building. He did not enter. He stood at the west entrance for eleven seconds, then left.

He had not been in the east hallway. The frequency had not needed him to be.

His expression is the expression of someone who has just confirmed something they already knew.]**

---

The Exchange Student, who had been in the library during all of this, wrote something in his notebook and closed it.

When Protagonist A came to the library after school, the Exchange Student was still there. He looked at them when they came in. Not with surprise. With the expression of someone who has been waiting for the arrival of a specific variable.

"The east hallway," he said.

Protagonist A sat down. "You weren't there."

"No."

"How did you know about the east hallway?"

The Exchange Student looked at his closed notebook. "My grandfather's account mentioned the frequency. He said you feel it before you can name it."

A pause. "What is it?"

"It's a signal that something is beginning," the Exchange Student said. "It doesn't mean what you think it means. It doesn't mean what you feel when it happens. It means something is in range that is much bigger than what you felt."

Protagonist A thought about the seven seconds. The afternoon light. The three rectangles on the linoleum.

"Is that supposed to be reassuring?" they said.

"No," the Exchange Student said. He was looking at the window. "It's supposed to be accurate."

## CHAPTER 7 — "WHAT THE CAMERA OP FILED"

---

*[This chapter is the Camera Op's perspective. It exists in the same timeline as Chapters 4–6, moving through Days 3–5 from a different vantage point. The Camera Op does not yet know she is also a protagonist.]*

---

On Day 3, the Camera Op filmed the school for six hours and reviewed three hours of usable footage.

The usable footage contained: the production crew's scheduled interviews with teachers and school office staff (polite, uninformative, the footage of people who have been told what to say), four incidental shots of students in hallways (none named, none interviewed, none remarkable), and one thirty-second sequence in which the Camera Op had been panning across the library's periodical section and caught something in the frame that she did not notice until review.

In the far left of frame, at a table near the library's east window: a student with glasses and a scarf, writing in the margin of a book. Not unusual. The unusual part was the book. The Camera Op zoomed in during review. The book was a 1978 edition of a Scottish geological survey. The student was annotating it in the margins with dense, precise handwriting.

Not a student doing homework. A student cross-referencing something.

The Camera Op filed this under: *Exchange Student, Day 3. Cross-referencing a 1978 Scottish geological survey. Note for follow-up.*

She then noticed that the Exchange Student was cross-referencing the geological survey against something in a separate notebook. She could not see the notebook's contents. But the handwriting in the margins of the geological survey was moving at the speed of someone copying, not summarizing.

What was in the notebook was what the Exchange Student had brought with him.

On Day 4, the Camera Op filmed Protagonist B in the east hallway between fourth and fifth period. Not an interview — Protagonist B wasn't aware of being filmed. The Camera Op was testing angles for a hallway sequence, had the camera up, and Protagonist B walked through frame talking to the Exchange Student.

The Camera Op reviewed this footage in the evening. She watched the Exchange Student and Protagonist B talking. She couldn't hear them — too far, wrong angle for the mic. But she watched the Exchange Student's body language and Protagonist B's body language and filed the following note:

*Exchange Student is informing. Protagonist B is receiving. Protagonist B is not alarmed by what they're receiving — their posture stays open, slightly forward, the posture of someone interested rather than worried. Exchange Student is watching Protagonist B carefully. He's assessing the reception. He wanted to know how Protagonist B would take it.*

*They took it fine. Better than fine.*

*The Exchange Student looks satisfied. Note: he has a grandfather who has been here before. He is assessing the people who are here now. He is deciding something about Protagonist B.*

She paused the footage at the moment the Exchange Student's expression shifted — the small relaxation around the eyes that happens when you've confirmed something you needed to confirm.

She noted: *Protagonist B: passes the Exchange Student's assessment. Filed. What was the assessment for?*

---

On Day 5, the Camera Op was in the mobile production unit reviewing the day's footage when the Beleth signal came in from the monitoring team.

She looked at the timestamp. She looked at her footage from the east hallway. She had not been in the east hallway at the time of the signal. She had been in the west courtyard, running a test with Camera C's wide angle.

She checked her west courtyard footage from the timestamp.

In the far right of frame, at the west entrance of the building, for eleven seconds: the man who is not quite the right age.

He was not looking at the building. He was looking at the street.

The Camera Op held the frame.

He had not been inside. He had been at the entrance for eleven seconds. The Beleth signal had come from the east wing while he was standing at the west entrance.

She looked at this for a long time. Then she wrote, in her log:

*He's not causing it anymore. He's tracking it.*

She looked at that sentence. She left it.

She wrote beneath it: *The frequency is in the school. He knew it would be. He came to confirm.*

She looked at that. She also left it.

She wrote one more thing: *Two students in the east hallway when it happened. Protagonist B (filed, source on record). And Protagonist A — I haven't filmed them yet. They said no to the production assistant.*

She pulled up the list of student refusals. Protagonist A's name. The timestamp of the refusal. The production assistant's note: *polite, clear, final.*

She added her own note: *The ones who say no. I should find the east hallway footage from before the bell.*

She found it. A security camera, not her camera, covering the east hallway from above. Two students, eight feet apart, facing each other. The afternoon light making three rectangles between them. Seven seconds. Then both walking away.

The Camera Op watched this footage twice.

She added a note: *Protagonist A. They're in the record now whether they know it or not. Not because I put them there. Because the east hallway put them there.*

She closed the file.

She sat in the mobile unit for a while.

She had been filming documentaries for nine years. She knew what it felt like when the subject of the documentary was not what the documentary thought it was. She had felt it before, on smaller scales.

She felt it now, on a larger scale than before.

She was not documenting Tyler.

She was documenting whatever Tyler was the center of.

*This is bigger than us*, she wrote in her personal notebook. Not the production log. The personal one. *The school. The frequency. The Exchange Student with the geological survey. The two kids in the east hallway. He's not the subject. He's the — architecture.*

She stopped. She crossed out "architecture."

She wrote: *He's the load-bearing part.*

She looked at this. She left it uncrossed.

She would not share this with the production team. Not yet. Possibly not ever.

She put the notebook away. She went back to the footage.
