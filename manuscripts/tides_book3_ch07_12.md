## CHAPTER 7: THE FREQUENCY

Emily Springerton was at her kitchen table — the same table from Chapter 1, the same notebook, page forty-seven still blank — at 10:08 AM on a Tuesday when she felt the Amon frequency shift.

She had been tracking the Goetia windows since Book 2, Chapter 3. Not with equipment — she didn't need equipment. The frequencies had been audible to her since she was eleven. Her mother, before she died, had told her this was a family trait. Her grandfather, before he died, had told her the same thing and added: "don't tell the archive you can hear them directly; it complicates the documentation protocols."

She had not told the archive. She had kept a separate log.

At 10:08 AM, the Amon frequency — 1.618 Hz, the reconciliation window — began building from three streets away. She felt it in the way she felt load-bearing structural things: not as sound, exactly, but as the quality of the air changing in a specific way that the air changed when something large and long-held was releasing.

She opened the notebook to page forty-seven.

She picked up her pen.

She waited.

---

At 10:49 AM, the Amon frequency resolved.

Not faded. Resolved — the distinction she had learned from her grandfather's notes in the Edinburgh file, which he had not thought anyone would ever read and had therefore been fully honest in. *A sustained frequency that fades is something that was started and ran out of source material. A frequency that resolves is something that completed its function. The difference is audible if you know what you're listening for.*

She knew what she was listening for.

She wrote on page forty-seven:

*Transfer complete. Chain closed cleanly. SPRING-4 acknowledges receipt. The custody is held.*

She dated it. She capped the pen.

She sat back and looked at what she had written and felt the specific sensation of a very long calculation arriving at its answer — not triumph, exactly. More like recognition. The answer had always been there. She had just been building the proof.

---

She called Protagonist A.

"It closed," she said.

Protagonist A was quiet for a moment.

"The sentence."

"Day 49. I can hear the frequency — Amon resolved about three minutes ago. Sequential activation. The event, not just the announcement. He said it."

"What did he say."

"That's not mine to tell. It's in the record." A pause. "What matters for us is the chain is clean. The custody transferred. The architecture map is complete." She looked at page forty-seven. "It's in the notebook."

"Callum was right," A said. "The three conditions."

"He was." Emily stood, walked to the window. "Something that knows the weight of the thing, something that can hold the weight, something that wants to. All three." She looked out at the city. "The custody chain is the same architecture. The SPRING line is the fourth wall: the people who understand what the chain is for and want it to work. My grandfather. My mother. My aunt. Me."

"You," A said.

"Me."

## CHAPTER 8: PROTAGONIST A'S DECISION

The thing Protagonist A had been building toward since Book 2 Chapter 9 — the unfinished thing between her and Callum, the incomplete resolution, the open wound — was not the same thing she thought it was in Book 2.

She understood this on the afternoon of the sentence completion.

She was sitting in the apartment, the arrows on her notes pointing at their convergence point, the convergence point now no longer pending — the sentence had closed, the chain was clean, the notebook's blank page had writing on it. She was looking at the arrows and thinking about the word *want.*

Callum had said: *Something that wants to.*

The third condition.

The third condition wasn't about the custody chain. The third condition was about doing the right thing because you want it done — not because you're obligated, not because it's your job, not because the architecture requires it. Because you genuinely want the weight transferred and the chain closed and the person carrying it to be able to put it down.

She had been approaching the unfinished thing with Callum as if it were a problem to solve. A logical structure: these are the facts, these are the conditions, this is the resolution. She had been thinking about what was correct.

She had not been thinking about what she wanted.

She picked up her phone. She called him.

"Callum," she said.

"Yeah."

"I want this to be resolved. Not because it's the correct next step. Because I want it to be resolved. Because I want—" *(pause)* "—this specific thing, with you, to exist properly. In the right form."

A long pause on his end.

"That's new," he said.

"I know."

"The old version was always 'this is the right structure.'"

"I know."

"This one is—"

"Wanting," she said. "It's wanting. Third condition."

He laughed. It was the specific laugh of someone who has been patient for a very long time and is relieved and knows that he was right to be patient and is not going to say so.

"Where are you?" he said.

"Home."

"Stay there."

He came over. It took twelve minutes. The unfinished thing finished itself in the kitchen, in the space between the table and the window, without either of them planning it — which was correct, because the third condition is the one that cannot be planned, only recognized.

The Exchange Student's field note, Volume 3 Entry 15:

*A finished the thing with Callum. I could tell from the specific quality of her silence when I texted her that afternoon — it was the finished silence, not the ongoing one. The Book 2 Chapter 9 wound is closed. The resolution was the correct kind: not tidy, not painless, but honest and wanted by both parties. The architecture required this. The architecture requiring it and both parties wanting it being the same thing: that's the third condition. That's the whole point of the third condition.*

*The Exchange Student notes for the record: the information Callum held from Book 2 Chapter 9 — the information Protagonist A had not consented to him knowing — was addressed directly. He told her. She was angry. Then she wasn't. Then she understood why he had held it and had not used it, even when using it would have been easier. He held it correctly. He never weaponized it. He waited until she was ready to hear it. That is also the third condition, actually.*

*Clean builds first. Then custody. Then everything else.*

*A said that out loud after Callum left. She didn't know where it came from. She'll find out eventually.*

## CHAPTER 10: THE TABLE

Emily Springerton had not planned to go back to Bacon's building. She went because the frequency register in her personal log showed an anomaly she wanted to investigate: a new signature at the bacon_estate coordinates that she had not seen before.

She rang the bell. Bacon answered.

"Ms. Springerton," he said. He looked at her with the expression he had worn in Chapter 5 — the expression of someone confirming something they already knew. "Of course. Come in."

She crossed the threshold.

The table was different.

Not the shape, not the material, not the chair at the end — but the chair at the end was different. The configuration was different. The count.

"How many chairs," she said.

"I haven't counted today." He said it the way he had in Chapter 5 — not evasively, just accurately. "I've been deliberately not counting."

"Why."

"Because I knew you were coming and I wanted you to count."

She counted.

Twelve.

The empty chair at the end. Eleven others. One of the eleven she hadn't seen before — set in a position that hadn't been occupied in any of the previous counts she had heard described.

"Is that mine?" she said.

"Since Wednesday," Bacon said. "The chair added itself when you signed the ledger."

She looked at the chair. It was a reasonable chair — nothing decorated, nothing ornate. The same wood as the table, which was not any known wood. The chair looked like it had been there for a long time. It had not been there on Wednesday. Both of these things were true simultaneously.

She walked to it. She looked at it.

She sat down.

The hospitality radius extended briefly — she felt it as a change in air pressure, then settling — and returned to its standard fifteen meters.

Bacon sat across from her. Not at the round table. He came and sat at the small auxiliary table he used in Chapter 5 for practical meetings.

"The architecture map," he said. "Is it what you expected?"

"It's simpler than I expected."

"It always is." He looked at the round table. "The custody chain for the Cube is simple. What makes it complicated is the length of time it's been running without a clean custodianship at the SPRING position. The years between your aunt moving to Portugal and you signing the ledger — that was a gap."

"A 12-year gap."

"Eleven and a half. The chain was technically intact but running on a single point of failure." He looked at his hands. "It was stable because of Tyler's continued presence in the temporal architecture. He was covering the gap. He had been covering gaps in the chain for four hundred years."

Emily looked at the empty chair.

"And now he doesn't have to."

"And now he doesn't have to."

She sat at the table for thirty-seven minutes. They did not discuss anything operationally significant. Bacon told her about the hardware store across from the previous Detroit address (before the city's geography shifted). She told him about the 1993 field report — CEDAR-4, her mother, the library. He already knew; he confirmed it without surprise.

When she stood to leave, she looked at her chair.

"Can I come back?" she said.

"You have an open invitation," he said. "Since Wednesday."

She left. The chair remained.

The Cataloguer, if they had seen this, would have noted it as functionally identical to Emily's grandfather's methodology: always leave a blank page for what hasn't happened yet. Always make room for the receipt.
