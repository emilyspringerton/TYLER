## CHAPTER 1 — "THE MORNING AFTER THE DETONATION"

---

Three days.

Protagonist A has been going to school because the alternative is staying home, and staying home means thinking, and thinking is currently a hostile environment in a way that feels structural — like the floor of it will hold, but only if you keep moving.

The love triangle detonated. One resolution (real, in the east hallway, confirmed). One complication (Callum knows something he wasn't supposed to know and Protagonist A doesn't know how he got it). One open wound (the thing that was almost said to B in the library, before Protagonist A saw the grandfather's handwriting in the notebook and everything rerouted).

Three days. Going to school.

---

The Edinburgh Exchange Student is at the school entrance.

She is always at the school entrance. This used to seem coincidental.

She has a file folder. She always has a file folder. The file folders contain things — field notes, clippings, photographs, annotated fragments from other annotated fragments. ## CHAPTER 2 — "PROTAGONIST B MAKES A CHOICE THEY DON'T TELL ANYONE ABOUT"

---

Protagonist B does not go to school on the third day.

They sit at their kitchen table for four hours and think. This is, for Protagonist B, unusual — they are a person of action, or they have been told they are, or they have acted like one for long enough that it amounts to the same thing. Sitting still and thinking is not their mode.

The documentary changed this.

What they know: the documentary is a binding ritual. It is the Jiangshi Syndicate's mechanism for documentation-as-capture. The Camera Op published the footage unilaterally. Tyler walked away from Stage 4.

What they believe, despite everything: a bound Tyler is a safer world. They have seen what unmoored things do to people. They watched it happen to someone they loved. A person who exists in superposition — who can be two places at once, two versions at once, who cannot commit to a single timeline — leaves marks. Not deliberately. That's almost worse. The damage is not malice. It's physics.

Protagonist B believes in Tyler. They also believe in the value of being fixed.

They make a decision at 11:47 AM.

They open the documentary production app. They start composing a message.

They stop twice. They finish on the third attempt.

The message is three words: *I know where.*

They don't finish the sentence. They don't fully realize they've done this — that they have done the thing Tyler does, the un-definition, the withheld completion. They learned it from watching the footage, which they've watched eleven times.

They send the message.

The production app shows it as delivered.

## CHAPTER 3 — "BACON HAS BEEN EXPECTING THIS"

---

"It's more honest than using your name. You are operating as a protagonist. The designation is accurate." She adjusts the file folder. "Also, I didn't want the report to get personal."

The bus arrives.

---

Dr. Aldous Vane. Culinary historian. Visiting lecturer. Department of food anthropology. The kind of academic who is simultaneously overqualified and inexplicably employed in a position that requires constant relocation and never checks institutional memory too carefully.

He is expecting them.

He opens the door before they reach it. This is not, Protagonist A realizes, a trick or precognition. He simply has very good windows and watches them from the moment they turn onto the street. He has been expecting someone. He has been expecting someone for a long time. When the someone finally arrives it is not surprising. It is — relief.

The table is set for three.

Protagonist A says: "How did you know we were coming?"

Bacon says: "I didn't know it was you specifically. I knew it was time."

---

There is dinner. Protagonist A will later have difficulty describing the dinner to people who weren't there — not because anything unusual happened, but because the food was too precisely right. The right number of dishes. The right temperature for the season. The particular hospitality of someone who has been feeding people for a very long time and understands, at a structural level, what it means to make someone feel their presence is expected rather than merely permitted.

The Exchange Student eats three helpings. She does not appear to notice that with each one — with each serving of the cold starter, the warm main, the thing that was technically dessert but tasted like a memory of something — she has been given a piece of information. The information is delivered in the way all good information is delivered: embedded in something pleasurable, accessible only in retrospect.

She takes notes. She does not know what she is taking notes about yet.

"Because 'not using it' and 'not delivering it' require different kinds of vigilance. Not using it is easy. The Cube does not want to be used. It is an instrument, not a drive. It waits. "Not yet."

## CHAPTER 4 — "THE SHELL PARLIAMENT SENDS AN INVOICE"

---

The invoice arrives the next morning in the form of a text from a number Kei doesn't recognize, directing them to an email address that contains a secure document portal.

This is unusual. The Shell Parliament usually routes invoices through the established Crustocrat family channels. Routing through Kei's personal devices means one of two things: either the relevant social graph is thin enough that Kei is the only suitable node, or the Parliament is choosing to make this personal.

The invoice is for 340 shell-marks. Line item: *Unauthorized access to contested temporal asset, compounded at standard rate, 1623 CE to present.*

The asset: the Chrono-Cube.
The primary debtor: TYLER (unresolved; collection ongoing; see file SM-1620-T).
The co-debtor, added via recent amendment: *PROTAGONIST A (pending formal confirmation of awareness and intent).*

Kei reads this three times.

340 shell-marks is not an impossible debt. It is the kind of debt that takes two years to clear, methodically, if the holder has steady income and no unexpected claims. Kei's debt was down to 62 shell-marks. It had taken four years to get there.

The collection case has a cross-reference tag: *TEMPORAL_ASSET_001 — co-debtor inducted on basis of voluntary access to contested asset records, awareness confirmed, intent pending.* There is a footnote: *Note: awareness-without-action does not constitute full induction. Collection protocol: advisory contact first, formal demand only after intent established.*

Advisory contact. Kei has been assigned to make advisory contact.

With a person at their own school. In their own year. A person Kei has passed in the hallway seventeen times without speaking.

Kei closes the portal. They look at the ceiling. They have a very old argument with the Shell Parliament in their head — the argument about whether the Parliament's debt architecture is justice or infrastructure or both — and then they close the argument and do what they always do: plan the approach.

## CHAPTER 5 — "PROTAGONIST C FINISHES A SENTENCE AND IT'S THE WRONG ONE"

---

The octopus has been settling.

This is the clinical description of what happens when a partial Cephalopod infestation reaches equilibrium — the host's neural architecture and the cephalopod substrate reach a stable interface. Not merged. Interfaced. Two systems running concurrently, occasionally in communication, mostly respecting each other's operational boundaries.

Protagonist C has been finishing sentences correctly for three months. Not their sentences — other people's. The partial infestation means they sometimes perceive a sentence as a completed object before the person speaking it reaches the end. They finish it. Usually correctly. The person being finished looks slightly unnerved and Protagonist C says "sorry, I do that" and the moment passes.

In Chapter 5, they finish the wrong sentence.

It happens in the middle of a conversation about the school lunch menu. Protagonist C is talking to a friend. The friend says something about whether the Thursday option is better than the Wednesday option. Protagonist C opens their mouth to agree.

What comes out is: "—the Cube was never the point. The fixed coordinates are the point. The Cube is just the key."

Four seconds of silence.

The friend says: "What?"

Protagonist C says: "I — sorry. I don't know where that came from."

They do not know where it came from. Their octopus does. The octopus encountered a signal fragment at the edge of its substrate access range — a fragment of a dead-drop message that the Subscriber routed through channels that overlap, at the margins, with Cephalopod Collective infrastructure. The fragment lodged. Protagonist C's sentence-completion reflex fired.

## CHAPTER 6 — "THE SPINNER ARRIVES"

---

The coffee shop Protagonist A has not been to before. In a part of the city they had no particular reason to visit. Finding it required a chain of decisions — missing the regular bus, choosing a different route, walking an extra four blocks — that felt, in retrospect, less like decisions and more like the path of least resistance.

The Spinner is already there when Protagonist A walks in.

She is 16 or 17, with the particular look of someone who has processed too much simultaneous information for too long and arrived at a state that resembles calm but is actually a very practiced equilibrium. She is sitting at a corner table. She has two cups of tea. One of them is for Protagonist A, who she ordered for fifteen minutes ago.

She says, before Protagonist A sits down: "You are going to need what I know. I will have told you already. This is that conversation."

Protagonist A sits down. They say: "That's not how sentences work."

"I know." She sounds genuinely apologetic. "It's very annoying. The web sees all timelines at once. I just translate. Some of the translation is bad. The tense markers are the worst part."

---

She explains the Kuroshio Arachnids in the way someone explains a thing they did not choose to know — matter-of-factly, with the mild exhaustion of having understood something that makes the world more complicated.

The web is not a metaphor. It is a substrate-level structure — older than the factions, older than the archive, older than most things that describe themselves as old. The Kuroshio Arachnids are partial hosts to it. They can see the web's current state: every timeline, every contested point, every place where the branches of possibility overlap or diverge. They cannot change the web. They can only see it.

What the web shows: the Chrono-Cube's operation creates permanent nodes. Every time the Cube pins a temporal coordinate, it creates a fixed point in the web — a stable anchor, immovable, impervious to the frequency counter-magic that Tyler (and others) use to disrupt web growth. The Arachnids have been waiting for the Cube to be used because fixed nodes are resources. Enough nodes and the web becomes impossible to disrupt.

"Tyler's hum," Protagonist A says. "The 7.83 Hz thing."

"Counter-magic against the web. It has been effective for four centuries because no permanent nodes exist in his operational range. If the Cube is used — if even one fixed node is placed — the hum's effectiveness in that region drops. Permanently."

Protagonist A is quiet.

"Who's about to use it?"

The Spinner drinks her tea. She says: "The Subscriber is about to use it. Will have been about to use it. I'm sorry, the tense problem again." She sets down the cup. "The Subscriber has been patient for 380 years because they needed the custody chain to be clean before they could operate. The custody chain became clean recently. They are not being patient anymore."

## CHAPTER 8 — "THE INVOICE IS DELIVERED"

Kei waits for the right moment.

The Shell Parliament's collection protocol for advisory contact is specific: approach in a context of low social pressure, non-confrontational positioning, documented as professional rather than personal, no threats of formal collection action in the first contact. Protagonist A is not aware of the Shell Parliament's existence.

Kei recalibrates.

---

Protagonist A looks up. They have not spoken before. They recognize each other the way you recognize someone you've passed in the hallway seventeen times — with the vague awareness of having seen this face without having assigned a name to it.

## CHAPTER 9 — "TYLER VISITS BACON (WE DON'T SEE THIS DIRECTLY)"

---

Protagonist A goes back to Bacon's apartment three days later.

They go alone this time — the Exchange Student has a field report to file and has agreed to sit this one out, though she has left Protagonist A with three annotated index cards covering what she thinks are the most likely outcomes of the visit. The annotations are uncomfortably accurate.

Bacon says: "This morning."

"Tyler."

Tea made. Sitting down.

Protagonist A looks at the table.

"They understood this," Bacon says. "They gave me the Cube so the decision couldn't be made in a moment of — wanting to fix things. So the wanting would have to go somewhere else."

## CHAPTER 12 — "THE SPINNER EXPLAINS THE WEB (AND IT'S WORSE THAN EXPECTED)"

---

The Spinner does not wait to be invited.

She arrives at the coffee shop where Protagonist A, Kei, and the Edinburgh Exchange Student are having their first formal meeting — the one Protagonist A called to share everything from the previous two weeks: Bacon's testimony, the invoice, the dead-drop information from the tanuki. The Spinner sits down at the table before they have finished ordering and says: "You are going to need what I know. I'll make the tenses as manageable as possible. Kei — you'll want to take notes. Your linear reconstruction will be the clearest record of this."

Kei takes out a notebook. They have not previously committed to note-taking as a role. They find, looking at the Spinner, that they are going to do it anyway.

---

The web: a substrate-level structure that predates faction architecture. The Arachnids: partial hosts who can perceive the web's state without being consumed by it (mostly). The Spinner: a partial host who can translate the web's non-linear perception into language, with acknowledged accuracy limitations.

The web's relationship to the Chrono-Cube: every time the Cube pins a temporal coordinate, it creates a permanent node in the web. The node is fixed. Immovable. Impervious to frequency disruption — specifically to Tyler's 7.83 Hz counter-magic hum.

"Tyler has been using the hum for four centuries," the Spinner says. "It works because there are no fixed nodes in his operational range. The web can't anchor if there's nothing to anchor to. If even one fixed node is placed — if the Cube is used even once — the hum's effectiveness in that region drops. Permanently and irreversibly."

Kei writes. *Web nodes = Cube anchors. Tyler's hum = counter-web. One Cube use = hum degraded.*

"And the Subscriber wants the Cube used."

"The Subscriber wants it used comprehensively. Not once — across every contested migration point in the timeline simultaneously. Every Mandela Effect seam. Every branch-bleeding anomaly. Every place where two branches overlap and the residents of each branch remember it differently."

The Exchange Student looks up from her notes. "All of them at once."

"Yes."

"What does that do?"

The Spinner's shadow shifts. "Every contested memory resolves. One branch becomes canonical. Every person in every other branch whose memory of the event differs — their memory is reclassified as false. Permanently. At the substrate level. Emily OS would have to execute the reclassification."

## CHAPTER 13 — "THE CAMERA OP FINDS THE LIST"

---

The list has eleven items.

It has been a list since item three, when the Camera Op realized they were making one — that the things they were not reporting to the Producer had a pattern, and a pattern was a document, and a document was something they could review.

Item 1: *The first time the documentary felt like something other than a documentary. (Pilot. The coin flip. Two seconds of eye contact with a lens. I felt like a participant.)*

Item 2: *The moment I realized I was keeping a list.*

Item 3: *Tyler at the production office. The hallway. The three fragments. "I want to tell you something about why I—" He said it to the camera, not to the room. He was saying it to whoever would eventually see the footage.*

Item 4: *The four frames. Library entrance, school footage. Tyler present. Looking at something the camera can't see. Was there before we arrived. Left something.*

Item 5: *The end logs. The first one that said BIRD CORRECTION PENDING. I read it four times before I reported it as an equipment glitch. I don't know why I called it that. It isn't a glitch.*

Item 6: *The way the frequency readings change when Tyler is in the room. Not interference. Something is running at 7.83 Hz and the equipment picks it up as a carrier signal. The production tech thinks it's local electrical noise. It isn't.*

Item 7: *The night the production equipment ran a frequency activation without being programmed to. 2.22 Hz for eleven seconds. Not our equipment. Not Tyler. Something external.*

Item 8: *The footnote in the archive the Exchange Student left in my equipment bag. "Footnote 47b: This observer is lying." I read the whole archive.*

Item 9: *The end logs aren't the crew's output. They're annotations on the crew's output. Someone is reading what we produce and responding to it. The responses are always accurate. The responses know things the documentary should not know yet.*

Item 10: *I know what "the substrate" is now. I learned it from reading the end logs backward. I did not know this word three weeks ago. I know it now the way I know something I learned a long time ago and forgot and then found again.*

Item 11: *I think the four frames at the library entrance are a message. I think Tyler put himself in those four frames deliberately. I think he's been leaving messages in footage since the pilot and I've been carrying them without reading them.*

---

The Camera Op reads the list back to themselves.

They read it a second time.

They see what it is.

It is not a list of omissions. It is a map. It is a map of all the points where the documentary stopped being a documentary and started being something the Camera Op is the protagonist of, and each item on the list is a waypoint on the route from "camera operator" to "whatever they are now."

They sit with this for twenty minutes.

Then they pick up their phone.

They call a number they got from the Edinburgh Exchange Student's field notes — the number for the person who has been in the same story as them from a different angle.

When Protagonist A picks up, the Camera Op says: "I think we're in the same story."

Protagonist A says: "I know. I've been waiting for you to call."

⟳ SYNC: *The call. Both series converge here. The Camera Op is the bridge — between the documentary and the teenager, between the footage and the people in it, between what the Jiangshi Syndicate thinks is happening and what is actually happening. The Camera Op has been the bridge since the pilot. They are only now aware that bridges work in both directions.*
