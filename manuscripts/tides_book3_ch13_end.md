## Chapter 13: The New Blank Page

Emily's notebook has forty-seven complete pages and then a blank one.

She sits with this for three days after the transfer. Not paralyzed — working. EINHORN_INDUSTRIAL has projects, clients, dependencies that don't pause because the custody chain completed. She attends meetings. She answers messages. She writes estimates and proposals and dependency maps for clients whose supply chains have nothing to do with 400-year custody architectures.

But she keeps returning to the notebook.

On the fourth day, she opens it to page 48. She writes:

*What did the chain require that I now no longer have to do?*

She makes a list. It is long. It contains things she didn't know were chain-requirements until she wrote them down: certain kinds of caution, certain kinds of keeping-distance-from-things-she-wanted, certain kinds of holding-available. The list is mostly things she *didn't* do rather than things she did.

She looks at the list.

Then she writes the second question:

*What do I want to build that I've been keeping available space for?*

She writes for two hours. The second list is also long. It doesn't overlap with the first at all. The first list is things the chain required her to not-do. The second list is things she wanted to do that she kept adjacent-to-available, in reserve, waiting for the moment when the chain was done and the weight came off and the space opened.

The space is open.

She looks at the second list for a long time. Then she circles three items. Then she circles them again, harder, in a different pen.

She closes the notebook. She opens her laptop. She begins.

Page 48 has one line on it: the second question. The rest is still blank. She doesn't need to fill the page. The page is the container for the work. The work is happening in the room where the work happens.

The Cataloguer note for this chapter: *Emily Springerton fills pages 49 through 94 in the following eight months. The pages document the first construction of the Series 2 period. Page 48 remains the question. The pages after it are the answer. The question is load-bearing.*

## Chapter 15: The Exchange Student's Archive

The CEDAR-4 file is 47 pages.

The Exchange Student reads it in the school library over three lunch periods. She does not rush. The file is careful — CEDAR-4 was careful, the way people who keep records are careful when they know the records matter more than they can fully explain at the time of keeping.

What the file contains:

A record of supply orders for a stationery company in Edinburgh, 1981–1993. The orders are for specific types of archival paper — acid-free, water-resistant, designed to last. The ordering entity: a logistics coordinator named Margaret Springerton. The delivery address: a residential address in Edinburgh that the Exchange Student looks up and finds is now a regular house with no archival significance visible from the outside.

Margaret Springerton. The Exchange Student writes the name in her own notebook. She has read the Tides Book 3 manuscript up to the point where Emily discovers she is SPRING-4. She knows who SPRING-2 was. She knows SPRING-2 was Emily's mother.

Margaret Springerton ordered archival paper for twelve years. The paper was the kind used for keeping records that need to survive. CEDAR-4 tracked the orders because CEDAR-4 was tracking what the SPRING-line witnesses needed to do their work. The paper was what SPRING-2 used to keep the records she kept for the chain.

The Exchange Student writes in her notebook: *She was keeping something. For a long time. Without knowing all of what she was keeping.*

Then she writes: *Same as me.*

She sits with that for the third lunch period, not reading anymore. Just sitting with the fact that CEDAR-4 tracked Margaret Springerton for twelve years, and that CEDAR-4's file ended up in a folder in a stationery orders directory that the Exchange Student found because she was looking for something else, and that the something else she was looking for is connected to what CEDAR-4 was tracking.

She doesn't know what to do with this yet. She knows she is not done with it.

She closes the file and goes to her afternoon classes. The file goes in her bag. She does not leave it in the library.

## Chapter 17: What Margaret Kept

The Exchange Student finds Emily Springerton's office through EINHORN_INDUSTRIAL's website.

She sends a message. She is not sure what to say, exactly, so she says what is true: she found a file called CEDAR-4 in a stationery orders directory at school, and the file is about a person named Margaret Springerton, and she thinks Margaret Springerton might be related to Emily, and she doesn't know what to do with the file but she knows she shouldn't leave it in the school library.

Emily responds within two hours.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON (via message):**
Margaret Springerton is my mother. She passed away in 2019. The file you found is real and it matters. Can you bring it to me?

The Exchange Student brings it on a Saturday. Emily Springerton's office is in a building that looks exactly like a normal logistics consultancy, which is what it also is. The Exchange Student sits across from Emily at a desk with three monitors and a very organized set of notebooks stacked in a specific order.

Emily reads the file. She reads it carefully, the way she reads everything — as if the dependency structure is present in the text and needs to be found.

She gets to the end. She closes it.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
My mother ordered archival paper for twelve years. I thought she was just organized.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**

Emily opens a cabinet. She produces a box — not large, document-sized. Inside: notebooks. Specifically the kind of notebooks that would be ordered from a stationery company that specializes in archival-quality materials. Forty-seven of them.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
I inherited these when she died. I read them all. I thought they were just journals. Very well-organized journals from a very organized person. Then I found out about the SPRING line.

She puts one of the notebooks on the desk. She opens it. The paper inside is dense with notes — clearly organized, cross-referenced, the work of someone who knew what records were for.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
My mother was SPRING-2. She spent twelve years keeping records that I spent three years thinking were just journals. CEDAR-4 was tracking what she needed to do the work. You found the tracking record. I have the work.

The Exchange Student looks at the notebooks.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
What are they records of?

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
*(simply)*
What it looked like, from the outside, in real time, to be part of a custody chain that hadn't completed yet. What the architecture looked like before anyone knew what the architecture was. My mother didn't know she was SPRING-2. She just kept very careful records about a set of concerns she had that she couldn't fully explain.

A pause.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
She was doing the work without knowing what the work was for.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
Same as me. Until I didn't have to anymore.

The Exchange Student sits with this.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
CEDAR-4 tracked what she needed. Who was CEDAR-4?

Emily looks at her for a moment. The question is the right question. The Exchange Student doesn't know it's the right question yet. That's why it's the right question.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
Someone who understood the architecture well enough to track what the witnesses needed without being a witness themselves. Someone downstream of the chain but not in it. Someone who made sure the witnesses had what they needed to do the work.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
*(slowly)*
And CEDAR-4 is gone now. The file ends in 1993.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
The file ends when CEDAR-4's function was complete. My mother stopped needing to be tracked in 1993 — the chain moved to the next node, and CEDAR-4's role in supporting SPRING-2 was finished. The function closed.

The Exchange Student looks at the box of forty-seven notebooks.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
*(carefully)*
The transfer completed last year. The chain is closed. SPRING-4 is active. The architecture is in its use phase now. The use phase needs — *(she pauses)* — it needs what the build phase needed. People downstream of the chain who make sure the builders have what they need.

She looks at the Exchange Student.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
CEDAR-4's function closed in 1993. It hasn't been open since. The architecture has been waiting for the right person to reopen it.

Long silence.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
You're not asking me.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
*(simply)*
No. I'm not. The architecture doesn't ask. It finds. I'm telling you what I know about what CEDAR was. What you do with that is yours.

The Exchange Student picks up the CEDAR-4 file. She reads the last page again — Margaret Springerton's last order, 1993, forty-seven notebooks, acid-free, water-resistant, designed to last.

She thinks about the Exchange Student function she has been performing without knowing it was a function: finding things in archive folders that were waiting to be found, connecting records that didn't know they were connected, making sure the right information reached the right person at the right time.

She thinks about the CEDAR-4 file in her bag. The fact that she didn't leave it in the school library.

She puts it back in her bag.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:**
I'll think about it.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON:**
*(a nod — this is also the correct answer)*
Take your time.

An organization that does deliberately what EINHORN_INDUSTRIAL currently does intuitively. Not one person tracking one family of witnesses. A function. A methodology.
