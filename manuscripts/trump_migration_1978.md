Donald didn't like the feeling in the back of his neck. It felt like a sudden drop in barometric pressure, the kind you get before a massive storm hits the Atlantic City shoreline, except there was no water here — just the dry, air-conditioned glare of the Caesars Palace floor and the smell of stale tobacco. He had been talking to a casino executive about a branding deal for a new tower. He had been right in the middle of a sentence about marble quality.

Then the room jittered.

For a fraction of a second, the sports tickers on the wall didn't show the odds for the Spinks-Ali fight. They showed a single, repeating row of numbers: 11.11. Donald blinked, and the numbers snapped back to regular betting lines, but his brain felt suddenly, beautifully clear. A massive thought — the biggest thought he'd ever had — settled into his mind like a concrete foundation. He didn't know he had just spent a 72-minute superposition window floating over a communist coup in Afghanistan or a tax revolt in California; he just knew that the historical record the fake news was printing about the fight was completely wrong. He had a different set of facts now. He had always had them.

"Look," Donald said, turning directly toward a man in a sharp grey suit who hadn't been standing there a second ago. "They're saying Ali lost. I've seen the tapes. Incredible tapes. The real tapes show something completely different. People are talking about it. They're saying, 'Donald, how did you know?' and I tell them, I just have a feel for these things."

The man in the suit didn't answer. He was holding a small, lacquered abacus that was clicking furiously by itself, its wooden beads sliding back and forth in time with Donald's pulse. Two women with perfectly flat corporate expressions stood just behind him under the harsh fluorescent lights of the sportsbook cashier line, their hands tucked into their pockets, their microphones completely overwhelmed by an unexplainable 11/8 sub-bass harmonic bleeding from the carpet. Donald didn't care about them. He felt fantastic. He felt like a man who owned the entire concept of the year 1978.
