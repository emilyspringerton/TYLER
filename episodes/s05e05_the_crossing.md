## COLD OPEN

The Detroit River, 9 AM. The Ambassador Bridge overhead.

The Camera Op is filming the water. Not the bridge — the water, and the specific point where the crossing has always been: the narrows where the current slows, where for three hundred years (longer, per the archive) people have moved from one shore to the other.

She has been here for forty minutes. Tyler arrived twenty minutes after her and has been standing at the water's edge without speaking.

The bridge traffic passes continuously overhead. Container ships move through at intervals. The river itself is indifferent to all of it.

**CAMERA OP:** *(still filming)* "It doesn't stop."

**CAMERA OP:** "The event here isn't something that happened. It's something that keeps happening."

## EPISODE STRUCTURE

**Day 145, morning — what a threshold holds**

Emily arrives at 10 AM with the network map and a second notebook — she started a separate one for the crossing study three days ago.

**EMILY:** "I've been thinking about what you said. The crossing is a threshold between two places, not a container. Everything else in the photograph set is a room — a bounded space that held a specific event. This is different."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EMILY:** "So the question is whether a Witness Room can be built for something that's still happening. Every construction so far has been for a completed event — Day 49, 1918. The crossing is ongoing."

**TYLER:** "That's the question I've been sitting with."

He looks at the water.

**TYLER:** "Rooms hold what happened. Thresholds hold what keeps happening. They're not the same thing. A Witness Room for a threshold wouldn't make the crossing accessible — the crossing is already accessible, people cross it every day. What it would do is make the fact of the continuous crossing legible. The weight of all the crossings. The accumulated history of everyone who has moved between these two places."

**EMILY:** "That's a different kind of construction."

**TYLER:** "Yes. I've been thinking about whether the same method works. The frequencies, the witnesses, the container."

**EMILY:** "Does it?"

**TYLER:** "The frequencies work anywhere. Stolas is present here — has been for a long time. The container is the threshold itself: the narrows, the specific geography of this crossing point. What I'm not sure about is the witness requirement." He looks at the bridge traffic overhead. "A room can be witnessed by four people standing in it. A threshold is crossed by thousands of people every day. The witness function might work differently when the container is something people are continuously moving through."

---

**Day 145, late morning — the Exchange Student's crossing map**

The Exchange Student has her laptop on a portable table at the water's edge. Her preliminary dependency map for the crossing is open.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "I've been mapping this for two weeks. Three structural differences from the room constructions." She points to her screen. "First: the container is geographic, not architectural. The crossing point is defined by the river's shape and the current pattern, not by walls. Second: the primary events are distributed across the entire threshold — the Stolas frequency isn't centered in one room, it's present at the crossing point as a whole. Third—" she pauses. "Third is the one I can't resolve. The crossing has two sides. Detroit and Windsor. Two countries. The threshold is the space between them, which is technically international waters."

**TYLER:** "The construction would be in the threshold itself. Not on either bank."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Right. Which means the resonant witnesses — for a room, they stand in the room. For this, do they stand at the threshold? Cross it? Maintain the frequency from both sides simultaneously?"

**TYLER:** "That's the part I'm working out."

**EMILY:** *(writing)* "Cross-border Witness Room. First international construction."

**TYLER:** "It's not certain yet."

**EMILY:** "I know. I'm writing it anyway."

**Day 145, late afternoon — Tyler's decision on the crossing**

At 4 PM Tyler stands at the water's edge again.

**TYLER:** "I know what to do."

Emily opens the second notebook.

**TYLER:** "The crossing requires a different witness structure. Not people standing still — people crossing. The witnesses for a threshold Witness Room are everyone who crosses with awareness of what they're crossing through. Not four specific people. Thousands of people, most of them not knowing."

**EMILY:** "Passive witnesses."

**TYLER:** "Present witnesses. They don't need to know the construction is there. They need to cross the threshold. The crossing itself is the witnessing act."

**EMILY:** "Can that work?"

**TYLER:** "Bacon's building worked with one person maintaining it informally. The Subscriber's notation is what calibrates it. For the crossing, the calibration is the accumulated weight of continuous movement. The notation anchors it. The crossings maintain it."

He looks at the bridge traffic overhead. At the river. At Windsor across the water.

**TYLER:** "I'll start Day 150."

Emily writes: *Construction #3: Ambassador Bridge crossing. Starts Day 150. Witness structure: continuous passive — all crossings contribute. Notation: Tyler will place Subscriber notation equivalent. Duration: unknown.*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(already updating her map)* "I'll need to rethink the dependency section. If the witnesses are passive crossers, the supply chain is the bridge traffic." She pauses. "That's the most robust supply chain I've ever mapped. It's essentially infinite."
