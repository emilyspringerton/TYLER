*The Cataloguer notes: the network is larger than the archive knew. The archive did not know how much was in the libraries because nobody read the libraries for this purpose. Valentina Alcântara read the libraries for this purpose for fifteen years. The archive is in her debt in a way that is structural, not financial — the Heikegani have handled the financial.*

*The Cataloguer also notes: in reviewing the Córdoba extension records (850–950 CE), one of Maryam bint Khalaf's references mentions Tyler in a context that is not purely professional.*

*We are not editorializing. We are noting that Maryam bint Khalaf, in a personal letter preserved in the same collection as her music catalogues (discovered by Valentina, not previously referenced), wrote:*

*'The man at the eastern gate has been here longer than I can remember. My daughter asks why he doesn't leave. I told her: some people are present the way a tone is present — not because they choose to be, but because the air at this place holds them.'*

*Her daughter's name, per family records Valentina researched: Safiya bint Ahmad al-Khalaf.*

*The Córdoba archive records Safiya bint Ahmad al-Khalaf as one of the library's instrument calibrators from approximately 970 CE to 995 CE.*

*A separate note in Safiya's hand, in a different document from a later date, reads: 'He gave me his name at last. He said it was not a name anyone had given him, but one he had chosen. I have written it only in this document. I will not write it elsewhere. A name given once, in private, to one person, in this library, in this year — that is not a name for the public record. That is a name for the person who received it.'*

*The name is not in the document. She did not write it.*

*The Cataloguer is noting this because: Tyler gave his name to one person in Córdoba in approximately 975 CE. This is the first recorded instance of Tyler giving his name voluntarily to another person in the archive. It predates the archive by 152 years.*

*We do not know what name he gave.*

*We know he gave it once, to Safiya bint Ahmad al-Khalaf, in the Library of Córdoba, around 975 CE.*

*We also know that when the Eastwind Owls first approached Tyler in the modern era and asked his name, he declined.*

*Eastwind archives do not speculate. We note what is in the record. The record says: he gave his name once, to one person, in Córdoba, in 975 CE, and did not give it again for at least fifty years afterward, and possibly never again to another person.*

*The name he gave, at 28.7 Hz, in the library that had been calibrating its instruments to his presence for over a hundred years, is in no archive.*

*The name lives, privately, in a document that Safiya chose to seal.*

*We have the seal. We have not broken it.*"*

## ACT ONE

**INT. FEZ RIAD — MORNING**

*Valentina has the Safiya letter.*

*She puts it on the table carefully. Camera Op and Dr. Hassan look at it. Jiangshi stands behind them.*

**VALENTINA**
I found this two years ago. After I found Maryam's frequency notation. The family archive — the al-Khalaf family papers — were acquired by the Fundação Biblioteca Nacional in 1987. My mother catalogued them. She flagged this letter as significant without knowing why. The flag was in her notes.

*(pause)*

I read my mother's notes when she died, six years ago. I reopened the al-Khalaf collection six months later.

**CAMERA OP**
*(looking at the letter)*
"He gave me his name at last." She knew him.

**VALENTINA**
Safiya bint Ahmad al-Khalaf was the library's instrument calibrator from 970 to 995 CE. She spent twenty-five years working in the same building where Tyler had been for at least a hundred and twenty years. She knew him the way you know a building's frequency — not as a discovery, as a given.

*(pause)*

She's the one who asked his name. Not as a journalist. She'd known him for twenty-five years. She asked his name the way you eventually ask the name of someone you've known so long you forgot you didn't know it.

**DR. HASSAN**
And he told her.

**VALENTINA**
And she wrote it once, privately, and sealed it.

*(a pause)*

I haven't read it. The seal is Safiya's. The seal is not mine to break.

*(she looks at Camera Op)*

I brought it here because I thought the archive should know the letter exists. The archive should decide what to do with the seal.

*(Camera Op looks at the letter for a long time.)*

**CAMERA OP**
We leave the seal.

**VALENTINA**
*(nodding)*

*Jiangshi looks at the letter for a moment, then away. She does not argue.*

**INT. FEZ RIAD — MID-MORNING**

*The five-timeline structure is being assembled on the courtyard table. Maps, chronologies, Valentina's site folders.*

**DR. HASSAN**
If we accept Valentina's nine confirmed sites and the Alcântara archive's extension of the existing network, we have five distinct interpersonal periods where Tyler's relationship to another person in a specific place is documented.

**CAMERA OP**
Five timelines.

**DR. HASSAN**
Five locations where Tyler was known as a person, not just a frequency signature. Where someone wrote about him in the first person.

*(he lays out the maps)*

First: Córdoba, 850–985 CE. Tyler at the Library. Maryam bint Khalaf documents him as ambient presence for forty years. Safiya bint Ahmad al-Khalaf knows him personally for twenty-five years, receives his name in private.

*(he marks it)*

Second: Toledo, 1040–1088 CE. Tyler at the Tagus overlook. T-001. The extended Alcântara records include a single document from a Toledan scribe, approximately 1055 CE, who mentions "the stranger at the overlook who has been here since before the campaigns." Nothing personal — Tyler as presence, not person.

Third: Marrakech, December 1119 CE. The archive's origin. Ahmad ibn Yusuf. Tyler sat at the wall and wrote the first word. Ahmad watched and did not speak. The most documented Tyler relationship in the archive, and the least personal — Tyler never said his name. Ahmad named the archive for the conversation on the ship.

Fourth: The ship, 1119–1120 CE. Ahmad and Tyler, four days crossing. Tyler answered three askings. The closest thing to a direct conversation in the pre-archive record.

Fifth: The present. The documentary. Morocco. The Camera Op.

*(he looks at the group)*

Five timelines where Tyler was in a sustained relationship with another person. Five timelines where the 28.7 Hz frequency is documented. Five timelines that the Alcântara archive has been tracking.

**VALENTINA**
And in three of the five, the relationship is complicated by something that is not purely professional or archival.

*(a pause)*

Safiya was twenty-five years in that library. Tyler was there for at least a hundred and twenty. They knew each other for a quarter century. I've read everything she wrote. She knew him.

**CAMERA OP**
*(carefully)*
Knew him.

**VALENTINA**
*(meeting her eyes)*
She asked his name. He gave it. She sealed it. That's not a professional relationship.

*(pause)*

And the ship. Four days crossing the Mediterranean. Tyler waiting for Ahmad to ask the question three times. Ahmad asking. Tyler answering the third time with the word that became the archive's first word. That's not a professional relationship either.

**DR. HASSAN**
*(quietly)*
And the present.

*(a long pause)*

**CAMERA OP**
I'm not — we're not — I'm a documentary filmmaker.

**VALENTINA**
*(not arguing, not pushing)*
Yes.

*(pause)*

So was Ahmad, in his way. He was a geographer. He documented Tyler's presence in the world. He spent his life writing about what Tyler left behind. He never said Tyler's name in the archive — Tyler never gave it to him. He called him "the man at the wall" in his manuscripts. He named the cave "al-Waqfa" — the pause, the standing. He used every indirect method available to document someone he was not going to name.

*(she looks at Camera Op)*

Ahmad wrote around Tyler for thirty years and never wrote his name. I don't think that's because Ahmad was maintaining archival distance.

*(pause)*

I think it's because some things you document around the edges because if you named them directly you'd have to decide what they were.

*(a very long beat)*

**CAMERA OP**
*(very quietly)*
Entry 91 starts here.

Like Safiya's letter.
