## EPISODE STRUCTURE

**Day 220, 7 AM — the photograph**

He has not yet said who he is telling. The camera is filming. He continues as if the camera is the correct recipient, which it is.

**TYLER:** "This photograph is from June 1952. I was doing the survey. Eleven sites, 340 photographs, six months." He sets the photograph down. "But I'd already been to this corner. Nine years earlier. In 1943."

Camera Op: *He knows I'm here.*

**TYLER:** "The archive doesn't have a 1943 Detroit entry for me. The archive wasn't established in Detroit by 1943 — CARDINAL-3 was operating out of the Detroit station from 1947 to 1962. So there's no contemporaneous documentation." He looks at the photograph. "The 1952 photograph is in the archive as a survey photograph. A candidate site. It's been filed as potential future construction." He sets the coin on the table, not in his pocket. "It wasn't a candidate site in 1952. I already knew what was there. I went back."

She writes: *He went back. 1943 to 1952 to 2026.*

**TYLER:** "I should tell you what happened at that corner in June 1943."

---

**Day 220, 7:15 AM — what happened at Vernor and Hastings**

Tyler speaks for twenty-two minutes. The camera runs. This is the longest continuous address to the camera in the series record.

He describes a Sunday. Early June 1943, before the riot — the riot was June 20. The corner at Vernor and Hastings had been the location of an informal Sunday music gathering for approximately two years. He does not know who started it. He does not know exactly when it began. He knows it happened because he heard it from a block away on his way somewhere else and stopped.

**TYLER:** "I don't play anything. I never have. I stood at the edge of the group for three hours. There were maybe forty people. Some playing, some listening. Children at the periphery. Neighborhood — people who lived within six blocks who had come outside because the weather was right and the music was good."

He pauses.

**TYLER:** "The music stopped at approximately four in the afternoon. People began to leave. Nobody said anything about it being the last time. Nobody knew." He looks at the window. "I didn't know until the following Sunday, when I walked past the corner and there was nothing. And the Sunday after. I counted twelve Sundays before I accepted it wasn't coming back."

**CAMERA OP:** *(off screen, quietly)* "What happened to the people?"

**TYLER:** "The neighborhood changed. The riot happened two weeks later — June 20th, Belle Isle, spread from there. Hateful nights. The neighborhood didn't go back to what it was before. The people who played at the corner on Sundays — some moved, some didn't come back outside in the same way, some were afraid. The gathering was never formally organized, which means it was never formally disbanded. It just stopped."

He puts the coin back in his right pocket.

**TYLER:** "Something ended and nobody recorded what. The James Elwood interview — the oral history from 1978 — that's the only documentation that the practice existed. He was twelve years old in 1943. He called it 'the room where we practiced.' He meant the corner. The corner was the room."

**Day 220, 9 AM — at the corner**

Construction #4 is already at this geography. Tyler started a deliberate construction here in Season 5, working through the I-375 concrete, oriented to the buried street geography. The deliberate construction is at Day 36.

Tyler walks to the center of the buried Vernor/Hastings intersection — the geographic position that is now beneath the I-375 infrastructure, accessible only through the approximate above-ground alignment.

The Camera Op positions herself at what would have been the Vernor approach — the direction people would have arrived from if the street still existed. She does this without direction. She knows this site.

Tyler stands at the center position.

He does not read it the way he read A-267 and A-268. He does not assess and catalog. He stands there and is simply present — in a different mode than the archive visits, less archaeology and more something else, something the Camera Op's footage will capture but the Camera Op will not name in Entry 39.

At six minutes:

**TYLER:** "I started this in 1943 and I didn't know I was starting it. And then I went back in 1952 and I made it stronger without knowing I was doing that either. And then I came back in 2026 and started a deliberate construction on top of it." He doesn't raise his voice. "I've been building this corner for eighty-three years."

Silence.

**TYLER:** "The 2026 construction is sitting on top of eighty-three years of accumulation I left behind without knowing what I was leaving." He takes the coin out. He turns it over — 1901 on the face. "All those Sundays I counted after the music stopped. Twelve Sundays walking past an empty corner. Every time I passed it I was adding something to what was there. I didn't know that. I thought I was just grieving something."

He closes his hand around the coin.

**TYLER:** "I was building."

**Day 220, afternoon — at Bacon's building**

Exchange Student has pulled the full Elwood interview transcript and is cross-referencing with the archive's Detroit 1943 weather and event records.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "James Elwood says the music started 'sometime in early 1941' and the last session was 'the Sunday before the trouble.' June 13, 1943. A Sunday. The trouble was June 20th — Belle Isle." She looks at her notes. "Elwood describes approximately forty people at the peak. He says 'it felt like it had always been there' and 'when it stopped we didn't talk about it, we just knew.'"

**TYLER:** "I was there the last Sunday. I wasn't at any of the others. I heard the music walking past and I stopped and I stayed for three hours." He is quiet. "I'd been in Detroit since early 1943, working on something else. I walked past that corner on June 13th by accident."

**EMILY:** "You were there for the last one and only the last one."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EMILY:** *(writing)* "The unrepeatable moment — because it was the last time, you knew it was the last time, even though you didn't know that yet." She looks up. "That's the mechanism. You don't have to know in the moment that it's unrepeatable. The knowledge arrives later. But the attention was there — the quality of attention that pays the right kind of attention to something it will never see again." She writes something else. "Reims: you knew the window was having its first morning. You knew it was unrepeatable from the start. Hastings 1943: you didn't know. The knowing came later, when you counted twelve empty Sundays. But the attention was already in the geography."

**TYLER:** "The construction was already started."

**EMILY:** "The construction was already started."

Long silence. Exchange Student keeps her notes. Camera Op is still filming. Everyone in the room is aware that something is being resolved that has taken a very long time.

**TYLER:** "How many of the 340 photographs have this kind of history behind them?"

**EMILY:** *(not looking up from the map)* "We won't know until you visit them. But I think: more than we expect."

**TYLER:** "And the ones from before 1947 — before the CARDINAL-3 station opened, before the archive had Detroit documentation — those are just gone. I have no photographs from those years. Just the memory."

**EMILY:** "The memory is in the geography. You don't need the photographs."

He looks at the coin for a long moment.

**TYLER:** "I've been building for a long time without knowing I was building."

**EMILY:** "Yes."

**TYLER:** "The network is very large."

**EMILY:** *(writing, not looking up)* "The network is very large."
