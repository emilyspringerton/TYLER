## PRODUCTION NOTES

- Episode type: The Retrograde, Phase I continues. Paris, Days 369–378.
- Purpose: Three additional Paris departure sites measured for retroactive FAREWELL classification.
- Sites: A-Commune (Père Lachaise, 1871, Bloody Week), A-Terror (Marais/Saint-Gervais, 1793, departure before Terror), A-68 (Latin Quarter, May 1968, student uprising departure).
- Thesis: Paris as Tyler's city of revolutionary endings. Tyler has been in Paris for 1789, 1793, 1848 (not filmed yet), 1871, 1968 — always at the edge of something becoming itself, always leaving after. The Retrograde is not just cataloguing departures. It is recognizing a pattern: Tyler goes to Paris when the argument is at maximum density and leaves when the argument resolves into violence or quietude.
- FAREWELL taxonomy update: A-Commune becomes the second FAREWELL-A in the network. A-Terror is FAREWELL-B. A-68 is a new category: FAREWELL-DEFERRED (Tyler knew he was leaving, but did not believe the departure was permanent — because Paris had already taken him back three times).
- Camera Op note: she films differently in Paris than she did in Amsterdam. Amsterdam she was looking for light. Paris she is looking at Tyler looking at history. She is making a film about something she cannot see directly.

*[She looks up from the list.]*

**TYLER:** "I left after each resolution. Which is not the same thing as leaving after each one. Some resolutions take longer than others." He looks at the city. I left in August." He is quiet for a moment. "I always tell myself I am leaving for the summer."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "And then it becomes permanent."

## ACT ONE — 1871

### A-COMMUNE — PÈRE LACHAISE CEMETERY, PARIS

*[Day 370. 20th arrondissement. Père Lachaise Cemetery. The Mur des Fédérés — the wall where the last Communards were shot on May 28, 1871.]*

*[The Exchange Student has the Paris file open to the 1871 section. Tyler is at the wall.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "CARDINAL-3 has no original 1871 filing from Paris. The Commune sites were not in the initial audit."

**TYLER:** "I was not in Paris for the initial audit in the way I was in Paris in 1871. The difference is the proximity of the departure." He looks at the wall. "In 1871 I was at this wall on May 28th. I was not supposed to be here. No one was supposed to be here who was not Versaillais or Communard. I was here because I had spent the last two months inside the Commune's lines and I could not make myself leave until it was over."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "You were in Paris during the Commune."

**TYLER:** "I was in Paris during the Commune. March through May, 1871. Two months. I watched the Commune govern the city. It was — the most sustained thing I have witnessed at that density, at that speed. It was not perfect. It was not competent in every direction. But the density of active civic experiment within that perimeter was extraordinary. I was watching people who had decided that the argument was worth running all the way through, even if the ending was predictable."

**CAMERA OP:** "Was the ending predictable?"

**TYLER:** "From the inside: not quite. From thirty years later: yes. The Versaillais had the army. The Commune had the barricades and the conviction. The argument was wrong about the odds." He pauses. "That has happened before. The argument was also wrong about the odds in Amsterdam in 1787."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(instruments ready)* "You left on May 28th."

**TYLER:** "I left on May 28th. After the executions at this wall. The last group of Communards were brought here and shot against these stones. I was — I should not have been here. I was here. I watched it." He looks at the wall. "And then I left. I walked out of Paris through the Vincennes gate on the afternoon of May 28th. I told myself I would return when it was possible to be in Paris again without it being impossible to be in Paris."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "And you returned?"

**TYLER:** "In 1877. Six years. I came back for a few months and then left again. The city had quieted in the way that cities quiet after something catastrophic — the surface was normal and the surface was unbearable."

*[She runs the instruments at the Mur des Fédérés.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Reading coming in." *(pause)* "34ft. FAREWELL-A." She looks at the reading. "Conscious departure. You knew you were leaving."

**TYLER:** "I was standing at this wall on May 28th, 1871, watching the last of the Commune end, and I understood that I was leaving Paris that afternoon. I was not uncertain about it. I was leaving." He is very still. "The 34 feet is May 28th at this wall. The knowledge that the thing I had been watching for two months was over, and that I was going."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Second FAREWELL-A in the network. A-355 Herengracht at 30ft. A-Commune at 34ft. You were more conscious of leaving Paris than you were of leaving Amsterdam."

**TYLER:** *(quietly)* "In Amsterdam I knew I was leaving a city I had loved for a long time. In Paris in 1871 I was leaving the specific thing the Commune had been — which was briefer and more concentrated. The love is the same mechanism. The concentration of it was higher."

*[The Camera Op is not filming Tyler at the wall. She is filming the names engraved nearby — the thousands of Communards buried here. She is filming something Tyler is not saying.]*

## ACT TWO — 1793

### A-TERROR — RUE DES BARRES, SAINT-GERVAIS, PARIS

*[Day 373. 4th arrondissement, just behind the Église Saint-Gervais-et-Saint-Protais.]*

*[Tyler's 1793 Paris address — a room above what was then a bookshop. Now a residential building in a narrow medieval street.]*

**TYLER:** "I had been in Paris since 1789. July 1789 to July 1793. Four years. The Revolution and the Republic and the Committee of Public Safety forming. The King's trial and execution. January 1793. The Republic at war with everyone." He looks up at the building. "By July the Committee was accelerating. I understood that what had been a movement was becoming a mechanism for eliminating the movement's members. This is a known progression. I had seen versions of it."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "So you left."

**TYLER:** "I left. On August 3rd, 1793. I had a room here — similar to the Île Saint-Louis, four years later. I left the room. I said I was leaving because the political situation was becoming untenable for anyone not affiliated with the Committee. I said I would return when the situation stabilized."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "And you returned in 1795."

**TYLER:** "I returned in 1795, yes. The Thermidorian reaction — Robespierre executed in July 1794, the Terror ending, the Convention reformed. I came back in 1795 for three months and left again." He pauses. "The stability I had said I was waiting for was not the stability that arrived. What arrived in 1795 was exhaustion rather than stabilization."

*[The Exchange Student runs the instruments.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "A-Terror. 1793. Rue des Barres address." *(reading)* "12ft. FAREWELL-B."

**TYLER:** *(half to himself)* "I told myself I was coming back."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "You returned in 1795."

**TYLER:** "For three months. Not to this room. To Paris, yes, but not to this room. The departure from this room in August 1793 was permanent." He looks at the building. "I was in Paris in 1789 when the argument became a revolution. I was in Paris in 1791 when the Champ de Mars massacre happened — the Republican petition gunned down by the National Guard. I was in Paris in 1793 when the revolution became the mechanism for eliminating itself. And I left." He pauses. "I told myself the reason was external — the political environment. The actual reason was that I could not watch what the argument was doing to the people who had been making it."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "FAREWELL-B. Smaller reading than 1871."

**TYLER:** "In 1793 I told myself I was coming back. In 1871 I knew I was going. The difference is in the quality of the departure, not in the loss."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Were you more attached to Paris in 1793 or in 1871?"

**TYLER:** *(long pause)* "In 1793 I had lived in Paris for four years. In 1871 I was there for two months." He is quiet. "The Commune site is larger. I think because the density of what happened in those two months was higher than the density of those four years. Not that the four years was empty — but 1789–1793 was watching history over a long stretch. The Commune was two months of maximum density followed by one day of absolute clarity: the argument is over and I am going." He looks at the old bookshop building. "The mechanism rewards clarity of departure. The 1793 departure is blurred by the self-deception that I would return. The 1871 departure is the cleanest thing in the Paris file."

## ACT THREE — 1968

### A-68 — BOULEVARD SAINT-MICHEL, PARIS

*[Day 376. Latin Quarter. The Boulevard Saint-Michel.]*

*[Tyler looking at the boulevard. Students. Cafés. The same street that in May 1968 had barricades.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "May 1968. You were here."

**TYLER:** "I arrived in March 1968 — I had heard the student organizing beginning at Nanterre, the Mouvement du 22 Mars. I was interested. The argument was being made again in the form of a student occupation. The argument has been made in this form many times — 1968 was not the first time. But 1968 in France was notable for the speed at which the argument scaled. March to May. Eight weeks." He looks at the boulevard. "By May 10th the barricades were here. Rue Gay-Lussac. I was here."

**CAMERA OP:** "Did you participate?"

**TYLER:** "I observed. Which is — I have learned that I do not participate in the arguments I witness. I am a documentation project. I observe." He pauses. "I helped carry someone who had been hurt from one of the barricades to a doorway. That is the most active I was. I was nearby for eight weeks and I helped one person to a doorway."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "And you left in August."

**TYLER:** "I left in August 1968. The government had given the wage increases. De Gaulle had given the speech. The National Assembly elections had returned a Gaullist majority. The students had mostly gone home or reconvened their summer arguments in smaller rooms." He looks at the boulevard. "I left in August and told myself it was for the summer. I said: I will come back in September."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "And you didn't."

**TYLER:** "I didn't come back until 1974. Six years." He is quiet. "The 1968 argument had ended in the summer — not in violence, not in triumph. In negotiation and exhaustion. I left because the argument had become the administration of its own aftermath. The most interesting moment was over. I told myself this would pass. That Paris would be interesting again by September." He pauses. "Paris is always interesting by September. But I didn't come back for six years."

*[The Exchange Student runs the instruments.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "A-68. Boulevard Saint-Michel area. 1968." *(reading)* "Reading at 19ft." She consults the FAREWELL taxonomy notes. "Between FAREWELL-A and FAREWELL-B. You knew you were leaving but you told yourself it wasn't permanent."

**TYLER:** "That is — more accurate than either category captures."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "A third FAREWELL type. The departure where: you know you are leaving, but you do not believe that this departure is the last one. Because this place has already taken you back three times."

**TYLER:** "I was in Paris in 1789. I was in Paris in 1871. I was in Paris in 1968. Paris had always taken me back. I had no reason to believe August 1968 was the final departure — there was no mechanism for the departure to be permanent because the pattern of return was too established. And yet it became six years."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(writing)* "FAREWELL-C. Departure from a site where the subject has an established pattern of return. Subject believes the pattern will continue. Pattern breaks. Density between FAREWELL-A (conscious) and FAREWELL-B (unconscious): 15–22ft range. Mechanism records the departure as departure even when subject does not believe departure is permanent, because the subject is relying on a pattern the mechanism does not consider guaranteed."

**TYLER:** "The mechanism knows Paris doesn't owe me a return."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The mechanism knows Paris doesn't owe you a return. Correct."

## INTERLUDE — THE SHAPE OF THE PARIS FILE

*[Day 377. The Seine. Walking.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Four Paris departure sites measured. A-278: FAREWELL-B, 11ft. A-Commune: FAREWELL-A, 34ft. A-Terror: FAREWELL-B, 12ft. A-68: FAREWELL-C, 19ft."

**CAMERA OP:** "Three different categories."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Three different ways of leaving Paris." She looks at Tyler. "Which one is the real one?"

**TYLER:** "They are all real. The differences are in what I knew at the time." He pauses. "1793 — I did not know I was leaving. 1871 — I knew I was leaving absolutely. 1968 — I knew I was leaving but believed I would be back. Three ways of not staying."

**CAMERA OP:** "What about 1789? The July departure you mentioned — you left Paris in 1795 for the last time that era."

**TYLER:** "1795 is not in the retrograde list. I was not measuring Paris in 1795 as someone who had not yet developed a category for departure. The 1795 departure is — earlier than the instrument." He looks at the river. "And 1848. I was in Paris in 1848. February through June. The Second Republic. Louis-Philippe abdicating. The National Workshops. The June Days — June 23rd through 26th, the workers' uprising suppressed by General Cavaignac. I left in July 1848."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "1848 is on the retrograde list?"

**TYLER:** "1848 is on the retrograde list. We did not measure it this visit. I have not been ready to measure 1848." He is quiet. "1848 was the argument I was most convinced would work. More than 1789, more than the Commune, more than 1968. In February 1848 I was convinced — for approximately three days — that the argument had won. That the Second Republic was the thing all the prior arguments had been trying to make. I was wrong. But for three days I was convinced." He looks at the water. "The June Days ended that. I left in July. I have not measured the July 1848 departure. I am not ready to see what the instruments say about it."

*[The Camera Op keeps the shot on the river. She has learned to wait.]*

## CLOSING SHOT

*[Day 378. The Gare du Nord, Paris. Departure.]*

*[Paris retrograde: 4 sites measured, 1848 deferred. Tyler, Exchange Student, Camera Op at the departure platform.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Paris total, retrograde sites: 4 completed, 1 deferred. Full Paris retrograde: 5 sites after A-278 on the original list, plus 1848 which was added today."

**TYLER:** "Six Paris departure sites. Four measured. 1848 deferred."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Why deferred?"

**TYLER:** "Because I already know what the reading will be."

*[The Camera Op films the platform. Departure boards. The station that in 1871 was a staging ground for Versaillais troops.]*

**CAMERA OP:** *(to herself, filming)* "You've been to Paris five times. Six times. More. You keep coming back. And you keep leaving. And you say you know what the 1848 reading will be and you won't look at it yet."

*[She doesn't say this out loud. She keeps filming.]*

**TYLER:** *(to the Exchange Student, about the next city)* "Lyon."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Lyon for the retrograde?"

**TYLER:** "Lyon is not on the primary retrograde list. But I want to go to Lyon." He is quiet. "I was in Lyon in 1793. The siege. The Convention's forces against the federalists. I was in Lyon for the siege and the aftermath." He pauses. "I left Lyon in February 1794. I have never measured the Lyon departure. It is not on the retrograde list because the Lyon sites were never entered into the full archive."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "You have sites outside the archive?"

**TYLER:** "The archive starts in 1622, when the Eastwind system began. The Lyon departure is from 1794 — it should be in the archive. But I have never — I have never told anyone about Lyon 1793–1794. It is not in the archive because I have not put it there."

*[The train. The departure board. Lyon.]*
