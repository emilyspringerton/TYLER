### COLD OPEN

**[INTERIOR — WESTMINSTER ABBEY NAVE. DAY 293. EARLY MORNING.]**

*[The nave. Before the tourists arrive. The light through the high windows is the specific quality of January morning light in stone — grey-white, old. The hum is here: quieter than in the City, but continuous, the same frequency, unchanged.]*

*[Tyler is sitting in a chair near the north transept. He has been here for some time before the Exchange Student and Camera Op arrived. He is not doing anything in particular. He is sitting in the Abbey.]*

*[The Exchange Student comes through the north door, clipboard and the CARDINAL-3 file under her arm. She sees him and does not say anything. She sits in a chair two rows back and opens the file.]*

*[The Camera Op enters behind her. She takes in the scene — Tyler in the nave, morning light, the hum — and starts filming without saying anything.]*

*[For a moment, nobody speaks. The Abbey does what it does: it holds everything that has ever happened in it.]*

**TYLER:** *(not looking up)* "You found me."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "You said Westminster Abbey, first morning."

**TYLER:** "Yes." He is still for another moment. "I always come here first when I'm back in London. It's been the case for a very long time. Not to — I don't have a reason. I just come here first."

*[The Camera Op has the lens on him. He is aware of it. He does not acknowledge it more than that.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The Westminster CARDINAL-3 file has six entries. Two have the PRIOR notation." She opens to the first page. "Westminster Abbey is one of them."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The other is — actually, the second PRIOR marker is a question mark. The site date is unclear."

**TYLER:** "We'll get to it." He stands. "We should start from here."

**[EXCHANGE STUDENT measures. She walks from the north transept slowly outward, marks the edge at the north door, continues outside into Parliament Square, marks the edge again.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "79 feet." She checks. "The construction goes outside the building."

**TYLER:** "It does."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "It starts here" — she gestures toward the chair where Tyler was sitting — "and runs out through the north door to approximately — " she consults her measurements — "the north pavement of Parliament Square."

**CAMERA OP:** "Why outside the building?"

*[Tyler considers.]*

**TYLER:** "I wasn't only inside. I was outside first." He looks at the north door. "The coronation procession went down to Whitehall after. When the crowd thinned, I came inside. I was outside for the ceremony and inside for the — after."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Coronation date?"

**TYLER:** "January 15, 1559."

*[The Exchange Student writes this on the map without a pause. January 15, 1559. She marks the PRIOR symbol. 79ft.]*

*[The Camera Op holds the frame. Tyler at the spot in the nave where the construction is anchored. 79 feet running from January 1559. 467 years. Nobody remarks on the arithmetic.]*

**TYLER:** *(reading from the journal)* "January 1559. Elizabeth's coronation day. I was outside first, in the crowd near the north entrance. The procession was long — several hours — and very cold. When the Queen and the court moved down toward Whitehall, the Abbey opened for general entry. I went inside. The candles were still lit. The nave was very full of the morning. I don't have the right word for it: everything that had just happened was still in the room. I sat for a time near the north transept. Two men near me were crying, which I thought was appropriate. The acoustics in the nave are very good." He closes the journal. "That's the entry."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The men crying — was that—"

**TYLER:** "I thought it was correct. Something had just ended. Something had just begun. The room held both things at once." He looks up at the nave ceiling. "The acoustics are still very good."

### ACT ONE — THE JANUARY PAIR

**[EXTERIOR — WESTMINSTER HALL, OLD PALACE YARD. DAY 295. AFTERNOON.]**

*[The hum. Parliament Square, then the Palace of Westminster complex. The Exchange Student is checking the file. Camera Op filming the approach. Tyler is walking ahead, his pace specific — not fast, not slow, the pace of someone who has been in this geography before.]*

*[Westminster Hall: the oldest part of the Palace of Westminster. The great hammer-beam roof. Where Parliament sat for centuries. Where trials were held.]*

*[Tyler stops at the Old Palace Yard approach, facing the entrance to the hall.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The notation date is January 25, 1649."

*[Tyler nods.]*

**CAMERA OP:** "The trial."

**TYLER:** "Day three of the trial. I was in the gallery above the main floor. There were —" He pauses. "It was crowded. They'd opened it to spectators. I stood in the gallery with perhaps two hundred other people."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "What was the trial date range?"

**TYLER:** "They opened January 20. The verdict was January 27. I was there on the 25th." He looks at the entrance. "I went because I thought I should see it. I had not seen anything like it. I have not seen anything like it since."

*[He walks to the entrance. He stands at the threshold.]*

**TYLER:** *(reading from the journal, quietly)* "January 25, 1649. Westminster Hall. The trial, Day Three. I was in the gallery above. The room knew. Everyone in the room knew the outcome before it happened. They had known before January 20, I think, most of them. They came anyway. I understood why they came. When something this large is ending, the correct response is to go and stand where it is ending. To be present for the record. I did not know I was making a construction. I knew that I was bearing witness to something that would not happen again." He closes the journal. "That's the entry."

*[He stands at the threshold. He does not go inside.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(checking the measurement)* "71 feet."

**TYLER:** "I knew the verdict. I went anyway." He is quiet for a moment. "That's what the room was full of. People who knew and came anyway."

*[The Camera Op has been very still behind the lens.]*

**CAMERA OP:** *(after a pause)* "You went to see something that was already decided."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**CAMERA OP:** "You understood that."

**TYLER:** "The understanding was why the construction formed." He pauses. "I think."

**[EXTERIOR — BANQUETING HOUSE, WHITEHALL. DAY 295. LATE AFTERNOON.]**

*[A short walk from Westminster Hall. Whitehall. The Banqueting House: Inigo Jones's Italian Baroque ceiling, the Rubens paintings above, and outside it — the scaffold, January 30, 1649.]*

*[Tyler walks directly to the spot on the pavement in front of the Banqueting House where the scaffold was erected. He knows the position without measuring.]*

*[The hum.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(quietly)* "This is the same week."

**TYLER:** "Five days later. January 30."

*[He stands in the position. He does not speak for a moment.]*

**TYLER:** "I came back on the 30th." He takes the journal from his coat pocket. He opens it. He reads.

**TYLER:** *(reading)* "January 30, 1649. Whitehall. The scaffold was erected outside the Banqueting House — they built it against one of the first-floor windows so he could step directly from the room to the platform. The crowd was very large. It was a cold morning, very clear. I was perhaps forty feet from the scaffold, on the south side of the crowd. I could see clearly." He pauses. He reads on for another moment, silently. Then he closes the journal.

*[He holds the closed journal for a moment.]*

**TYLER:** "I'll leave the rest in the journal."

*[He looks at the Camera Op. Two seconds. The camera has him, holds the frame. Two seconds: a taunt, not a revelation. Then he looks at the Banqueting House.]*

*[The coin stays in his pocket. He does not flip it. The camera registers the absence.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** *(after the measurement)* "76 feet."

**TYLER:** "The largest in Westminster."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Why larger than the trial?"

**TYLER:** "The trial — I went knowing what I thought I should witness. The 30th —" He pauses. "I went to stand where something was ending that I had seen in parts for thirty years. The English Civil War was a very long thing to watch. The 30th was where all of it resolved." He is quiet. "I went to stand at the end of it. 76 feet seems right."

*[He looks at the Banqueting House once more, then turns and walks back toward Parliament Square.]*

**TYLER:** *(over his shoulder)* "The January pair. Same week. Westminster Hall and the Banqueting House. I went to both. I did not know what I was marking. Now I know: I was marking the end of a thirty-year event that I had been watching. I was marking it in my specific way. The mechanism recorded the markings."

*[The Exchange Student marks A-315 on the map. The map has two new UNINTENTIONAL symbols in Whitehall: A-314 and A-315. Five days apart.]*

*She is not calling it "attention to absence" anymore. Absence isn't quite right. The events were present — vivid, immediate. Tyler was present to them. But he was present to them at their edges: the after, the edge, the crowd around the inevitable.*

### ACT TWO — WHAT HE NEEDED

**[EXTERIOR — ST. JAMES'S PARK, NORTH SHORE OF THE LONG WATER. DAY 297. MORNING.]**

*[The park. Early. The long water catching the light. Ducks and pelicans and the specific quietness of a London park in the morning. The hum, lower here, the construction at 34 feet is gentler than the Whitehall sites.]*

*[Tyler is standing at the water's edge. He has a cup of coffee. He looks comfortable in a way that he hasn't looked for the last two days.]*

**TYLER:** "I needed somewhere without history for a while. And there it was, surrounded by history on every side."

*[He gestures at the geography: Parliament to the east. Buckingham Palace to the south. Whitehall to the north. The park in the middle.]*

**TYLER:** "I started coming here sometime in the 1740s. I still come here. The pelicans have been here since 1664 — a present from the Russian Ambassador to Charles II. They've had pelicans continuously since then. Different pelicans."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Different pelicans but the same pelicans."

**TYLER:** "Different pelicans but the same pelicans." He sips his coffee. "Yes."

*[The measurement. 34 feet, extending from the waterline north into the park path.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "STAYING. 1740s onward."

**TYLER:** "I had a route I walked from where I was living toward Whitehall when I was in London for long periods. Through the park, north shore, then out at the Horse Guards. I walked it so many times that I stopped counting. I just kept coming back."

**CAMERA OP:** "What were you in London for? In the 1740s?"

**TYLER:** "Things. Various things." He looks at the water. "I was in London on and off throughout the 18th century. Parliament. The coffee houses. The general intellectual life of the city at that period was very good. I was here for pieces of it."

**CAMERA OP:** "Not building anything."

**TYLER:** "Not intending to build anything. Apparently building this." He gestures at the 34-foot radius. "34 feet from the pelicans and the long water and the specific quality of walking here in the mornings. That's the park. I think that's correct."

*[He finishes his coffee. He looks at the water for another moment. He does not read from the journal.]*

**TYLER:** "Some sites don't need the journal. The park is very clear. I like the park."

*[The Exchange Student marks A-316: ST. JAMES'S PARK, LONG WATER. 34FT. STAYING. 1740S–PRESENT. The symbol fits on the map without crowding anything. There is room here.]*

**[EXTERIOR — VICTORIA EMBANKMENT, WESTMINSTER APPROACH. DAY 298. EVENING.]**

*[The Embankment. Evening light on the Thames. The specific quality of the Thames at Westminster — the Parliament buildings on the far side of the Embankment, the river wide and grey-green.]*

*[Tyler is walking. Not measuring yet — just walking the Embankment path.]*

**TYLER:** "They opened it in 1870. It runs from Westminster Bridge to Blackfriars. Joseph Bazalgette designed it — he also did the main sewer system underneath, which is the more important project but less visible. When they opened the Embankment, I walked it every morning for three weeks."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Why every morning?"

**TYLER:** "Because it was new and very good and clearly going to be here for a long time. When something is built well and clearly designed to last, I like to walk it in the first weeks. Before everyone settles into taking it for granted."

*[They measure the site. 22 feet, from the Embankment path toward the river wall.]*

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "STAYING. 1870. Three weeks of morning walks."

**TYLER:** "Three weeks, and then I left London for a while, and when I came back I walked it again, and when I came back after that, I walked it again. Not every time. But often." He leans on the Embankment wall and looks at the river. "The engineering was excellent. Still is. Bazalgette built it for twice the population London had at the time. He calculated for growth. That's the kind of planning I respect."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "22 feet seems small for three weeks of consistent morning walks."

**TYLER:** "Three weeks is three weeks. And I was admiring the engineering, not — I wasn't in the presence of something unrepeatable. I was walking a very good piece of infrastructure." The smile: slightly too knowing. One second toward the Exchange Student. "Some things don't need to accumulate. Three good weeks is three good weeks."

*[The Exchange Student marks A-317: VICTORIA EMBANKMENT, WESTMINSTER APPROACH. 22FT. STAYING. 1870+.]*
