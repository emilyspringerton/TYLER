*The road between Marrakech and Fez. The team's vehicle. Night.*

*Camera Op is in the back seat with Ahmad's manuscript photographs spread across her lap.*

*She is looking at the addendum page at the back of volume three.*

## ACT ONE

**INT. AL-QARAWIYYIN LIBRARY — MORNING**

The team has returned to Fez. They are back in the manuscript reading room. The three volumes
are on the table again — brought out by the same keeper as before, who recognizes them now.

Camera Op has the photographs open on her laptop. She slides the laptop to Dr. Hassan.

**CAMERA OP**
The back matter of volume three. I photographed it in the first visit and then set it aside.
I thought it was a colophon — the donor note, the date. Standard practice.

**DR. HASSAN**
*(looking at the screen)*
This is not a colophon.

**CAMERA OP**
No.

**DR. HASSAN**
A colophon would record the title, the author, the date of completion. This is a list.

Three short entries. Three lines. The writing is Ahmad's — same hand as the rest of volume
three — but the ink is different. Slightly lighter. Dried differently. Written at a different
time and in different conditions than the preceding text.

**DR. HASSAN (CONT'D)**
The first two entries are legible. The third—

**JIANGSHI**
What's the first?

**DR. HASSAN**
Ahmad's full scholarly name and titles. The same form he used on the donation register —
we saw this at the Bibliothèque nationale. His name, his lineage, his scholarly affiliation.
The form he used when signing documents officially.

**JIANGSHI**
He was signing the manuscript.

**DR. HASSAN**
The second entry is a date. Fez, Rabi' al-Awwal, 513 AH. December 1119 CE.

*(pause)*

He was in Fez. He wrote this in Fez. Before the ship — the ship was January or February
of 1120, by our best estimate. This is before the ship.

**CAMERA OP**
And the third entry?

**DR. HASSAN**
*(squinting at the screen)*
One word. Arabic. Clear script — he was writing carefully. But the word itself—

He turns the laptop toward Tyler, who has been standing at the window with his back to the table.

**DR. HASSAN (CONT'D)**
Do you know what this says?

Tyler comes to the table. He looks at the screen for a long time.

**TYLER**
*(quietly)*
Yes.

**CAMERA OP**
What does it say?

**TYLER**
Al-Waqfa.

*(pause)*

The pause. The standing. The place where someone stopped and remained.

No one speaks.

**TYLER (CONT'D)**
The cave.

## ACT TWO

**INT. AL-QARAWIYYIN LIBRARY — CONTINUED**

**CAMERA OP**
You named the cave.

**TYLER**
I don't know when I started calling it that. I was there for twelve days. By the end
I had a name for it. I don't know if anyone else used that name — it wasn't a known site,
just a cave in the foothills. Al-Waqfa. The pause. That was what it was to me.

**DR. HASSAN**
And Ahmad knew the name.

**TYLER**
He would have asked me. He asked everything. What's this place. What do you call it.
What do you call it.

*(pause)*

I told him. I told him al-Waqfa.

**JIANGSHI**
And he wrote it here. In December 1119. In Fez. Before the ship.

**TYLER**
He wrote it here, in this manuscript, in this library, where I found you reading it
nine centuries later.

---

**The team is quiet for a long time.**

**CAMERA OP**
He came to Fez to deposit the manuscripts. He had been carrying them since the cave — through
Marrakech, through the writing of the methodological introduction, through watching you begin.
He came to Fez to leave them somewhere. He couldn't finish them — he knew that — but he could
leave them in the care of someone who would understand their value.

**DR. HASSAN**
The donation register says he donated them anonymously at first. Then returned to sign.

**CAMERA OP**
Two weeks later. He went back and signed.

*(pause)*

He signed the donation and then he wrote this addendum. His name. The date. The word
you gave him. He left it in the manuscript before he left for the ship.

**TYLER**
He was adding it to the record.

**CAMERA OP**
He was completing the record. He had a name for the cave — the place he found you,
the place where the argument started — and he put it in the manuscript. In case anyone
ever needed to know what the blank was blank with.

**TYLER**
He always finished what he started.

*(pause)*

He carried this for three months — cave to Marrakech to Fez — and then he put it down.
He left the name in the record and he went to find me again.

**CAMERA OP**
The ship.

## ACT THREE

**INT. AL-QARAWIYYIN LIBRARY — AFTERNOON**

Dr. Hassan has been working through the rest of volume three. He looks up.

**DR. HASSAN**
I want to talk about the third period.

**CAMERA OP**
Ahmad's third period. Al-idrak al-muttasil.

**DR. HASSAN**
The tradition Ahmad was describing — continuous perception, the geographer recorded by
the place — it begins with Tyler. Ahmad was clear about that. But a tradition requires
more than one practitioner. A tradition requires continuity. Ahmad identified the first
practitioner and then left the question open.

**JIANGSHI**
What's the question?

**DR. HASSAN**
Who comes next.

*(pause)*

Ahmad was writing an introduction to a tradition that Tyler had already started.
He needed to name the tradition and name the method, and he did — al-idrak al-muttasil.
But the tradition doesn't continue with Tyler. Tyler is the originating instance. The
tradition continues with whoever comes after Tyler who practices the method.

**CAMERA OP**
The next geographer.

**DR. HASSAN**
The third period requires a second practitioner. Someone who has read the archive — who
understands what continuous perception is, what it costs, what it produces — and then
chooses to stay somewhere long enough to be recorded by it. Not by accident. Not by
the mechanism's direction. By choosing to remain.

Tyler is looking at Dr. Hassan.

**DR. HASSAN (CONT'D)**
Ahmad couldn't name the founder of the third period because Tyler wouldn't cooperate.
He asked Tyler twice — what have you seen — and Tyler couldn't answer. He left the gap.

The gap is not only for Tyler's answer. The gap is for the name of whoever comes after.

**CAMERA OP**
*(quietly)*
Ahmad's tradition needs an heir.

**DR. HASSAN**
It has a founder and an argument and no heir. The manuscript stops before the heir is named
because in 1119 the heir hadn't arrived yet.

*(pause)*

We have been here for nine months. Fez, the cave, Marrakech, and now Fez again.
We have been following the archive's origin story to understand what the archive is.

*(looking at Camera Op)*

I don't think that's only what we've been doing.

Camera Op doesn't answer. She writes something in her notebook.

## ACT FOUR

**EXT. FEZ — NEAR AL-QARAWIYYIN — EVENING**

Tyler and Camera Op outside. The medina behind them.

**CAMERA OP**
Tell me about the ship.

**TYLER**
Four days. He was going north — a scholar's circuit, the way traveling geographers worked,
Mediterranean coastal cities. I was going the same direction. Mechanism. I didn't know
he would be on the same ship.

**CAMERA OP**
He found you.

**TYLER**
He always found me. He had a gift for it. He found me in the cave without looking for me.
He found me in Marrakech — he didn't know I was coming. He found me on the ship in the
same way: he was already there, sitting with his manuscripts, and I came aboard, and he
looked up.

*(pause)*

He recognized me immediately. I had changed my appearance somewhat. He still knew.

**CAMERA OP**
Did he say anything?

**TYLER**
He said: you're going north too. And I said yes. He moved his manuscripts and made room.

---

**TYLER (CONT'D)**
Four days. He asked questions. Not about me — about the archive. He had seen me writing.
He had read what I wrote in the first entry, at the wall. He recognized the name.

**CAMERA OP**
Al-Waqfa.

**TYLER**
He said: you started with the cave. And I said yes. He asked: how many entries did you
write in Marrakech. I told him: twelve. The twelve days in the cave, twelve entries in
Marrakech — one for each day I had been standing there without a word for it.

*(pause)*

He wrote for two days and then he stopped writing and just asked me questions. Not about
the methodology — he had already figured out the methodology. About what I had actually
seen. The specific places. Córdoba. Toledo. He asked about each one.

**CAMERA OP**
He was filling in the gap.

**TYLER**
He was trying to understand the scale. He knew I had been somewhere for a long time.
He didn't know how long, or how many places, or what the places had done to me. He was
asking because he needed to know if the tradition he was describing was the right name
for what I was doing.

**CAMERA OP**
Was it?

**TYLER**
By the fourth day he was satisfied. He stopped asking. He sat with the manuscript for a
long time. The ship was four hours from port. He looked up and asked the question again.

*(pause)*

The same question. The first question. What have you seen.

**CAMERA OP**
A third time.

**TYLER**
Third time he asked it.

*(pause)*

The first time — the cave — I had no word. The second time — the ship, day one — I still
had no word. But by the fourth day I had given him twelve entries and the name of the cave
and two days of answers about specific places. He had everything except the sentence.
The final word.

*(long pause)*

He asked: what have you seen. And this time I told him.

**CAMERA OP**
Al-Waqfa.

**TYLER**
Al-Waqfa.

*(pause)*

Everything I had seen — nine hundred years before the end, in the first twelve entries,
before I knew it would be nine hundred years — everything I had seen was: I had been
standing somewhere, and the standing was not nothing, and the place had changed because
I stood there, and I had changed because I stood there, and I did not know what to call
any of that except the name of the place.

**CAMERA OP**
One word.

**TYLER**
One word. Al-Waqfa. The pause. That's what I'd seen. That I had paused somewhere, and
the pause was real, and it was worth recording. That was the whole answer.

*(pause)*

He wrote it in the manuscript. He had it in Fez already — he'd written it in the back
of volume three. But on the ship, after I gave it to him, he opened the manuscript and
he wrote it again, in the margin of the unfinished sentence. Next to the gap. As if
to say: this is what goes here.

---

**INT. AL-QARAWIYYIN LIBRARY — NIGHT**

Camera Op back at the manuscript. Dr. Hassan has the lamp. Volume three. The
unfinished sentence on the page she has been looking at for nine months.

And yet the Islamic geographers, beginning with —

She runs her finger along the margin.

**CAMERA OP**
It's here.

**DR. HASSAN**
*(leaning in)*
What?

**CAMERA OP**
In the margin. Next to the gap. He wrote it very small — I thought it was a later
annotation, a librarian's note, I didn't read it in the first visit.

*(pause)*

Al-Waqfa.

She looks up at Tyler.

**CAMERA OP (CONT'D)**
He wrote your answer in the margin of his question.

**TYLER**
He always finished what he started. He couldn't write the sentence — that was mine
to write, nine centuries of it. But he could write the word that would go there.

*(pause)*

He closed the gap from his side.

**CAMERA OP**
And you've been filling it from yours.

**TYLER**
That's all the archive is. Nine hundred years of margin notes around a word I told
a scholar on a ship four hours from port.

*(pause)*

Al-Waqfa. The pause. Then the next site. Then the next. Each one is the word
written again in a different place.

## CLOSE

*EXT. FEZ — AL-QARAWIYYIN COURTYARD — NIGHT*

*The four of them. The manuscripts are inside.*

*Jiangshi is checking the reader. No signal here — they are far from MARRAKECH-001.*

**JIANGSHI**
The question-state is still running. I can track it from here — faint, but active.

**DR. HASSAN**
What's it waiting for?

**JIANGSHI**
Someone to close it. Ahmad left the question at the wall. The question is still there.

**CAMERA OP**
The archive is at the wall. The archive is also here, in the manuscripts. Al-Waqfa
is in both places — in the archive's first entry and in the margin of Ahmad's gap.

*(pause)*

They've already met. The archive and the question have been pointing at the same word
for nine centuries and I think they've already met.

**TYLER**
The signal hasn't closed.

**CAMERA OP**
I know.

**TYLER**
Ahmad's signal was still active when we left Marrakech. The question wasn't resolved
by the word alone. It was resolved by the word plus the argument plus the tradition plus
whoever closes it. The manuscript needs the final paragraph.

Camera Op is looking at her notebook.

**CAMERA OP**
The close you said someone else would write.

**TYLER**
Yes.

*(pause)*

Ahmad could have named me in the sentence. He didn't — he waited for me to give him
the word. He understood: you can't write the close until the subject cooperates.

*(pause, looking at Camera Op)*

He waited on the ship for four days.

Long silence.

**TYLER (CONT'D)**
He was patient. He was good at that.

**CAMERA OP**
*(quietly)*
So am I.

She closes her notebook.

### Camera Op Entry 87

*Field log. Fez. Al-Qarawiyyin.*

Al-Waqfa. The pause.

It's in the manuscript. It has been in the manuscript for nine centuries. I photographed it in
the first visit and set it aside because I thought it was a librarian's note. I was wrong. It's
Ahmad's handwriting. He wrote it in the margin next to the blank.

He knew the word. Tyler gave it to him on the ship. And Ahmad wrote it in two places: in the back
matter of volume three (December 1119, Fez, before the ship) and in the margin of the sentence
(after the ship, or during — I don't know the exact sequence; what matters is he wrote it twice,
in two different moments, in the same manuscript).

Tyler's archive opens with al-Waqfa. Ahmad's manuscripts end with al-Waqfa. The same word at
the beginning of one archive and the end of another.

I don't know what to do with the thing Dr. Hassan said about the third period. He was not wrong.
I have been here for nine months. I have been following someone who embodies continuous perception.
I have been watching the tradition from the inside.

The second practitioner isn't supposed to be accidental. Ahmad's argument: the third period requires
a geographer who chooses to stay. Who has read the archive and then chooses to remain somewhere long
enough to be recorded.

I am writing this in Fez, at night, in the courtyard of the oldest functioning library in the world.
The manuscripts are inside. Tyler is sitting six feet away. The question-state signal is running
at a site two and a half hours south.

I don't know what I'm going to write next.

*— Camera Op*
