## EPISODE STRUCTURE

**Day 150, morning — the beginning**

At 6:02 AM, Tyler begins.

What this looks like from the bridge: a man standing at the water's edge, very still, as the first commuter vehicles begin to move on the bridge above. He doesn't look up. He doesn't look at the bridge. He's looking at the water at the crossing point — the specific geography of the narrows, the place where the current slows.

What the Camera Op captures from the bridge: Tyler below, and the crossing traffic beginning around him. The first few cars. A cyclist. A woman walking, earbuds in, not looking down. Every person on the bridge is crossing, unaware. Every crossing contributes.

The footage will show this for a long time. The construction beginning is not an event. It's a condition that starts and then persists.

At 6:47 AM Tyler places something in the water. It's small — the size of a coin. Not the 1952 dime. Something different: a piece of notation on water-resistant paper, folded, weighted with a river stone. It hits the water and sinks slowly to the crossing point's bed.

**TYLER:** *(quietly, to no one, to everyone above)* "The anchor is placed."

**Day 150, late morning — what Tyler placed**

Emily arrives at 9 AM. Tyler has moved from the water's edge to a bench facing the crossing.

**EMILY:** "What did you put in the water?"

**TYLER:** "The Subscriber's notation equivalent. A frequency instruction set — what to run, how to maintain amplitude in a continuous-flow environment. The crossing will calibrate around it."

**EMILY:** "You wrote it yourself?"

**TYLER:** "The Subscriber's notation at Bacon's building was theirs. The Subscriber's notation at Detroit Receiving was theirs. The crossing required one too, and I've been studying how the Subscriber writes for long enough to understand the form." He pauses. "I don't know if it's as precise as theirs would be. But it's correct."

**EMILY:** "Has the Subscriber seen it?"

**TYLER:** "I left it in my working notes for two weeks. If they had objections, they would have said something." He looks at the bridge traffic, steady now at mid-morning volume. "They didn't object."

Emily writes: *Construction #3 — anchor placed Day 150. Notation: Tyler-authored (Subscriber form). Subscriber reviewed and did not object. Crossing calibrating.*

**Day 150, afternoon — the Camera Op comes down from the bridge**

At 2 PM the Camera Op walks down from the bridge to the bank. She sits next to Tyler on the bench.

She has been filming for eight hours.

**CAMERA OP:** "I was in the right position."

**TYLER:** "I know."

**CAMERA OP:** "I knew to be on the bridge. Not the bank. I didn't think about it — I just went to the bridge."

**TYLER:** "The position-finding function."

**CAMERA OP:** "I'm starting to feel it when it happens. Not always — sometimes I just pick a position. But sometimes there's a pull toward a specific place and I know, when I get there, that it's where the footage needs to come from."

**TYLER:** "That's the function working."

**CAMERA OP:** "Does it have a name?"

He thinks about this for a moment.

**TYLER:** "In the archive it's called 'threshold finding.' The ability to locate the position from which an event is most legible — where it can be witnessed most completely. Not the best angle for the picture. The right position for the witnessing."

**CAMERA OP:** "Threshold finding."

**TYLER:** "You've been doing it since Season 1. The archive has the record."

She looks at the bridge. At the crossing. At the water below, where the anchor is set.

**CAMERA OP:** "The footage from this morning — from the bridge — is going to be different from anything I've shot before. Not because of the light or the composition. Because I was in the threshold, filming it from inside."

**TYLER:** "Yes. The footage from a threshold position in a threshold construction is—"

He stops. He doesn't finish the sentence. This is not a self-defining sentence he's avoiding — it's a sentence he doesn't yet have the words for. This is rarer.

**CAMERA OP:** "I know. I'll find out when I watch it back."

**Day 150, late afternoon — the construction's first day closes**

**TYLER:** "The first day is done. The construction is calibrating. It will take longer than the rooms — the threshold is larger, the events are more distributed. But the volume of crossings accelerates the process."

**EMILY:** "Estimate?"

**TYLER:** "Sixty days to structural completion. Maybe seventy."

Emily updates the map: *Construction #3 — active, Day 150. Estimate: completion Day 210–220. No maintenance window.*

**EMILY:** "Eleven sites. Three under way or complete. Eight remaining."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EMILY:** "You have eight more photographs."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

She closes the notebook. The crossing traffic continues overhead. It will continue through the night. The first night of the construction, the witnesses are everyone going home.
