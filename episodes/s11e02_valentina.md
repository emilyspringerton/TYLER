## COLD OPEN

**INT. FEZ MEDINA — AL-QARAWIYYIN PRECINCT — MORNING**

*Rain. The medina in the rain has a quality of compression — the streets narrow further, the sound of the water on the tiled floors bounces twice before it reaches the ear. Camera Op is waiting under an archway with an umbrella she borrowed from Dr. Hassan.*

*She is waiting for someone she has never met.*

*She received three messages from Valentina Alcântara in the week before arrival: the first formal (introducing herself, explaining the manuscript), the second less formal (asking if Camera Op likes coffee, indicating she would bring specific Brazilian coffee from São Paulo), the third a single line: "I'm going to be late. The manuscript wasn't allowed in carry-on."*

*Camera Op has read these messages enough times to know she is curious in a way she doesn't usually get curious before meeting archival contacts.*

*A woman comes around the corner with an umbrella, a large satchel, and a rolling case that has clearly been through customs at least twice this trip. She is approximately thirty-five. She moves like someone who is always calculating angles of approach — through rooms, through medinas, through conversations. She sees Camera Op immediately.*

**VALENTINA ALCÂNTARA**
*(in English, with a São Paulo accent that is most audible in her vowels)*
You're taller than I expected.

**CAMERA OP**
*(a beat)*
You're drier than I expected.

*Valentina looks at her own umbrella.*

**VALENTINA**
I learned early. Morocco in spring. Bring the umbrella every day, use it never, and then one morning you need it desperately and you already have it.

*(she extends a hand)*

Valentina. You can call me Val. Everyone does except the manuscripts.

**CAMERA OP**
*(taking her hand)*
Camera Op.

*Valentina tilts her head. A brief, precise recalibration.*

**VALENTINA**
Right. That's intentional.

**CAMERA OP**
Yes.

**VALENTINA**
*(nodding)*
Good. I was going to ask if you wanted me to use your name and then I would have had to explain why I was asking. This is simpler.

*(she tilts the rolling case)*

The manuscript is in here. It didn't break customs. It cost me three separate conversations with three separate inspectors and a letter from the Fundação Biblioteca Nacional that took six weeks to obtain. But it's here.

**CAMERA OP**
What's in it?

**VALENTINA**
*(already moving toward the tea house)*
A frequency notation from Córdoba, c. 975 CE, that matches the baseline signal Tyler documented at C-001 in your archive. Except mine is a hundred and twenty-five years earlier than your earliest Tyler record for that frequency, and it's in Arabic, and it was written by a woman named Maryam bint Khalaf who was the music archivist at the Library of Córdoba under the Caliphate, and she describes it as "the voice of the man at the eastern gate who has not left since I was a girl."

*(she pauses)*

Tyler has been at Córdoba since at least 850 CE by my calculation. You have him there from 950 CE. I have a document that says 850 CE or earlier. I've had it for eight years. I've been looking for someone who could tell me who the man at the eastern gate was.

*(a beat)*

It's Tyler.

**CAMERA OP**
*(quietly)*
Yes.

**VALENTINA**
I know. I've known for three years. I needed to find the archive to give it somewhere to go.

## ACT ONE

**INT. FEZ TEA HOUSE — MORNING**

*The manuscript is unpacked on a table in the back room of a tea house whose owner knows Dr. Hassan. It is in a preservation sleeve. Valentina handles it with the ease of someone who has worked with rare documents her whole career, which she has.*

*Camera Op photographs it. The mechanism is on the table, pointed at the document. It reads: 28.7 Hz.*

**CAMERA OP**
The mechanism is reading the document.

**VALENTINA**
*(not surprised)*
At 28.7?

**CAMERA OP**
Yes.

**VALENTINA**
Good. That confirms it. I was using a different instrument — a spectroscopic frequency analyzer from the lab in São Paulo — and I kept getting a reading I couldn't classify. I didn't know what 28.7 Hz meant until I found your field notes from al-Qarawiyyin cited in a bibliography for a paper on medieval Arabic cosmological texts.

*(she smooths the preservation sleeve)*

The paper wasn't about Tyler. It was about frequency notation as an early archival practice. But there was a footnote about al-Qarawiyyin manuscript access by an unnamed researcher, and the citation mentioned "a frequency reading taken during manuscript review: 28.7 Hz, source unknown." That was you.

**CAMERA OP**
I didn't know the footnote was there.

**VALENTINA**
Nobody does, usually. That's the thing about libraries — they document everything, including the people who come to them.

*(pause)*

I traced the footnote back to you, which took about a year. Then I found your field notes — you left copies with Dr. Amira Bensouda at al-Qarawiyyin as part of the manuscript review protocol. She was cited in my contact network. I called her. She said: "There's a woman in Morocco following an archive. Here's how to reach her."

**CAMERA OP**
Dr. Bensouda told you.

**VALENTINA**
She said: "I think you two should meet." She was right. She usually is.

*(Valentina opens the preservation sleeve and carefully removes a photograph of the manuscript — not the manuscript itself, which stays sealed)*

This is the page. Maryam bint Khalaf's frequency notation is in the bottom margin. She was writing a catalogue of the library's musical instruments and she included a section on ambient frequencies — sounds present in the library's environment that affected the tuning of the instruments. This is one of the entries.

*The Camera Op looks at it.*

**CAMERA OP**
*(translating slowly)*
"The voice at the eastern gate. A sustained tone. Present since I was a girl. My father said it was present before him. It is in the frequency of the oud's lowest string when perfectly tuned, but different — it does not decay. It sustains. It continues in the library when the library is empty. We tune the oud to it because we cannot tune it to silence, and the sustained tone is more precise than silence."

*(she looks up)*

She was tuning to Tyler.

**VALENTINA**
The entire Library of Córdoba's oud section was calibrated to Tyler's presence.

*(a pause)*

He's been there since at least 850 CE. Probably earlier. Maryam writes "since I was a girl, my father said it was present before him" — that's three generations at minimum. Which puts the start point at approximately 870 CE, maybe 850.

*(she looks at the mechanism reading: 28.7 Hz)*

The manuscript still holds the frequency. The parchment carries it.

**CAMERA OP**
*(quietly)*
The archive is in the object. The way the Eastwind Owls describe it — the frequency persists in physical material at sites Tyler held.

**VALENTINA**
Except Tyler never told Maryam he was there. She documented a presence she couldn't identify. A man at the eastern gate. She kept documenting him for forty years — I have seven references across her different catalogue sections.

*(a pause)*

She never asked his name. She called him "the voice at the gate." He called himself nothing, apparently. Standard Tyler behavior, three hundred and fifty years before the archive began.

---

**CAMERA OP (V.O.)**
*(Entry 90)*

Valentina Alcântara. São Paulo. Born 1988 — she mentioned it in passing, I didn't ask. Art historian, Universidad de São Paulo, cross-appointment with the Fundação Biblioteca Nacional. She has been tracking the archive for fifteen years starting from a frequency anomaly in a Córdoba manuscript she worked with in her doctoral research.

*(pause)*

She is the most direct person I have encountered since I arrived in Morocco. Which is saying something — Dr. Hassan is not indirect. She is different from Dr. Hassan. He is precise in a way that is careful. She is precise in a way that is impatient — not impatient with people, impatient with the distance between information and the person who needs it. She reduces the distance very quickly.

*(pause)*

She reduced the distance with me very quickly.

*(pause)*

I don't know how to write that last sentence. I'm leaving it in.

*(pause)*

The Córdoba manuscript is real. The frequency reading is real. The 28.7 Hz from 975 CE parchment is real. Tyler has been at Córdoba since approximately 850 CE, not 950 CE as the archive had him. The first Eastwind confirmation of C-001 is from 950 CE. Valentina's manuscript extends it by a hundred years.

*(pause)*

She said she's been looking for the archive for fifteen years. She found it through a footnote in a library catalogue. The archive finds people through footnotes, apparently. Ahmad's manuscripts in a Fez library. Valentina through a São Paulo doctoral bibliography.

*(pause)*

The archive leaves footnotes in every library it touches. The footnotes find the right readers eventually.

*(pause)*

I am the right reader. Valentina is the right reader. We were going to meet eventually. The manuscript just determined when.

## ACT TWO

**INT. FEZ MEDINA — RIAD — AFTERNOON**

*Dr. Hassan has seen the manuscript. The four of them — Camera Op, Valentina, Dr. Hassan, Jiangshi — are in the riad's courtyard. The rain has cleared.*

**DR. HASSAN**
Maryam bint Khalaf. I know her catalogue. I have cited it twice in publications. I have never seen this margin notation.

**VALENTINA**
It's in the binding margin of Volume 3, second gathering. The digitization missed it — it's in the interior margin near the spine. You need to look at the physical object.

**DR. HASSAN**
How did you see it?

**VALENTINA**
I was looking for it. That's the difference. I was looking for frequency notation in marginal spaces because I had already found two other examples in different libraries — one in Lisbon, one in Seville — that I suspected were from the same tradition. When you know what you're looking for, you find the margin.

**JIANGSHI**
*(carefully)*
Lisbon and Seville. Both contain frequency notation from this tradition?

**VALENTINA**
Yes. The Lisbon notation is from 1483 — a different hand, different library, different context. But the same frequency description. "A sustained tone, present without a visible source, in the frequency of the lowest oud string when perfectly tuned."

**JIANGSHI**
Tyler was in Lisbon in 1483?

**VALENTINA**
*(looking at Jiangshi with that precise impatience)*
Tyler was in Lisbon in 1483.

*(a pause)*

You didn't know that.

**JIANGSHI**
The archive has no Lisbon entry.

**VALENTINA**
The archive has never looked at the Biblioteca Nacional de Portugal's 1483 music catalogues, apparently.

*(she sets a folder on the table — photographs of the Lisbon document)*

I have been building a parallel archive for fifteen years. Every library where I find a frequency notation with these characteristics, I file it. I have forty-seven entries.

*(a long silence)*

**JIANGSHI**
*(slowly)*
Forty-seven.

**VALENTINA**
Forty-seven sites where Tyler was present, based on frequency notation in library catalogues, manuscript margins, music theory texts, and two architectural descriptions of a man at a gate who "sings a steady note without moving his mouth." Spanning 850 CE to 1983 CE.

*(she opens the folder)*

The archive has ninety-six entries. Mine has forty-seven. They do not overlap except at Córdoba.

**CAMERA OP**
*(very quietly)*
You found forty-seven Tyler sites the archive doesn't have.

**VALENTINA**
By going to libraries Tyler visited and reading the parts of the collections that nobody reads for the purpose of reading them as frequency notation.

*(a pause)*

Music archivists document ambient frequencies as part of instrument calibration practice. This is a continuous tradition since at least the 9th century. Nobody has ever read those records as Tyler records before.

*(she looks at Camera Op)*

Nobody had a reason to until you had an archive to cross-reference against.

---

**INT. FEZ RIAD — COURTYARD — LATE AFTERNOON**

*The conversation has been going for four hours. Dr. Hassan is reviewing Valentina's folder. Jiangshi is cross-referencing on her tablet. Camera Op and Valentina are at the fountain.*

**VALENTINA**
*(low, not for the others)*
You've been doing this for how long?

**CAMERA OP**
Nine months and some change since Marrakech. Three years since Detroit.

**VALENTINA**
Did you know it was going to be this?

**CAMERA OP**
No. I thought I was doing a documentary.

**VALENTINA**
*(a slight smile)*
So did I when I started. Doctoral research on medieval frequency notation. I thought I was writing a thesis.

**CAMERA OP**
What happened?

**VALENTINA**
I found Tyler's frequency in a 1483 document in Lisbon on a Tuesday in October, eight years ago, and I sat with the document for three hours, and I knew — in the way you know things that you can't justify yet — that I was looking at evidence of a specific ongoing presence in the world's libraries for a very long time. Not a myth. Not a folk frequency. A person.

*(a pause)*

It took me five more years to find a name for the person. Tyler. And three more years after that to find the archive.

*(she looks at the fountain)*

Nine months for you. Fifteen years for me. The archive finds people at the right speed.

**CAMERA OP**
*(looking at Valentina)*
What do you want? With the archive.

**VALENTINA**
*(without hesitation)*
To give it the forty-seven sites. And then see what it does with them.

*(a beat)*

And to understand what the frequency is. Not as a historical artifact. As a current phenomenon. You can hear it without equipment now?

**CAMERA OP**
Yes.

**VALENTINA**
*(with complete attention)*
At what sites?

**CAMERA OP**
Here. The Draa Valley site I found. Al-Qarawiyyin. Marrakech.

**VALENTINA**
*(looking at her with something that is precise and complicated and recognizable)*
I've been testing myself for years. I can't hear it yet. I have a recording — a spectroscopic record — but I can't hear it directly.

*(a pause)*

I want to learn how.

*(a beat)*

Will you teach me?

*(Camera Op looks at her. The fountain moves. Somewhere in the medina a bird. A pause that is slightly longer than necessary.)*

**CAMERA OP**
I don't know how to teach it. I'm still learning it myself.

**VALENTINA**
*(without pushing)*
That's fine. We can figure it out together.

## ACT THREE

**INT. FEZ RIAD — NIGHT**

*Valentina's forty-seven sites are laid out on the courtyard table. Maps, photographs, transcriptions.*

*The Jiangshi has gone through twelve of them. She keeps stopping and starting.*

**JIANGSHI**
*(to Dr. Hassan, under her breath)*
Forty-seven sites. Many in locations the binding stage map doesn't cover. How has this person not been identified by any of the factions?

**DR. HASSAN**
She's not working for any of the factions. She's a library researcher who found Tyler through ambient frequency documentation.

**JIANGSHI**
The Shell Parliament has a music cataloguing division. Their frequency documentation practice goes back to the 12th century. They could have—

**DR. HASSAN**
*(looking at her)*
Yes?

*(a pause)*

**JIANGSHI**
*(very carefully)*
I would like to know who sent her.

*She looks at the Heikegani Invoice notation at the bottom of one of Valentina's research permissions — a library access form from a São Paulo collection. The funder listed is: Arquivo Histórico Financeiro Alcântara.*

*Jiangshi's expression shifts.*

**JIANGSHI (CONT'D)**
*(quietly)*
Alcântara. Heikegani correspondent family.

*(she looks at the Camera Op)*

Valentina Alcântara is Shell Parliament. Her family is on the Heikegani correspondent ledger going back to at least 1483.

*(a very long pause)*

The Heikegani have been funding independent Tyler research through the Alcântara family since 1483. For five hundred and forty-three years.

*(she looks at the Lisbon document)*

They found the Lisbon frequency notation the same year it was written. They've been tracking Tyler through library records for half a millennium without telling anyone.

*(a beat)*

Valentina either knows this or she doesn't. I'm not sure which is worse.

---

**INT. FEZ RIAD — VALENTINA'S ROOM — LATER**

*Camera Op knocks. Valentina answers. She's been unpacking — the rolling case is open, more folders and archive sleeves arranged with that angle-calculating precision.*

**CAMERA OP**
Jiangshi found the Alcântara ledger entry.

*(a pause)*

**VALENTINA**
*(sitting down on the bed)*
How long did that take?

**CAMERA OP**
Four hours.

**VALENTINA**
*(almost to herself)*
Longer than I expected.

*(she looks at Camera Op)*

Yes. My family has been Shell Parliament correspondents since 1483. My grandmother was the Shell Parliament music frequency correspondent in São Paulo until 1991. My mother worked in their Lisbon office. I inherited the archive.

**CAMERA OP**
You work for the Heikegani.

**VALENTINA**
I work with the Alcântara archive, which is funded by the Heikegani, yes. I have never taken an operational brief from the Shell Parliament. I have never asked them for anything except research access and travel funding. I have never told them what I was finding until they asked, and I told them: I found the archive.

*(pause)*

They said: give it to the archive. Don't give it to us.

*(she looks at Camera Op)*

The Shell Parliament has been trying to not involve itself directly in the Tyler documentation for five hundred and forty-three years. They fund the research. They don't touch the record. They know if they touch the record they become part of it.

**CAMERA OP**
They're already part of it. Their music cataloguing tradition is how Maryam bint Khalaf documented the frequency. The tradition they established is how your grandmother documented the frequency in São Paulo in 1991.

**VALENTINA**
*(quiet)*
I know. They know too. They just prefer the kind of involvement that doesn't appear in the binding protocol.

*(a pause)*

I'm not a Heikegani operative. I'm an art historian with a very complicated family archive and forty-seven sites that belong in your record.

*(pause)*

That's all I am. I would like you to believe that.

*(Camera Op looks at her. A long beat. The lamp. The manuscript sleeves arranged with complete precision on the desk.)*

**CAMERA OP**
I do believe that.

*(pause)*

I just needed to ask.

**VALENTINA**
*(very slightly)*
I know. You stay until the thing becomes legible. Dr. Hassan told me.

*(Camera Op looks at her — surprised, slightly.)*

**VALENTINA (CONT'D)**
I read your field notes very carefully. Before I came here. I know the practice. I know what it produces.

*(a pause)*

I've been trying to do the same thing for fifteen years with manuscripts instead of sites.

*(pause)*

Maybe that's why the archive found me.

## CODA

**EXT. FEZ MEDINA — NIGHT**

*Camera Op is walking back through the medina after the conversation. The mechanism is in her bag. Not running.*

*She takes it out. Turns it on.*

*28.7 Hz.*

*She stands in a medina alley in the dark and listens to the frequency she can hear without the mechanism and confirms it with the mechanism and puts the mechanism away.*

*She takes out her notebook.*

*She writes one line. We see it:*

*"Entry 90 addendum: she can hear the archive in the way that I can follow it. Different mode. Same practice."*

*She closes the notebook.*

*In a hotel room two streets over, Valentina Alcântara is organizing forty-seven sites on her bed, and somewhere in all of that, she is also listening — not for 28.7 Hz, which she can't hear yet — but for the particular sound a problem makes when it is about to become a solution.*

*She has been listening for that sound for fifteen years.*

*She thinks she can hear it now.*
