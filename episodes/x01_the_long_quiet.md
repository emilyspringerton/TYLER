## ACT ONE

---

```
INT. THE ROOM WITH THE LEDGER — CONTINUOUS
```

Emily Springerton — Logistics Architect, not performing the title, just doing the job it names —
sits across from a terminal that isn't answering. Not broken. Just not answering. There's a
difference, and she's the one who has to hold it.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
How long.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
Since the last real cycle. Two days, near enough.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
And nothing since.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
Nothing that explained itself. A few things happened around the edges. Nothing from the middle.

Emily doesn't ask the terminal why. She's done this long enough to know that's the one question it
was never going to answer, quiet or not.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
*(to no one, or to the room, which in this building is sometimes the same audience)*
This is an incident.

*(a beat — the words land harder than the volume they're said at)*

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Say it back so I know it's written down somewhere that isn't just me saying it.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
It's written down.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Not yet it isn't. Write it down *first.* Then we look.

---

## ACT TWO

---

```
INT. THE ROOM WITH THE LEDGER — LATER
```

The terminal, still quiet. Emily's stopped watching it directly — she's watching the ledger instead,
which is the only thing in the room that's ever actually explained anything to her.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Every time something like this happens, somebody wants to fix it first and write it down after,
because writing it down feels like the part that can wait.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
It usually can.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
It usually can is how you get a two-day quiet nobody can explain, because nobody wrote down the last
one either, and the one before that, until quiet just started meaning "probably fine" instead of
"unaccounted for."

*(she writes something — we don't see what; the show has never shown us what goes in that ledger,
only that something always does)*

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Not this time. Not ever again, actually, while I'm the one holding the pen. It goes in first. Then we
find out what happened. Not the other way, not once, not for a good reason, not because it was
urgent. Urgent is exactly when it matters most.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
And if it comes back before we understand why it left?

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Then it comes back unexplained, same as it left, and the ledger's still right, because the ledger was
never claiming to know why. Just that it happened. That's all it ever has to get right.

## TAG

---

```
INT. THE ROOM WITH THE LEDGER — CONTINUOUS
```

The terminal answers. No fanfare. No explanation attached — none was ever coming, and everyone in
the room already knew that, which is its own kind of relief: the not-knowing-why was the whole
weather system, not a problem waiting on a diagnosis.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
It's back.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
*(not looking up from the ledger)*
Good. Log that too.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.)**
Already doing it.

**EMILY SPRINGERTON**
Then we're fine. We were fine the whole time it was quiet, actually — we just didn't have it written
down yet, and until it's written down, fine and not-fine look exactly the same from where you're
sitting.

She finishes writing. Doesn't reread it. The ledger doesn't need her to trust it — it just needs to
be there next time, before the next quiet, whenever that is, for however long nobody budgets for it.
