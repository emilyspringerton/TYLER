## COLD OPEN

*The Bibliothèque nationale du Royaume du Maroc. Rabat. Morning. Day 2.*

*Camera Op is at the same table, in the same reading room. The box is still in front of her. She has been here for four days now.*

*She has the register. She has the photograph. She has Ahmad's name.*

*She is looking at the acquisition chain — the series of transfers, mergers, and institutional absorptions that brought the Almohad-period materials to this library.*

*She is working backwards. From now to 1121.*

*The librarian approaches.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
You were here yesterday.

**CAMERA OP:**
And the day before.

**LIBRARIAN:**
What are you looking for. Specifically.

*Camera Op looks up.*

**CAMERA OP:**
Manuscripts. Donated in 1121 CE by a scholar named Ahmad ibn Yusuf. Geographic texts. An unfinished work on the reconciliation of Greek and Islamic geographic systems.

*The librarian considers this.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
The 1121 materials were not held here. Not originally.

**CAMERA OP:**
I know. The institution that held them was absorbed in the fourteenth century. I am trying to determine where the manuscripts themselves went when they were absorbed.

*The librarian is quiet for a moment.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
Come with me.

---

## THE ACQUISITION CHAIN

*A different room. Archival storage. The librarian brings out a bound ledger — institutional transfer records.*

*She opens it and turns to a section.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
When the earlier institution was absorbed, the manuscripts were assessed for their condition and their scholarly value. Materials of primary geographical scholarship were handled separately from routine documents.

*She runs her finger down a column.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
Geographic manuscripts from the Almohad period. Here. 1339.

*Camera Op leans in.*

**LIBRARIAN:**
They were transferred to al-Qarawiyyin. In Fez.

*Silence.*

**CAMERA OP:**
The manuscripts were sent to Fez.

**LIBRARIAN:**
The Marinid transfer. The Qarawiyyin library was the appropriate destination for geographic scholarship of that period. It had the custodial capacity and the relevant collections.

*The Camera Op looks at the entry. She is very still.*

**CAMERA OP:**
Ahmad was heading to Fez. He never arrived. He ended up in Rabat.

*She looks at the ledger.*

**CAMERA OP:**
And two hundred years after his death, his manuscripts were transferred to the library in the city he was trying to reach.

*Long pause.*

*The librarian watches her.*

**CAMERA OP:**
*(quietly)*
They got there.

---

## THE CALL FROM THE CAVE

*Camera Op is outside the Bibliothèque nationale. She is sitting on a bench. Her phone is in her hand.*

*She calls Tyler.*

*He answers immediately. His voice is different — quieter, more interior, as if he has been somewhere without sound for a while.*

**TYLER:**
You found something.

**CAMERA OP:**
The manuscripts were transferred to al-Qarawiyyin. In the fourteenth century. Marinid period. Geographic scholarship went to Fez.

*Long silence.*

**TYLER:**
Ahmad's manuscripts arrived in Fez.

**CAMERA OP:**
Two hundred years after he donated them. But they got there.

*Tyler is quiet.*

**CAMERA OP:**
He was heading to Fez. Something interrupted him. He ended up in Rabat, donated the work, came back and signed his name. Two centuries after his death, the work reached the city he was traveling to.

*Very long silence.*

**TYLER:**
The work arrived where he intended it to arrive.

**CAMERA OP:**
Two hundred years late.

**TYLER:**
But it arrived.

*He pauses.*

**TYLER:**
The journey was interrupted. The work continued on its own.

*Long pause.*

**CAMERA OP:**
I'm going to Fez.

*Silence.*

**CAMERA OP:**
If the manuscripts survived the Marinid transfer and nine centuries since — if al-Qarawiyyin still holds them — they are in Fez. I want to find out if they exist. I want to find the physical texts.

**TYLER:**
The writing in layers.

**CAMERA OP:**
Yes.

*He is quiet for a long time.*

**TYLER:**
Ahmad never arrived. You are going to complete his journey.

*A different silence.*

**CAMERA OP:**
I'm going to try to find what he left there.

*Pause.*

**CAMERA OP:**
Tyler. How are you. You've been in the cave for two days now.

*A pause. Something changes in the silence.*

**TYLER:**
Something is beginning to happen.

---

## WHAT IS BEGINNING

**CAMERA OP:**
What kind of something.

**TYLER:**
The mechanism has been running the zero-taxonomy reading for thirty-eight hours. This morning, at approximately 4 AM, it produced a partial output.

*Silence.*

**CAMERA OP:**
What kind of partial output.

**TYLER:**
Not a classification. Not anything in the current taxonomy. The mechanism produced a frequency signature — a narrow band at 11.4 hertz — that it did not suppress. It let it through. It could not name it, but it did not suppress it.

*Long pause.*

**CAMERA OP:**
Eleven point four hertz.

**TYLER:**
Vassago 11.11 is the closest catalogued frequency. Soft precognition. But what the mechanism returned is not Vassago. It is 11.4 — close to Vassago but outside the nominal band. A frequency I don't have a name for. Possibly the pre-archive version of something I later learned to call Vassago.

*Pause.*

**CAMERA OP:**
The pre-archive version of soft precognition.

**TYLER:**
The mechanism is reading what I left here before I had a framework for what I was doing. What I left here was precognitive in some sense — I was in this place before I started building deliberately, and whatever I was doing in this place had the quality of reaching forward in time — and the mechanism is only now beginning to learn how to see it.

*Very long pause.*

**CAMERA OP:**
Tyler.

**TYLER:**
Yes.

**CAMERA OP:**
Are you okay being there alone.

*Long pause.*

**TYLER:**
The team is at the entrance. And the cave is — I am not alone in the cave. There is something of me here from before. I am sitting with an earlier version of myself that I cannot speak to directly and that I cannot yet fully read.

*He pauses.*

**TYLER:**
It is not comfortable. It is necessary.

---

## WHAT THE CAVE IS TEACHING

*Tyler is still in the chamber. It is dark outside now. The team has lit the area around the entrance.*

*He has been here for almost forty-eight hours.*

*The Team Lead comes to the entrance.*

**TEAM LEAD:**
The instrument readings since 4 AM. The 11.4 hertz signature — it's consistent. It appears at irregular intervals, sometimes for thirty seconds, sometimes for two minutes. The pattern is not repeating. It is — exploratory.

**TYLER:**
The mechanism is not repeating a reading. It is making successive attempts to interpret an unfamiliar signal. Each 11.4 return is a new attempt.

**TEAM LEAD:**
Learning.

**TYLER:**
The mechanism has been calibrated to my post-archive behavior for nine centuries. It has well-established frameworks for what I do. What is here pre-dates those frameworks. The mechanism is encountering something it recognizes as itself — as a frequency it contains at its baseline — and it is trying to develop a way to read it.

*He looks at the cave walls.*

**TYLER:**
The mechanism is teaching itself to see what it was before it knew what it was.

*Team Lead absorbs this.*

**TEAM LEAD:**
And you?

**TYLER:**
I am trying to remember.

*Long pause.*

**TYLER:**
I have eleven hundred years of organized memory. The mechanism organized it — gave me the structures to hold what I experienced. Before the mechanism, I have something else. Not fog exactly. Impression without structure. I was somewhere and I left something and I knew something was happening but I did not know what it was called.

*He pauses.*

**TYLER:**
What is here is the memory-before-categories. The experience before the framework for experience existed.

*He is quiet.*

**TYLER:**
Ahmad wrote his geography in layers. First pass, then corrections. He built frameworks over what he had first observed.

*He looks at the walls.*

**TYLER:**
I have been doing that for twelve centuries. The cave holds what I had before the first framework.

---

## FAR END OF THE CHAMBER

*Tyler, late evening. He goes deeper into the chamber for the first time.*

*He has stayed near the entrance for two days. Now he moves toward the far end.*

*The instruments follow, on long cables.*

*The 11.4 hertz signal intensifies as he moves deeper.*

*And then — at the far wall of the chamber — the mechanism produces something new.*

*Not a classification. Not 11.4.*

*A number.*

*The instrument displays the number. Only that. No category label. No unit. Just: 12.*

*Tyler looks at the number.*

*He sits down in front of the far wall.*

*He puts his hand against the stone.*

**TYLER:**
*(very quietly)*
Twelve.

*He doesn't say anything else for a long time.*

*The team watches the number on the display.*

*It holds at 12.*

*Then 12 again, on the next reading.*

*And 12 on the reading after that.*

*The mechanism has no category for 12. It has no unit to attach to 12. It is returning a number the way a person might say a word they don't understand — hearing it, repeating it, not yet knowing what it means.*

**TEAM LEAD:**
*(quietly)*
Tyler. What is twelve.

*Very long pause.*

**TYLER:**
I don't know yet.

*He keeps his hand on the wall.*

**TYLER:**
But I was here when I was twelve.

## CLOSE

*Camera Op. Rabat. Last night here. Her things are packed. She is flying to Fez in the morning.*

*She writes.*

---

**CAMERA OP (V.O.):**

The manuscripts went to Fez.

Two hundred years after Ahmad donated them, two hundred years after whatever interrupted his journey and kept him in Rabat — the geographic work, the unfinished reconciliation of two traditions — arrived where he was trying to take it.

I am going to Fez to find out if it's still there. The al-Qarawiyyin library is one of the oldest continuously operating libraries in the world. If the manuscripts survived, they are documented. If they are documented, I can find the reference. If I can find the reference, I can request access to the physical texts.

I want to see his handwriting.

Tyler told me Ahmad wrote in layers. First pass and then corrections. He said he would recognize it. He described the pen pressure — heavier on the first pass, lighter as the hand relaxed into the revision.

If the manuscripts survive, and if I can bring photographs, Tyler can look at the writing of the person he spent nine centuries trying to answer.

I don't know what that will do to him. I know I am going to try.

The cave is returning 11.4 hertz signals that the mechanism can't name. And now the number 12.

Tyler said: I was here when I was twelve.

I have been thinking about that on the train back to the hotel and I cannot make sense of it in the framework I have. Tyler does not have a birth date. He does not know his age. The network minimum is 899 years, which establishes 1127 CE Marrakech as the earliest confirmed archive site, but Tyler predates the archive.

The cave is returning a number. Twelve.

Tyler was twelve years old when he was in this cave. Or twelve years into something. Or there were twelve of something here. Or the mechanism is counting something Tyler left here and there were twelve of them.

I don't know which.

I know that Tyler was here before the archive, and the cave is telling him something about what he was, and the number is twelve, and tomorrow I'm going to Fez to find the work of the man who asked Tyler the question that started the archive.

The question Tyler couldn't answer.

The cave is answering something else. A different question. One that predates the ship.

*— Camera Op*
