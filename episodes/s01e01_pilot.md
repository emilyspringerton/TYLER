INT. KITCHEN — SAME APARTMENT — CONTINUOUS

Back to handheld. The camera peeks around a doorframe into the kitchen.

**TYLER** stands at the sink, washing his hands. He looks ordinary. That's the most important thing: he looks ordinary. He moves like he's never been surprised once in his life, but the presentation is unremarkable. He could be from any decade between 1890 and 2040 without adjustment.

He dries his hands carefully. Too carefully. The precise folding of the towel, the methodical pressure on each knuckle — it's mundane in the way of something that's been practiced into performance.

He glances at the camera without turning fully. His eyes go to the lens, and there's a flicker of something — acknowledgment, amusement, very old exhaustion — and then it's gone.

**TYLER**
You're early.

**CAMERA OP**
Who are you?

Tyler dries the last finger. Sets the towel down. Turns.

**TYLER**
Depends who's asking.

He smiles. The smile is gentle and practiced. It makes you wonder what he finds funny. It makes you want to know. It prevents you from collapsing him into a fixed interpretation without your quite knowing why.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
Is this the cut where you make me the villain?

**[01:10–01:25] — THE IMPOSSIBLE ACT (TINY BUT UNDENIABLE)**

Tyler reaches into his jacket pocket. He pulls out a coin.

He holds it between his thumb and forefinger for a moment — not performing, just looking at it the way some people look at their watch. A check-in.

He flips it.

The coin hangs in the air for a beat — one beat longer than physics allows — and then lands on the back of his hand.

The camera zooms in. The Camera Op is doing it unconsciously, the lens pulling toward the coin without a deliberate choice.

The coin's face shows a date. The camera focuses. The date changes. The camera refocuses. The date is different. The camera is catching this about a half-second too late, every time, as if the coin is timing itself to the lens reaction speed.

**CAMERA OP (whisper)**
What the—

**TYLER**
Shh.

He closes his hand over the coin.

He looks straight into the lens for the first time. The full two seconds. Direct, sustained, and it feels like being recognized by something that has recognized you before.

**TYLER**
If you keep filming, you become part of it.

*(a beat; the room holds its breath)*

But you're going to keep filming.

**[03:00–06:30] — INTERVIEW: SESSION ONE**

```
INT. LIVING ROOM — INTERVIEW SETUP — LATER
```

Tyler sits across from camera in the interview setup. Warm lamp light. The furniture has been rearranged slightly — not enough to be strange, enough to suggest someone has done this before.

He sits comfortably. He looks like someone who has sat in interview chairs many times. He is, in fact, someone who has sat in interview chairs many times. Three previous attempts. Forty-seven sessions documented in the Eastwind Owl archive. He knows the format.

He is currently humming. It is very quiet. The sound mixer adjusts levels and it persists anyway.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
What brings you to Detroit?

**TYLER**
What brings anyone to Detroit?

*(beat)*

The city.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
How long have you been here?

**TYLER**
How long do you think I've been here?

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
I'm asking what you know.

**TYLER**
*(slight smile)*
That's a much better question.

He looks at the camera. Not into it — at it. There's a difference.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
I've been in Detroit before.

*(beat)*

Hasn't everyone?

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Can you tell us about yourself?

**TYLER**
Sure.

A pause.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
I'm a—

He stops. Exactly as if the sentence ran out of sentence. He looks at his hands.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
I'm very interested in this city.

The Camera Op adjusts framing. They've noticed he stopped.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
What interests you about Detroit specifically?

**TYLER**
Detroit is good at remembering things that didn't happen exactly the way anyone remembers them happening.

*(beat)*

That seems important.

Subject #1 sits with the ease of someone who has been doing this for a while. They have the serenity of someone who answered all the hard questions long ago and now lives with the answers.

He's very good at being in the room.

*(pause)*

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Does that concern you?

**SUBJECT #1**

No.

*(longer consideration)*

Maybe it should.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Do you know who he is?

**SUBJECT #1**
I know what he is.

*(beat)*

What he is is — different from who he is. I can tell you what he is. I can't tell you who he is.

I'm not sure he can, either.

**SUBJECT #1 (CONT.)**
He asked me if he could borrow my phone to check something.

*(beat)*

*(pause)*

*(pause)*

I kind of like knowing what time it is in—

**SUBJECT #1 (CONT.)**
Is that something I should say?

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**

**[09:00–11:00] — THE COIN, FIRST DOCUMENTATION**

```
EXT. DETROIT STREET — DAY
```

Handheld footage. The crew has followed Tyler outside. He is walking at a pace that seems normal but covers more ground than it should.

He flips the coin as he walks. Not performing — checking in. The date on the coin changes each time it lands. The camera catches it every third flip. The other two times the camera is always a half-second behind.

**CAMERA OP (quiet, to Len)**
I keep missing it.

**LEN (quiet back)**
Get closer.

**CAMERA OP**
He times it to the lens speed somehow.
That's not possible.

Len doesn't have an answer.

Tyler stops at a crosswalk. He waits for the light. He looks both ways like a person who has crossed many streets and would like to cross this one without incident.

The Camera Op gets close. The lens is six feet from him.

He flips the coin.

For a fraction of a second — before the date resolves — the Camera Op can see two dates simultaneously. 1901 and this year. Superimposed.

Then the coin lands.

Tyler looks at it. Reads it. Nods once, slight, like confirming a meeting time.

He puts the coin back in his pocket.

The light changes. He crosses.

**CAMERA OP (whispered, to themselves)**
What's the date on it?

Tyler doesn't turn.

**TYLER**
*(over his shoulder)*
Today.

*(beat)*

Among others.

**[21:00–28:00] — INTERVIEW: SESSION TWO**

```
INT. LIVING ROOM — INTERVIEW SETUP — NIGHT
```

The lamp light is warmer at night. The shadows fill in the corners differently. Tyler sits more comfortably this time, or less — it's hard to tell. He is present. He is always present.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
The photograph from 1901.
Can you explain that?

**TYLER**
What photograph?

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
We have a photograph, from Detroit, 1901.
The man in it looks like you.
He's tipping his hat at the camera.

**TYLER**
*(without missing a beat)*
A lot of people tip their hat at cameras.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Not in 1901. Photography was unusual enough
that most subjects weren't aware of the camera.

**TYLER**
The man in the photograph sounds camera-aware.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
He is. That's the unusual thing.

**TYLER**
*(slight smile)*
Did you want to ask if that's me?

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Is that you?

Tyler looks at the ceiling. Not evasively — the way someone looks at a ceiling when they are actually thinking, when the answer is genuinely complicated and they are not going to simplify it.

**TYLER**
That's an interesting question about what "you" means across different—

He stops.

The sentence, again, ran out of sentence.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
The man in the photograph has a coin in his right hand.
I notice these things.

*(beat)*

That's probably not useful to you.

**PRODUCER (O.S.)**
Why do you say that?

**TYLER**
Because it's useful to me.

*(very slight pause)*

Which means it's probably exactly what you wanted to hear.

He looks at the camera for a moment. Not the two-second taunt — this is different. This is more like an acknowledgment. Like, yeah, I see what you're doing. I know what this is.

Then he looks away.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
What else did you want to ask?

**[35:00–39:00] — END OF EPISODE: THE LOOK**

```
INT. LIVING ROOM — INTERVIEW SETUP — END OF DAY
```

The crew is breaking down the interview setup. Tyler is still in the chair. He is watching them pack up. He is not impatient. He is, apparently, interested in watching people pack up equipment.

Subject #1 brings him a glass of water. Tyler accepts it. He has now received a glass of water from Subject #1 in this episode, which means he now owes a favor to someone the Syndicate has already documented. The debt is small. It is also exactly the kind of small debt that prevents the file from closing.

The Camera Op is wrapping a cable. They look up and find Tyler watching them.

**CAMERA OP**
*(casual, almost accidental)*
Do you know what we're actually doing here?

Tyler's expression does something that isn't quite readable. It's not the smile. It's something before the smile. The moment before the practiced answer.

**TYLER**
Do you?

The Camera Op looks at the cable in their hands.

**CAMERA OP**
We're making a documentary.

**TYLER**
*(very gently)*
Right.

*(beat)*

A documentary about what?

The Camera Op opens their mouth. Closes it. The question is simple and somehow there is no simple answer.

**CAMERA OP**
About you.

**TYLER**
About a man who— 

He stops. Doesn't finish it. Smiles.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
Yeah.

He stands up. He picks up his jacket.

**TYLER (CONT.)**
*(to no one in particular, or to the room, or to something in the room that doesn't have a name)*
Thank you for the water.

He leaves.

The Camera Op watches him go.

The ambient audio on the recording, in the silence after Tyler exits, for exactly 72 seconds — contains the hum. Without the source. Without him.
