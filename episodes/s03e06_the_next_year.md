## ACT ONE — [03:00–22:00]

---

**[03:00–10:00] — THE PRODUCTION OFFICE**

```
INT. PRODUCTION OFFICE — MORNING — DAY 61
```

Tyler walks into the production office — the second time in the documentary record. The Production Coordinator looks up.

**PRODUCTION COORDINATOR:**
Tyler. Do you want—

**TYLER:**
Is she in?

**PRODUCTION COORDINATOR:**
*(a beat)*
She's in the back.

Tyler goes to the back. The Camera Op is there — reviewing footage on a monitor. Old footage. From early in the season. She hears him come in. She doesn't turn around immediately, finishes the shot she's watching.

Then she turns.

**CAMERA OP:**
*(noting the coat, the way he's standing)*
You're going to tell me.

**TYLER:**
Yes.

**CAMERA OP:**
*(she reaches for the camera on the table beside her — picks it up, raises it)*
Running.

This is the specific thing: she could have not done that. The choice to raise the camera is hers. She doesn't ask if he wants it recorded. She raises it because the record is hers, and he is hers to record, and this is the documentary.

Tyler sees her do it. He sees her choose to do it.

**TYLER:**
*(quietly)*
Good.

He stands in the back of the production office. The light from the monitor. Old footage on it — early in the season, himself, not looking at the camera.

**TYLER:**
I want to go to 1952.

The Camera Op holds the shot.

**TYLER:**
Detroit, 1952. Early summer. I've been there before — I know the geography, I know the roads that are different. I know who to avoid and where it's safe to be. I want to go back with — *(he pauses)* — without the chain. Without the obligation. Just to see what the city looks like when I'm in it for my own reasons. I've never done that. In any year. Gone somewhere for my own reasons.

**CAMERA OP:**
*(behind the camera)*
1952.

**TYLER:**
Early summer. Before the season turns.

**CAMERA OP:**
How long?

**TYLER:**
*(considering it)*
I don't know. That's part of it. I don't know how long. I don't have a return obligation. I'll come back when I'm ready. First time I've ever been able to say that.

**CAMERA OP:**
You'll come back.

It is not a question. It is a statement of what she knows about him.

**TYLER:**
*(simply)*
Yes. I'll come back. The architecture is here. The people I want to build with are here. This is the year I'm connected to now. I'll come back to this year.

A pause. She lowers the camera slightly — not off, still recording, but her arms drop to a natural angle.

**CAMERA OP:**
Detroit 1952.

**TYLER:**
Detroit 1952.

**CAMERA OP:**
*(a beat)*
Take photos if you can.

Tyler almost smiles. Actually smiles. The full one that has been approaching throughout the season and has never quite landed until now.

**TYLER:**
I'll take photos.

**[10:00–22:00] — THE STREETS**

```
EXT. DETROIT STREETS — DAY 61 — LATE MORNING
```

Tyler and the Camera Op walking. The city. The same streets — they have been on these streets many times in 63 days. The Camera Op films and walks.

**TYLER:**
I want to say something about the series before we get to the tree.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.):**
Go.

**TYLER:**
I've been documented for 63 days. Before that, the Jiangshi had files going back to — the first TYLER file is 1887. They've had eyes on me, in various forms, for most of the time I've been in recorded history. The Eastwind archive goes back further than that. The Subscriber built the reception mechanism 380 years ago.

**TYLER:**
For most of this documentary, I didn't know what the documentary was for. I knew I was being filmed. I knew the Jiangshi had interests. I didn't know the camera was part of the architecture. I didn't know the Camera Op was found by the architecture the same way I was. I didn't know there was a Subscriber. I didn't know there was a SPRING line. I didn't know Emily Springerton was going to come to Detroit with a 37-node dependency map that described everything I'd been carrying.

He stops. The Camera Op stops. The city around them.

**TYLER:**
I know now.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.):**
Does it change anything? Knowing it was designed?

**TYLER:**
*(thinking about this genuinely)*
It changes the framing. It doesn't change the facts. The sentence was real. The coin in the right pocket was real. The choice to stay was real. Knowing the architecture doesn't make those things less real — it makes them more legible. I can read them now. I know what they meant. *(pause)* It's like — the work you do when you don't know you're doing it is still work. It still counts. The architecture received it. The receipts are real.

He walks.

**TYLER:**
I spent four hundred years doing real work in a context I didn't fully understand. Now I understand the context. The work doesn't retroactively become less real. It becomes more — *(he searches for the word)* — correctly placed. The work is in the right frame now. That's all.

**CAMERA OP (O.S.):**
That's not a small thing.

**TYLER:**
No. It's not small. But it's also not new information about the work. It's just... the frame finally being the right size.

**TYLER:**
I'll come back. I don't know when relative to now — that's the nature of it. But I'll come back. I'll knock on your door. I'll sit at the table in the production office and say "I went to 1952" and you'll say "I know, I watched you leave" and that'll be the gap.

**TYLER:**

**[32:00–42:00] — THE AFTERNOON**

```
EXT. DETROIT — DAY 61 — AFTERNOON INTO EVENING
```

Three hours in the city. Tyler and the Camera Op. Not talking much. Just the city — the streets and the buildings and the light changing as the afternoon turns. The camera running.

This is the episode's longest continuous footage sequence. No dialogue, just the city and the two of them in it. The Camera Op films Tyler looking at the buildings, the streets, the water visible at the end of one block. Tyler watching the city the way someone watches a city they love.

The Production Coordinator, in a later interview:

**PRODUCTION COORDINATOR (V.O.):**
Three hours. We have three hours of footage from that afternoon. Not all of it is in the episode, obviously. But I want to say something about what it was like to cut it. You'd expect it to be quiet — the end of something, a little sad, the finality of it. It isn't sad. It's the opposite. It looks like someone walking around a city they're happy to be in. That's the specific quality of it. Tyler, for the first time in 63 days of footage, looks genuinely happy. Not relieved. Not resolved. Happy. Like he knows something good is coming and he can feel it already. Detroit 1952. He's going to a city he loves in a year he's chosen because he wants to go there. That's what happy looks like on Tyler. We didn't know until Day 61 what it looked like. Now we do.
