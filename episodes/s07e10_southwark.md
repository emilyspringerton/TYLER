**CAMERA OP** *(quietly, off-camera)*: "What happened in April 1592?"

**TYLER:** "I was in London for two weeks. A contact I needed to see. I came to this market every morning that week for food."

**CAMERA OP** *(off-camera)*: "Every morning."

**TYLER:** "For fish, specifically. There was a vendor at this stall, or where this stall is now, who arranged the stock in a particular way. I noticed it the first morning. I noticed it again the second morning. By the fourth morning I was paying attention in my specific way and I didn't know I was doing it."

**CAMERA OP** *(off-camera)*: "What was it about the arrangement?"

**TYLER:** "I couldn't tell you now. The vocabulary didn't exist then and whatever it was naming has been gone for four hundred and thirty years. I wrote *April 1592* in the journal because I knew something had happened, and that was all I had."

*[The Camera Op looks at him. Then she looks at the stall. Then she writes in her notebook.]*

**TYLER:** *(quietly)* "The ledger needs to be complete."

### ACT FOUR — LONDON BRIDGE APPROACH

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "One hundred and sixty feet."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "One hundred and eighty feet. The measurement flattens around here on the Southwark side — approximately one-eighty at the approach; less once you're on the bridge itself. The construction is centered on the approach, not the crossing."

Tyler is standing at the exact point where the river opens on both sides. He is not looking at the Exchange Student. He is looking at the City ahead, the way he has looked at it, from this specific angle, at this specific point of opening, hundreds of times.

He has the quality of someone who has come home from a particular direction.

---

**CAMERA OP** *(off-camera)*: "How many times?"

**TYLER:** "Crossings? I counted once. In the 1780s."

**CAMERA OP** *(off-camera)*: "What did you count to?"

**TYLER:** "Three hundred. Then I stopped counting because the number had become irrelevant."

**CAMERA OP** *(off-camera)*: "But you kept crossing."

**TYLER:** "Yes. Until the old bridge was taken down in 1831. Then the new one. Then this one."

*[A pause. The Thames is visible east and west.]*

**TYLER:** "I always looked back from the same point on the approach. Here. Where the river opens ahead on both sides and for a moment you can see the full width of the water. That moment I paid attention to every time."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Staying construction."

**TYLER:** "Yes. From the pattern of that specific moment. Three hundred times at least. Probably more. The old bridge was here from before my London records begin."

*[He turns and looks at the approach behind him, then turns back to face the City. He does this as someone noting two directions — behind and ahead — from a point he has stood at many times, going both ways.]*

**TYLER:** "The medieval bridge had houses on it. Shops. The view was different — you could see the City ahead but the bridge was also a street, a neighborhood. When they took it down and opened the view, the approach changed. The construction was already here. It continued."

*[He takes the journal out. He writes: A-301. London Bridge approach. 180 feet. STAYING. The old bridge, and before. Still holding.]*

*[The Camera Op holds the shot on Tyler standing at the approach point. The river is visible east and west. The City is ahead. He is standing at a moment he has stood at for five hundred years or more.]*

*[The hum is present. Running. It has been running here as long as he has been crossing.]*
