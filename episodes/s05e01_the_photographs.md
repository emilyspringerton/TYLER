## COLD OPEN

The Camera Op is reviewing footage at the table. She has the photograph Tyler gave her on Day 76 propped against her bag. She's been looking at it most mornings since then.

The photograph is of the tree. Detroit 1952. The same tree from the footage — the one near where Tyler said the sentence on Day 49. In 1952, the tree is younger. Smaller. But it is unmistakably the same tree: the shape of the branching, the particular lean of the trunk toward the east.

The Camera Op picks it up. She looks at it for a long time.

Title card: **DAY 90**

Tyler comes in from the back of the building. He doesn't go to his chair. He sits at the table across from the Camera Op.

**TYLER:** "You've been looking at that every morning."

**CAMERA OP:** "Yes."

**TYLER:** "What are you seeing?"

She sets the photograph down between them.

**CAMERA OP:** "That you knew it would be the same tree when you went back. You didn't go to 1952 to see what was there. You went to see if what was already there was still there."

He looks at the photograph.

**TYLER:** "Yes."

Title card.

**TYLER**
*"The Photographs"*

## EPISODE STRUCTURE

**Day 90, morning — the rest of the photographs**

Emily arrives at 9 AM. The Exchange Student is already at her corner table. Tyler puts the photograph back in its place — not in his pocket, on the table in front of him, face-up.

He reaches into his jacket. He sets ten more photographs on the table in a single careful motion — all face-down.

**EMILY:** "You've had them since Day 76."

**TYLER:** "I needed to know the first one landed correctly before I showed the rest."

He looks at the Camera Op.

**CAMERA OP:** "It landed."

He turns the photographs over.

---

**What's in the photographs:**

Ten photographs. Each one taken in 1952 Detroit. Tyler's handwriting on the back of each: a location and a date from 1952, and a second date written smaller underneath — the date of something that happened there.

- Photograph 2: A corner of a warehouse on Hastings Street. Back: *Hastings / June 1952 / June 1943 — something ended here and nobody recorded what.*
- Photograph 3: A hospital corridor, empty. Back: *Detroit Receiving / August 1952 / 1918 — the influenza ward. The building knows.*
- Photograph 4: A bridge over the river, looking south toward Windsor. Back: *Ambassador Bridge construction site / July 1952 / ongoing — the crossing has been happening for 300 years. The bridge is the current container.*
- Photograph 5: A kitchen in a house. Ordinary. Afternoon light. Back: *Conant Street / May 1952 / Day 12 of an uncompleted thing. Still uncompleted. Still in the air.*
- Photographs 6–11: Six more locations. Each one the same structure — a place that held something that never fully left.

Emily spreads them out on the table. She opens the notebook to a new page.

**EMILY:** "These are candidate sites."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EMILY:** "For more Witness Rooms."

**TYLER:** "Some of them. Not all of them — the ones that held the event well. Some of these places held it perfectly and then changed too much. Some of them are still holding it and the building is still standing." He taps photograph 4. "This one is the most stable. Three hundred years of continuous crossing — the container is very old and very well-made."

**EMILY:** "The Ambassador Bridge."

**TYLER:** "The crossing. The bridge is current infrastructure. What I'm interested in is the crossing itself — the fact that people have been getting from one side to the other at this point in the river since long before there was a bridge."

---

**Day 90, late morning — the Exchange Student maps**

The Exchange Student has been photographing the photographs with her phone. She opens her laptop.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "I can start preliminary dependency maps for each candidate. Same structure as the Bacon's building map — frequencies, witnesses, material requirements, supply chain."

**EMILY:** "How long for preliminary?"

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "One day per site if the location is accessible and documented. A week for the bridge — there's a lot of history there."

She looks at the photographs again. She picks up photograph 5 — the Conant Street kitchen.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "This one says 'Day 12 of an uncompleted thing.' What does that mean?"

**TYLER:** "Something started in that kitchen in 1952 and stopped before it was done. Not interrupted — stopped deliberately, by the person who was doing it. They put it down intending to come back. They didn't come back."

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "How do you know that?"

**TYLER:** "The kitchen held it. The way rooms hold things. You walk in and you know there's something unfinished in the air."

The Exchange Student looks at the photograph.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "A Witness Room for an unfinished thing."

**TYLER:** "A room that makes the unfinished thing accessible. So someone can finish it, or witness the decision not to, or just know it happened." He pauses. "Not every Witness Room is about completion. Some of them are about the space between starting and not finishing. That space is also real. It also should stay."

---

**Day 90, midday — Emily's new map**

Emily has a new page in the notebook. Not a continuation of the 48-node map — a new structure. At the top: *WITNESS ROOM NETWORK — candidate sites.*

She draws nodes for each photograph: Hastings, Detroit Receiving, the crossing, Conant Street, and the six others. She connects them with lines to a central node: *WITNESS ROOM #1 (Bacon's building) — construction complete.*

**EMILY:** "The first room is the anchor. Everything else relates to it."

**TYLER:** "Yes."

**EMILY:** "What determines order? Which one gets built next?"

He looks at the photographs.

**TYLER:** "Which one needs it most. Which one is at risk of the event dissipating — the building changing too much, the container degrading. Some of these places have been holding their events for decades. They won't hold them forever."

He picks up photograph 3. The hospital corridor.

**TYLER:** "This one is urgent. Detroit Receiving has been sold. The building is being converted. The conversion starts in three months."

Emily writes: *URGENT — Detroit Receiving / 1918 influenza ward / 3-month window before conversion.*

She adds a red annotation to the node.

**TYLER:** "That's the second construction."

---

**Day 90, afternoon — witness continuity, resumed**

The Exchange Student closes her laptop and looks at the main table.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "The open item from my Season 4 map. Long-term witness continuity. I've been working on it."

She comes to the table with a single page.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "I was thinking about it wrong. I was treating witnesses as a supply chain problem — find people who resonate with the frequency, keep them available. But that's not how it works." She puts the page down. It has a diagram: a circle with four nodes (Tyler, Camera Op, Emily, Exchange Student) and an arrow pointing outward to an unlabeled cluster. "The witnesses who tracked the first construction's supply chain became resonant through the tracking. I became resonant by mapping the dependencies. The function creates the witness."

**EMILY:** "You're saying the CEDAR function—"

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "Creates the people it needs. Anyone who tracks what a Witness Room construction requires, who spends sustained time understanding its material dependencies — they calibrate to the frequency. They become witnesses in the process." She looks at Tyler. "Is that right?"

**TYLER:** "That's what happened with your mother."

The table is very quiet.

**EXCHANGE STUDENT:** "She became resonant through the mapping."

**TYLER:** "The CEDAR-4 file lists her as a supply chain consultant. It doesn't explain why she was effective at the function. She was effective because by the time she finished the first dependency map, she was calibrated. The function built the witness."

The Exchange Student writes one line in her notebook: *witness continuity = CEDAR continuity. Solved.*
