## ACT ONE — [01:30–11:00]

---

**[01:30–04:00] — INTERIOR: STINKIES COMMISSAIRE — 9:22 AM**

```
INT. STINKIES COMMISSAIRE — STORE 0 — MORNING
```

The Camera Op comes in. She's been here every morning. The Counter Person recognizes her now — Powderhorn, ground. The routine has established.

This morning: she has her camera bag. She set it down on the floor beside the counter while she waits for the grind. She doesn't unzip it.

The Counter Person sets a punch card in front of her with the coffee. Standard — offered at first purchase, given again if lost.

**COUNTER PERSON**
Fourth visit. Might as well start tracking.

She picks up the punch card. Reads the back: *Buy 10, get 1 free. No expiration. No bullshit.*

She puts it in her jacket pocket — same pocket as the STINKIES soap bar she took on day one and has been carrying.

She takes her coffee. She looks toward the door.

**CAMERA OP**
The place down the block — CARTEL. Is it open today?

**COUNTER PERSON**
Not officially. The owner might be in there though. Holiday.

She nods. She takes her coffee outside.

---

**[04:00–11:00] — EXTERIOR / INTERIOR: CARTEL — 10:05 AM**

```
EXT. CARTEL — PIKE STREET — 10:05 AM
```

The door is still propped. She can hear something faint from inside — not the sound system, just the hum of a building with power running to it. Equipment standby. The low electrical presence of a space about to become something.

She goes in.

```
INT. CARTEL — 10:07 AM
```

The room. The floor is concrete — poured recently, the smell still faintly present under the cleaning product from this morning. The bar runs the full left wall: stools up, surface clean. Behind the bar: stock is in but not fully organized. Miller High Life — 6-packs in the cooler, visible through the glass. A ROCKET display has been started and abandoned at three cans. Liquor bottles in rough groupings on the shelf, not yet alphabetized.

The subwoofers. Two of them, mounted rear-elevated. She looks at them the way you look at something large that is about to produce sound.

The room is the right size. She doesn't know how she knows this — she's been in hundreds of venues — but she knows within ten seconds of standing in it. The room is right.

A man is behind the bar, writing something on a clipboard. He looks up.

**BAR MANAGER**
Not open yet.

**CAMERA OP**
I know. Sorry. The door was open.

He looks at the door. Then at her.

**BAR MANAGER**
What do you need?

She looks at the bar. The Miller High Life cooler.

**CAMERA OP**
Can I get a beer?

He pauses. Then sets down the clipboard.

**BAR MANAGER**
Five dollars.

He opens a can. She sits at the bar. She drinks. She looks at the room. The Bar Manager goes back to his clipboard.

Four minutes of silence. The room holds it.

Then: the door opens. She doesn't turn around. She feels the shift in the room — the hum changes character, the way acoustics change when a body enters a space.

She turns around.

Tyler.

He has been watching the building being built for a month. He has stopped outside it eleven times. Today, on the Fourth of July, with the door propped open, he walked in.

He sees her at the bar. He does not stop. He walks to the bar. He sits two stools away.

**TYLER**
*(to the Bar Manager)*
Beer.

The Bar Manager opens a can. Tyler sets down five dollars. He drinks.

The three of them in an empty nightclub on Independence Day. Cleaning product and Miller High Life and the subwoofers waiting.

The hum — 7.83 Hz, always — does something specific in this room. The concrete floor and the speaker-mount geometry create a resonance. It's not louder. It's cleaner. Like the room was built for it.

She notices. She doesn't say anything.

He notices that she notices.

He drinks his beer.

She spoke first.

She has the camera bag. She has carried it every morning and not opened it. This morning she put it on the floor under the barstool and left it there, which is different from carrying it.

She said something about the room. About it being the right size.

He agreed. He said: a room is the right size when the sound can fill it without having to try. He said it like it was a technical observation, not a feeling.

She asked how long he'd been in Pontiac.

He said: a while.

She asked where he was from.

He said: *(here the reconstruction fails — NOOR-1 notes that the subject's body language shifted slightly at this question, not evasion, something more specific: the posture of a person for whom the question "where are you from" is genuinely complicated)*

She said she knew he wasn't going to answer that.

He said he wasn't not answering it. He said the answer was long.

She said she had time.

*(A pause — NOOR-1 notes: 14 seconds. The longest documented silence in a direct exchange with the subject that did not precede departure.)*

He said: you left your camera downstairs.

She looked at the bag under the barstool.

He said: I'm not talking about that one.

She understood. She said: yes, I left it at the motel.

He said: why.

She said she didn't know yet.

He said: *(reconstruction fails — NOOR-1 notes: the subject said something brief; the Camera Op laughed once, the first documented laughter in a subject-adjacent scene; the laugh lasted approximately one second and she covered it with her hand)*

Then: approximately 18 more minutes of conversation that the reconstruction cannot recover with confidence. NOOR-1's exterior log records: continuous proximity, no departure attempt by either subject, two more beers (one each), the Bar Manager returning at 10:43 to find them still at the bar.

At 10:40, she said something. He looked at her for slightly longer than two seconds — the third time in recorded Series X observation that he has exceeded his standard camera-gaze limit; the first two were building-directed, not person-directed.

He looked at his beer.

He said: I know why you came.

She waited.

He said: I'm not going to tell you.

She said: because you don't want to.

He said: because you already know and hearing it from me would change it.

*(The reconstruction ends here. NOOR-1 notes: the exchange concluded at approximately 10:40 AM. The Camera Op left at 10:44 AM after the Bar Manager returned. Tyler remained at the bar. He ordered a third beer. He flipped the coin — NOOR-1's exterior angle caught the coin in the air for two frames; the date on the coin is not visible from this angle. He read it. He put it in his pocket. He finished his beer. He left at 11:03 AM.)*
